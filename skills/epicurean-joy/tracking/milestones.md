# Milestones

## Stage 1: Awareness
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: User can distinguish between the three categories of desire in their own life and has begun to notice the energy cost of empty desires.
**The user can**:
- Sort their current desires into natural-necessary, natural-unnecessary, and empty — with specificity, not in the abstract
- Name at least two empty desires that are consuming disproportionate energy
- Describe the difference between katastematic pleasure (the pleasure of a satisfied state) and kinetic pleasure (the pleasure of satisfying a need)
- Have experienced at least one moment of intentional, savored, simple pleasure (hedone) and can describe what it felt like
- Understand ataraxia as a goal and rate their current level of it honestly
- Begin keeping a desire diary, noticing and categorizing desires as they arise throughout the day

**What to celebrate**: The moment the user catches an empty desire in real time and says, unprompted, "That's an empty desire, isn't it?" That is the awareness milestone. Name it and acknowledge it.

## Stage 2: Practice
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User is actively using the Epicurean framework as a decision-making tool and has applied the tetrapharmakos to a specific personal anxiety.
**The user can**:
- Catch themselves in the act of pursuing an empty desire and choose differently — at least once during the week
- Apply their personal tetrapharmakos to a fear or anxiety and report a reduction in its grip
- Identify their primary fear among the four (cosmic hostility, death, inaccessibility of the good, unbearability of the bad) and articulate why the remedy applies
- Complete the Simple Pleasure Week experiment and report specific observations about the quality of their hedone
- Distinguish between ataraxia (genuine peace) and numbness (emotional shutdown)
- Begin to invest more deliberately in philia — at least one intentional, depth-focused conversation with a friend during the week

**What to celebrate**: The moment the user reports using their personal tetrapharmakos in the middle of an anxiety episode and noticing a genuine shift. That is the practice milestone — the philosophy is moving from concept to tool.

## Stage 3: Integration
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User has redesigned aspects of their daily life to reflect Epicurean priorities and is actively cultivating philia as a central practice.
**The user can**:
- Describe their personal "garden life" design with specificity — morning sanctuary, work of sufficiency, daily pleasure, philia practice, evening return
- Name at least one friendship that has meaningfully deepened during the skill through deliberate investment and vulnerability
- Report a measurable decrease in time or energy spent on empty desires — with specific examples of choices made differently
- Apply the desire classification framework automatically — not as a deliberate exercise but as a natural filter on new desires and decisions
- Define their personal line of sufficiency — the point of enough — in at least one life domain (income, achievement, possessions)
- Endure a period of discomfort or disappointment without catastrophizing, using the fourth remedy as support
- Practice the Evening Return regularly — a nightly noticing of the day's simple goods

**What to celebrate**: The moment the user describes a day that felt genuinely good — not because anything exceptional happened, but because ordinary things were enough. That is the integration milestone — the Garden Life is becoming real.

## Stage 4: Mastery / Graduation
**Marker**: User has internalized the Epicurean framework and is living a version of the Garden Life that is self-sustaining.
**The user can**:
- Classify desires, apply the tetrapharmakos, and prioritize philia without coaching prompts — these have become natural reflexes
- Design and execute their own experiments in simplicity and pleasure when life circumstances change
- Explain the difference between ataraxia and numbness, between simplicity and deprivation, and between enjoying natural-unnecessary pleasures and being enslaved by them — using their own examples, not borrowed language
- Maintain at least one philia-quality friendship through active, regular investment — and can describe how that friendship has changed during the skill
- Navigate a new life challenge (career change, loss, conflict) using the Epicurean framework independently
- Describe their relationship with desire as fundamentally different from when they started — less driven, more chosen, more at peace
- Articulate their line of sufficiency across major life domains and honor it in practice
- Teach the desire classification or tetrapharmakos to someone else, in their own words

**What to celebrate**: The moment the user says something like: "I realized I already have enough. I just wasn't paying attention." That is graduation. The Garden is planted. It will continue to grow without coaching.

**Next step**: Suggest `wabi-sabi` for deepening the acceptance of impermanence and imperfection, `seneca-letters` for building Stoic resilience alongside Epicurean tranquility, or `money-mind` if their relationship with money still reflects old desire patterns that need direct attention. If the user expresses interest in building daily routines to support their Garden Life, suggest `atomic-habits` for the structural scaffolding.
