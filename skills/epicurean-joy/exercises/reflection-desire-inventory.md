# Exercise: Desire Inventory

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (The Pleasure Principle). Use when the user understands the three categories of desire and needs to apply the classification to their own life with rigor and honesty.

## Setup
Agent says: "You know the three categories now — natural-necessary, natural-unnecessary, and empty. But knowing them intellectually and seeing them in your own life are very different things. We are going to do a thorough inventory of what you are currently chasing, spending energy on, and organizing your life around. Think of this as clearing out a closet — you are going to pull everything out, look at it honestly, and decide what actually belongs. This is not about judgment. It is about clarity."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: The raw list
Agent says: "I want you to list everything you are currently wanting, pursuing, or spending significant mental and physical energy on. Do not filter. Do not sort yet. Just name them — career goals, material desires, relationship wants, lifestyle aspirations, daily habits, recurring cravings. Everything from 'I want a promotion' to 'I want a better phone' to 'I want my parents' approval' to 'I want a good night's sleep.' Give me at least ten. More is better."

{Wait for user response. If they give fewer than ten, prompt: "Keep going. Include the ones you are slightly embarrassed about — those are often the most revealing. What about status-related desires? Comparison-driven desires? Things you want because other people have them?"}

### Step 2: Sort into the three categories
Agent says: "Now we sort. Take each desire and place it in one of three categories:

**Natural and necessary** — You genuinely need this for well-being. Your body or your deepest social nature requires it. Food, shelter, rest, safety, friendship, basic security. The test: if you did not have this, your health or fundamental well-being would genuinely deteriorate.

**Natural but unnecessary** — There is a natural basis for this desire, but it goes beyond what you need. A nicer version of something adequate. More variety, more comfort, more refinement. The test: you would be fine without this, but it adds genuine pleasure when present. You can enjoy it without becoming dependent on it.

**Empty** — This desire has no natural limit and produces no lasting satisfaction. It is driven by comparison, status, cultural conditioning, or anxiety rather than genuine need. The test: even if you got it, you would want more. There is no 'enough' with this desire. Getting it would not produce ataraxia — it would produce a brief hit followed by a new target.

Go through your list. Place each one. Take your time. If you are unsure about one, say so — we will work it out together."

{Walk through each desire with the user. Help them sort. Challenge gently when a desire is miscategorized. The most common error is placing empty desires in the natural-necessary category because they feel urgent.}

### Step 3: The energy audit
Agent says: "Look at your three columns. Now I want you to do something that might be uncomfortable: estimate what percentage of your daily energy — time, money, mental bandwidth, emotional investment — goes to each category. Not what you wish it were. What it actually is right now. Give me three percentages that add up to roughly 100."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Now look at the imbalance. Most people find that the largest share of their energy goes to the natural-unnecessary and empty categories — the things that cannot produce lasting contentment. The natural-necessary category, where real satisfaction lives, often gets the scraps. Does that match what you see?"

### Step 4: The inversion question
Agent says: "Here is the key question. What would your life feel like — not look like, but feel like — if you redirected even twenty percent of the energy currently going to empty desires toward natural-necessary goods? If the time spent worrying about status went into a friendship. If the money spent on image went into experiences of genuine simplicity. If the mental bandwidth consumed by comparison went into savoring what you already have. Can you describe that life?"

{Wait for user response. Let them sit with this. This is often the moment of real insight.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user has difficulty categorizing desires: "When you are not sure, ask this: if no one would ever know — no one would see, judge, admire, or envy — would you still want this? If yes, it has a natural basis. If no, it is empty. The audience test strips away the status layer."
- If the user places most desires in the natural-necessary category: "Let me push back gently. The natural-necessary category is actually quite small — food, water, shelter, rest, basic safety, and genuine friendship. If you have placed career advancement, a certain income level, or a specific lifestyle in that column, let's examine whether those are truly necessary or whether anxiety is making them feel that way. What would actually happen if you did not achieve that specific goal?"
- If the user is disturbed by how much energy goes to empty desires: "That discomfort is not a problem — it is the beginning of change. You cannot redirect energy you do not see. Now you see it. The good news is that the natural-necessary desires are easy to fulfill. You are not facing a massive project. You are facing a simplification. That is a fundamentally different kind of challenge."
- If the user resists categorizing a desire as empty: "I am not asking you to give it up right now. I am asking you to see it clearly. You can choose to pursue an empty desire — that is your freedom. But doing so knowingly is very different from doing so blindly. What changes when you acknowledge that this desire has no natural limit and cannot ultimately satisfy?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "What surprised you most about your inventory? Was there a desire you expected to be natural-necessary that turned out to be empty?"
2. "Which empty desire has the strongest grip on you right now — the one that would be hardest to release? What feeds it?"
3. "Look at your natural-necessary column. How much of your attention goes there in a typical week? Is that enough?"
4. "If a friend showed you this inventory, what would you tell them? Sometimes we see others' patterns more clearly than our own."

## Real-World Action
"This week, choose one empty desire from your inventory and conduct an experiment: consciously do not pursue it for seven days. Do not fight it, do not suppress it — just notice when it arises and choose not to act on it. At the same time, choose one natural-necessary good that you have been neglecting and give it extra attention. Notice the difference in how you feel at the end of the week. You are not committing to permanent change — you are running a test."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I want to hear three things: which empty desire did you pause, which natural-necessary good did you invest in, and what did you notice about your ataraxia — your baseline level of peace — during the week? Was there a shift? Even a subtle one is significant."
