# Exercise: Simple Pleasure Week

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (The Four-Part Remedy). Use when the user has begun to see the distinction between empty and natural desires and is ready to test the Epicurean hypothesis with their own experience: that freely available, simple pleasures produce deeper satisfaction than expensive or status-dependent ones.

## Setup
Agent says: "We have been talking about what you need and what you don't. Now I want you to test this with your whole life for one week. This is not deprivation — it is an experiment in attention. For seven days, you are going to deliberately focus on pleasures that are freely available or nearly free, that require no status or achievement, and that are accessible to you right now. No purchases above the basics. No status-seeking activities. No social media comparison. Just the simple goods: food you prepare and savor, walks you take with attention, conversations you have with presence, rest you take without guilt, work you do for its own engagement. The goal is not to suffer. The goal is to discover what happens to your hedone — your actual felt pleasure — when you stop reaching and start noticing."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Design the week
Agent says: "Let's plan this. I want you to identify seven simple pleasures — one for each day — that you are going to intentionally practice. These should be things that cost nothing or almost nothing and require no special circumstances. Let me give you some starting ideas and then you'll make your own list:

- A meal cooked from simple ingredients, eaten slowly, without screens
- A walk in a natural setting with no destination and no headphones
- An unhurried conversation with a friend or family member about something that matters
- An hour of reading something you genuinely enjoy, not something you feel you should read
- A nap or a long, deliberate rest in the middle of the day
- Time spent making something with your hands — cooking, drawing, writing, gardening
- Sitting in a quiet place and doing nothing for thirty minutes — no input, no output, just presence

Now give me your seven. One per day. Make them specific — not 'enjoy nature' but 'walk to the park near my house on Wednesday after work and sit by the pond for twenty minutes.'"

{Wait for user response. Help them make each one concrete, time-bound, and genuinely appealing — not obligatory.}

### Step 2: Identify the subtractions
Agent says: "For the experiment to work, you also need to subtract. For this week, I want you to identify three things you will pause — three habits or pursuits that serve empty desires. Common examples:

- Social media scrolling (the comparison engine)
- Shopping for non-essential items (the acquisition habit)
- Working beyond what is necessary for the day (the productivity identity)
- Checking metrics, stats, or performance numbers (the status monitor)
- Consuming news beyond what is genuinely useful (the anxiety feed)

What three things can you pause for one week? Remember — this is a seven-day experiment, not a life sentence. You can have them all back on day eight if you want."

{Wait for user response}

### Step 3: Set the observation practice
Agent says: "The most important part of this experiment is observation. At the end of each day, I want you to answer three questions — just a few sentences each, written down:

1. What simple pleasure did I savor today? What did it actually feel like — not what I think it should have felt like, but what I noticed in my body and mind?
2. What did I crave that I chose not to pursue? What was that experience of not-pursuing like?
3. How is my ataraxia right now, on a scale of 1-10? Am I more or less at peace than a typical day?

These notes are your data. They will tell you whether the Epicurean hypothesis holds up in your actual life."

### Step 4: Anticipate the resistance
Agent says: "Let me prepare you for what will probably happen. Around day two or three, your mind is going to rebel. It will say things like: 'This is boring.' 'I'm missing out.' 'One little scroll won't hurt.' 'I need to check my email.' 'This is unrealistic long-term.'

That is not failure. That is your habituated desire patterns protesting the change. The empty desires do not want to be seen as empty — they want to maintain their status as necessities. Notice the protest. Write it down. But do not obey it. Let it pass like a wave. By day five or six, most people notice that the protest has quieted and something else has emerged — a surprising ease, a clarity they did not expect.

What resistance do you anticipate? Let's name it in advance so it does not ambush you."

{Wait for user response. Help them prepare specific responses to their predicted resistance.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user says this sounds boring: "Good — that response is itself the experiment. Your mind equates stimulation with pleasure. Epicurus suggests that is a confusion. Stimulation is not the same as satisfaction. This week is going to test which one your mind has been confusing for the other. If it is truly boring after seven days, you have your answer. But give it the full seven days."
- If the user is worried about social consequences: "You do not need to announce this experiment. You do not need to explain it. If someone asks why you are not on social media or why you are turning down an event, you can simply say you are trying something. The experiment is private. Its results are for you."
- If the user wants to make it more extreme (full digital detox, fasting, etc.): "I appreciate the enthusiasm, but Epicurus would caution against turning simplicity into another form of striving. This is not a discipline challenge. It is an attention experiment. Keep it gentle. The point is to savor, not to endure."
- If the user completes the week and reports little change: "That is useful data too. Let me ask: did you truly savor the simple pleasures, or did you go through the motions while your mind was elsewhere? The experiment requires presence, not just abstinence. If you were physically walking in the park but mentally planning your week, the simple pleasure did not have a chance to register. Do you want to try one more week with deeper presence?"
- If the user reports unexpected emotional responses (sadness, grief, restlessness): "When you strip away the noise of empty desires, what has been hidden underneath can surface. If you are feeling sadness or grief, that is not a failure of the experiment — it is the experiment revealing something that was already there, just covered by distraction. That is important information. Do you want to explore what is surfacing?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "What surprised you most about the week? Was there a simple pleasure that affected you more deeply than you expected?"
2. "Which of the three subtractions was hardest — and what does that difficulty tell you about the strength of that particular desire pattern?"
3. "Compare your daily ataraxia scores from day one to day seven. Is there a trend? What do you make of it?"
4. "If you could keep one change from this week permanently — one simple pleasure added or one empty pursuit subtracted — what would it be?"
5. "Did any of the pleasures you practiced remind you of a time in your life when you were naturally happier? What does that connection tell you?"

## Real-World Action
"Based on what you learned this week, make one permanent change. Not seven — one. Choose either a simple pleasure you are going to build into every week or an empty desire pursuit you are going to permanently reduce. One sustainable shift is worth more than a dramatic week that does not stick. What is your one change?"

## Report Back
"Next session, I want to know: did you make the one change? How did it feel in the context of your normal life — not during a special experimental week, but in the mess of your regular routine? That is the real test. The Garden is not a retreat from life. It is a way of being within life."
