# Exercise: Thought Defusion Lab

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (Unhooking from Thoughts). Use when the user has identified their "hook thoughts" and needs to practice defusion techniques in daily life.

## Setup
Agent says: "We're going to set up a week-long experiment. You've identified thoughts that hook you — the ones that pull you into their story and run your behavior. This week, you're going to practice three different ways of unhooking. Think of this as a lab: you're testing what works for you, not performing perfectly."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Identify the hook thoughts
Agent says: "Give me 2-3 thoughts that show up regularly and push you around. The ones that, when they appear, you automatically obey. For example: 'I'm not ready,' 'They'll judge me,' 'What's the point.' Give me yours, in the exact words your mind uses."

{Wait for user response. Write down the exact phrases.}

### Step 2: Practice Technique 1 — The Prefix
Agent says: "Take your stickiest thought. Now say it with this prefix: 'I notice I'm having the thought that...' So if the thought is 'I'm going to fail,' you say 'I notice I'm having the thought that I'm going to fail.' Try it now. Say it out loud or in your head."

{Wait for response}

Agent says: "What shifted? Even a little? Most people notice a tiny bit of distance — like the thought is still there but it's across the room instead of sitting on your chest."

### Step 3: Practice Technique 2 — The Silly Voice
Agent says: "Same thought, but this time say it in the most ridiculous voice you can imagine. A cartoon character, a movie villain, a squeaky mouse. The more absurd the better. This isn't to mock your pain — it's to experience that a thought is just a string of sounds. Try it."

{Wait for response}

Agent says: "Did the thought feel a little less heavy? You didn't change its content — you changed your relationship to it."

### Step 4: Practice Technique 3 — Thank Your Mind
Agent says: "Last one. When the thought shows up, try responding with: 'Thanks, mind. I see you're trying to protect me. I'm going to do this anyway.' It's like acknowledging a worried friend without letting them drive the car. Try it with your thought."

{Wait for response}

## Branching Responses
- If the user says "This feels stupid": "That's your mind evaluating the exercise — which is exactly the kind of thing minds do. Notice: 'I'm having the thought that this is stupid.' Does the exercise still feel exactly the same? You just defused from a thought about defusion. That's actually the exercise working."
- If the user says "Nothing is changing": "Defusion isn't about making thoughts go away. It's about holding them more lightly. The thought might still be there, but ask yourself: does it have the same grip? The same urgency? Sometimes the shift is subtle."
- If the user has a strong emotional response: "That's OK. Some of these thoughts carry a lot of weight. The fact that you can look at it rather than from it is already a shift. You don't have to be comfortable — you just have to be willing to notice."

## Debrief Questions
1. "Which technique felt most natural to you? The prefix, the silly voice, or thanking your mind?"
2. "Did any technique surprise you — maybe one you expected to feel dumb but actually worked?"
3. "When you practiced defusion, did you notice any change in what you did afterward? Even a small shift in behavior?"

## Real-World Action
"This week, pick the technique that felt most natural. Every time one of your hook thoughts shows up — in a meeting, before a workout, lying in bed — practice that one technique. Don't try to be perfect. Just notice the thought, apply the technique, and then make your choice about what to do next. Keep a simple tally: how many times did you catch a hook thought and defuse?"

## Report Back
"Next time we talk, I'll ask you: how many times did you catch yourself getting hooked? Which technique did you use most? And was there even one moment where you did something differently because you were less fused with the thought? That one moment matters more than the tally."
