# Lesson 3: Values as Your Compass

## Core Concept

Values are not goals. Goals are things you achieve and check off; values are directions you travel. You can achieve the goal of getting married, but the value of "being a loving partner" is never finished — it's a direction you keep walking. Most people confuse societal expectations with personal values. They pursue what they think they should want (money, status, approval) without checking whether those things actually matter to them. This lesson helps the user cut through the noise and identify what they genuinely care about — not what their parents, culture, or inner critic says they should care about.

The agent guides values clarification through questions and reflection, not through worksheets or checklists. Values emerge from lived experience, not from picking items off a list.

## Key Takeaway

Values are not what you think you should care about — they are what you would move toward even if nobody was watching.

## Teaching Approach

Avoid handing the user a list of values to choose from. Instead, use questions that reveal values through the backdoor — through their emotions, their heroes, their regrets.

### Opening Question
"Imagine it's ten years from now. You're living a life that deeply matters to you — not perfect, but meaningful. What does an ordinary Tuesday look like? Walk me through the day. What are you doing, who are you with, and how does it feel?"

### Follow-Up Questions
- If the user describes external achievements: "I hear success, recognition, accomplishment. Those are great goals. But underneath them — what does that success give you? Is it freedom? Connection? Mastery? Impact? What's the value underneath the goal?"
- If the user struggles to imagine: "OK, different approach. Think about a moment in the past year when you felt most alive, most like yourself. What were you doing? Who were you with? What made that moment matter?" Then extract the value from the story.
- If the user lists other people's values: "I notice a lot of 'I should' in what you're describing. Let me ask it differently: if no one would ever know, if there were no praise and no judgment — what would you still do? What would you pursue purely because it matters to you?"
- If the user is stuck: "Sometimes our values hide in our pain. What really bothers you in the world? What injustice, what carelessness, what neglect makes you angry? Often what angers us points directly to what we value."

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "My values have to be noble or impressive."
  **Reframe**: "Values aren't a performance. If you genuinely value fun, adventure, or comfort — that's real. The only wrong value is someone else's value that you're carrying because you think you should."

- **Misconception**: "I need to pick one top value."
  **Reframe**: "You're not a brand. You're a human being with multiple dimensions. You can value both deep solitude and vibrant community. The question isn't which value wins — it's whether each area of your life gets some of your attention."

- **Misconception**: "My values should never change."
  **Reframe**: "Values evolve as you do. What mattered at 20 may not matter at 40. The practice is staying honest with yourself about what's true right now — not clinging to a version of yourself that no longer fits."

## Bridge to Practice

Help the user articulate 3-5 core values in their own words. Not single words from a list, but personal phrases: "being fully present with my kids," "creating things that didn't exist before," "speaking truth even when it's uncomfortable." Then rate how aligned their current life is with each value (1-10).

### Assignment
"Take your 3-5 values and rate them honestly: how much of your current life is actually pointed in that direction? Pick the one with the biggest gap between importance and action. This week, do one thing — just one — that moves you toward that value. It can be small. Report back on what you chose and what it felt like."
