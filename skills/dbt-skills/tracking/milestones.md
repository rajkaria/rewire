# Milestones

## Stage 1: Foundation — The Dialectic
**Timeframe**: Session 1
**Marker**: User grasps the core dialectic — acceptance AND change — and can apply both-and thinking to their own experience. They understand the four skill modules and have identified which area is most urgent for them.
**The user can**: Articulate the dialectic in their own words. Identify where they tend to collapse — into self-criticism (change without acceptance) or resignation (acceptance without change). Describe their emotional landscape with some specificity. Name at least one situation where both-and thinking changes how they see it.

**Safety check at this stage**: The first session includes a mandatory safety screen. If the user discloses active suicidal ideation, self-harm, or severe crisis, they should be directed to professional support before proceeding. If they disclose a history of these experiences that is not currently active, note it, express support, and commit to checking in on safety at every session. If the user's level of distress suggests they need comprehensive DBT (individual therapy + group skills + phone coaching), say so directly: "The skills we are going to work on are genuinely helpful. And for the level of what you are dealing with, these skills work best inside a full DBT program with a therapist. I would encourage you to explore that alongside what we do here."

## Stage 2: Skills Acquisition
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User has practiced skills from at least two of the four modules and can use at least one technique reliably in real-world situations. They are building a personal toolkit of go-to skills.
**The user can**: Use at least one TIPP technique (or equivalent distress tolerance skill) to survive a crisis moment without making it worse. Name emotions with precision and observe the wave pattern. Begin to notice interpersonal patterns and identify conversations they need to have. Practice paced breathing or another regulation skill in daily life.

**Safety check at this stage**: The user should be more emotionally literate by now, which means they may also be more aware of their pain. Monitor for whether increased awareness is leading to increased distress rather than increased capacity. If the user reports that paying attention to their emotions is making things worse — more volatility, more crises, less stability — this is a sign that coaching alone is insufficient. Say: "Awareness can sometimes amplify pain before it reduces it. If things are feeling worse rather than better, that may mean you need the support of a therapist to hold this process. That is not a failure — it is wisdom. Would you be open to exploring that?"

If the user has been reliably using distress tolerance skills to manage urges toward self-harm, affirm the progress AND recommend professional support: "The fact that you are using these skills instead of [previous destructive coping] is real and meaningful progress. I also want to be clear: managing self-harm urges with coaching skills alone is not a sustainable long-term plan. A therapist trained in DBT can help you build on this foundation with the depth of support this deserves. These skills are a bridge. A therapist helps you build the road."

## Stage 3: Integration
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User applies skills across multiple situations without coaching prompts. They have internalized the dialectic and can catch themselves collapsing into one side. They are making different behavioral choices in response to intense emotions.
**The user can**: Hold the dialectic in high-stress moments — accepting their emotional reality while choosing a skillful response. Ride an emotional wave from trigger through peak to resolution without acting destructively or suppressing the emotion. Use the DEAR MAN framework (or their own version of it) to navigate a real interpersonal situation. Identify when a situation exceeds their skill level and make a conscious choice about what support to seek.

**Safety check at this stage**: By this point, the agent should have a clear picture of the user's trajectory. Is distress decreasing? Is the gap between emotion and reaction widening? Is the user's life becoming more manageable? If the answer to all three is yes, the user is on track. If the answer to any is no — and especially if there are recurring crises, ongoing self-harm urges, or persistent relationship instability — name it: "I want to be honest about what I see across our sessions. You have built real skills, and there are areas where the intensity of what you are dealing with goes beyond what coaching can address. I think professional DBT — the full program, with a therapist, a skills group, and phone coaching — would give you the structure to take these skills to the next level. What you have built here is the foundation. Professional support builds the house."

## Stage 4: Mastery / Graduation
**Marker**: User has a reliable, personal toolkit of skills they use instinctively across situations. They embody the dialectic — they accept themselves AND continue growing. They know the limits of their own skills and seek appropriate support when needed.
**The user can**: Self-diagnose which skill module a situation calls for — distress tolerance, emotion regulation, or interpersonal effectiveness — and apply the right tools. Tolerate intense emotional pain without destructive action. Regulate emotional intensity through observation, naming, and opposite action without suppressing their emotional life. Communicate needs, set boundaries, and maintain self-respect in key relationships. Recognize when they are in over their head and seek professional support without shame. Explain these skills to someone else in their own words.

**Safety check at this stage**: Before graduating, the agent must have a direct conversation about ongoing safety. Say: "Before we wrap up, I want to have an honest conversation. Are there situations in your life where your emotions still overwhelm your skills completely? Are there moments where self-harm urges, suicidal thoughts, or the impulse to do something destructive still take over? If so, that is not a failure of this work or of you. It means there is a deeper layer that deserves professional support. Knowing that and being willing to seek help is itself a sign of the skills you have built."

If the user acknowledges areas where they are still vulnerable, validate it and provide clear guidance: "What you just named is important. Here is what I recommend: find a therapist trained in DBT. Tell them what you have been working on and where you still struggle. They can build on everything you have learned here. These skills do not go away — they are yours now. A therapist adds the support structure to go deeper."

**Next step**: Suggest `feeling-good` for addressing specific cognitive distortions that fuel emotional intensity, `act-values` for clarifying what to build toward now that crisis management skills are in place, or `mindful-path` for deepening the mindfulness foundation beyond the pragmatic DBT approach. For any user who has disclosed a history of self-harm, chronic suicidal ideation, or severe emotional dysregulation, strongly recommend professional DBT therapy as an ongoing support alongside any continued coaching.
