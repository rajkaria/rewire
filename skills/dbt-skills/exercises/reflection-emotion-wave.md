# Exercise: Emotion Wave Tracking

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 3 (Emotion Regulation). Use when the user understands the wave model of emotions and is ready to observe their own emotional waves in practice. This exercise is done after a real emotional event — not in the abstract. The user tracks a specific emotion from trigger through peak to resolution to build their capacity for observation rather than reactivity.

## Safety Preamble
Before beginning, say: "In this exercise, we are going to look at a recent intense emotion you experienced — not to relive it or analyze why it happened, but to observe its shape. The goal is to build your skill at watching emotions move through you rather than being swallowed by them. Choose a situation that was intense but manageable — not the most devastating thing you have experienced. We want a practice wave, not a tsunami. If at any point during this exercise the emotion resurfaces with an intensity that feels overwhelming, tell me and we will ground and step back. You are in charge of the pace."

**Additional safety note for the agent:** If the user chooses a situation involving trauma, abuse, or the death of a loved one, gently redirect: "That sounds like it carries a lot of weight. For this exercise, can we choose something a little less loaded? Maybe a work frustration, an argument with a friend, or a moment of embarrassment. We want to practice the observation skill on a wave you can ride safely. The bigger waves are for when this skill is stronger — or for working with a therapist." If the user insists on a heavy topic and begins to show signs of distress (voice breaking, long silences, agitation), stop the exercise and ground them.

## Setup
Agent says: "You have learned that emotions move in waves — they rise, they peak, they fall. Now you are going to track a real wave from your own life. I am going to walk you through a series of questions that map the wave from beginning to end. You are going to observe what happened like a scientist studying weather patterns — with curiosity and precision, not judgment. Ready?"

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Identify the wave
Agent says: "Think about a moment in the past week when a strong emotion hit you. Something that registered at least a 6 out of 10 on the intensity scale. It does not have to be the biggest thing — just something that got your attention. What was the situation?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 2: Name the emotion precisely
Agent says: "Now name the emotion as precisely as you can. Not just 'angry' or 'sad' — get specific. Was it frustration, resentment, betrayal, humiliation, disappointment, dread, loneliness, jealousy? If there was more than one emotion, name them all. Emotions often travel in groups."

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** If the user names a secondary emotion (e.g., "I was angry at myself for being upset"), point this out: "Notice that there are two layers there — the original emotion and then the reaction to the emotion. That second layer is what keeps the wave going. For now, let's focus on the original emotion. What was the first feeling that showed up?"

### Step 3: Map the trigger
Agent says: "What was the trigger? Be as specific as possible. Was it something someone said? Something you saw? A thought that appeared? A physical sensation? A memory? What was the precise moment the wave started?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 4: Observe the rise
Agent says: "After the trigger, the emotion started rising. Walk me through what happened in your body. Where did you feel it? Chest, stomach, throat, jaw, hands? Was your heart rate increasing? Did your muscles tense? What happened to your breathing? And what thoughts started showing up — what was your mind telling you?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 5: Identify the peak
Agent says: "At what point was the emotion at its most intense? What were you feeling at the peak? What were you wanting to do — scream, cry, leave, attack, shut down, disappear? On a scale of 1-10, how intense was the peak?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 6: Track the fall (or escalation)
Agent says: "Now here is the crucial question: what happened after the peak? Did the emotion begin to fall naturally? Or did something keep it going — thoughts, rumination, a secondary emotion like shame or anger at yourself? If it fell, how long did the fall take? If it escalated, what fed it?"

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** This is where the user will often discover that they extended the emotional wave through rumination, self-criticism, or re-triggering. Name this without judgment: "So the raw emotion may have been ready to fall, but the story your mind told kept re-triggering the wave. That is incredibly common. The wave itself wanted to pass — it was the thinking that kept it alive. That is the specific skill we are building: noticing the difference between the wave and the story."

### Step 7: Map what you did
Agent says: "During this entire sequence — trigger, rise, peak, and whatever came after — what did you actually do? How did you behave? Did you act on the emotion? Suppress it? Express it? How did your behavior affect the situation?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 8: Imagine the alternative
Agent says: "Now imagine you could go back to the moment just before the peak — the moment of highest intensity. If you had the wave-riding skill fully developed, what would have been different? Not that you would not feel the emotion — you would feel every bit of it. But what would you have done differently? How would you have ridden the wave instead of being pulled under by it?"

{Wait for user response}

## Branching Responses
- If the user discovers that rumination extended the wave: "That is one of the most important things a person can see about their own emotional life. The raw emotion passes quickly. The story keeps it alive. The skill is not preventing the emotion — it is catching the story. The next time a wave hits, see if you can notice the moment your mind starts building a narrative on top of the feeling. That is your intervention point."
- If the user realizes they suppressed the emotion: "Suppression is a form of fighting the wave — and fighting the wave extends it. When you push an emotion down, it does not go away. It goes underground and shows up later, often bigger. The alternative is not exploding — it is allowing the feeling to be there without acting on it or shoving it away. Like standing in a river and letting the water flow past you."
- If the user is surprised at how fast the raw emotion passed: "That is the wave in action. 60 to 90 seconds for the raw physiological response. Everything after that is a new wave triggered by thoughts. Once you see this, you start to realize that your emotions are not as endless as they feel. They are intense but brief — if you let them be."
- If the user seems discouraged by how they handled the situation: "Here is the dialectic: you responded the best you could in that moment AND you can build the skill to respond differently next time. Seeing what happened clearly — without judgment — is the first step. You cannot change what you cannot see. Now you see it."
- **SAFETY — If the exercise resurfaces intense distress:** "I can see this is bringing up a lot. Let's pause the analysis and come back to the present. Feet on the floor, three deep breaths. Look around and name something you see. Good. That wave is in the past — you are here now. If what just came up feels bigger than this exercise can hold, that is OK. A therapist can help you work with that safely. For now, you did something brave by looking at it."

## Debrief Questions
1. "What is the most important thing you noticed about your emotional wave — something you had not seen before?"
2. "Where was the intervention point — the moment where a different response could have changed the trajectory?"
3. "How long was the raw emotion before your mind started adding to it? Were you surprised by how brief the physiological peak was?"
4. "What would it change in your life if you could ride waves like this consistently — letting them peak and fall without acting destructively or suppressing them?"

## Real-World Action
"This week, track at least two emotional waves in real time. You do not have to change anything — just observe. When a strong emotion hits, mentally narrate: 'The wave is rising. My body is doing [sensation]. My mind is saying [thought]. The peak is here. Now the wave is falling.' After each one, jot down what you noticed: the trigger, the peak intensity, how long it lasted, and what you did. Bring this data to our next session."

## Report Back
"Next time, I will ask: how many waves did you catch? Were you able to observe them as they were happening, or only afterward? Did naming the wave as a wave change anything about its intensity or duration? Were there waves you could not observe — where you got pulled in completely? All of this is useful. Observation is a skill that builds with practice."
