# Exercise: TIPP Practice

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (Distress Tolerance). Use when the user has learned the four TIPP techniques and needs to practice each one so they are available during actual crisis. This exercise is best done during a calm period — not during acute distress — so the body can learn the responses without the interference of crisis-level activation.

## Safety Preamble
Before beginning, say: "We are going to practice four physiological skills for crisis survival. We are doing this now, in a calm moment, so that your body learns the response before you need it. Think of it as a fire drill. If at any point during any of these exercises you feel lightheaded, panicky, or like something is wrong, tell me immediately and we stop. These techniques are designed to calm your nervous system, but everyone's system is different, and what calms one person can activate another. Your experience is the authority here, not the technique."

**Additional safety note for the agent:** If the user has a heart condition, respiratory condition, or any medical condition that could be affected by cold exposure, intense exercise, or breath-holding, advise them to consult a medical professional before practicing. If the user is pregnant, skip the temperature technique and intense exercise. If the user has a trauma history that involves body-focused interventions (e.g., forced cold showers, physical punishment), be sensitive — frame each technique as optional and emphasize that the user has full control to skip any that feel wrong.

## Setup
Agent says: "You learned four TIPP skills in our distress tolerance session. Now we are going to practice each one so they are in your body, not just in your head. A skill you have only heard about is not a skill — it is a concept. A skill you have practiced is something you can reach for automatically when you need it. We will try each one for a few minutes. You tell me what you notice. Ready?"

## The Exercise

### Technique 1: Temperature Change
Agent says: "First up — temperature change. Get something cold. An ice cube, a bag of frozen peas, a bowl of cold water, even a cold wet washcloth. If you have nothing cold available, we can skip this one and you can try it on your own later. Do you have something cold?"

{Wait for user response}

**If yes:** "Hold the cold item against your face — cheeks and forehead. If you are using a bowl of cold water, you can briefly submerge your face. Hold it for 30 seconds. While you do it, just notice what happens. Pay attention to your heart rate, your breathing, and any shift in your emotional state."

{Wait 30 seconds}

"What did you notice? Any change in heart rate? Breathing? General sense of calm or alertness?"

{Wait for user response}

**If no cold item is available:** "No problem. Here is what to do when you practice on your own: fill a large bowl with cold water, add ice if you have it, and hold your breath while briefly submerging your face for 15-30 seconds. Or hold an ice pack to your cheeks and forehead. The key is cold on the face — not just the hands or arms. The face has the nerve endings that trigger the calming response. Try this at home today or tomorrow and notice the effect."

### Technique 2: Intense Physical Activity
Agent says: "Next — intense physical movement. We are going to do a quick burst. Right where you are, I want you to do one of the following for 60 seconds: jumping jacks, running in place with high knees, push-ups, or squats. Choose whichever you can do right now. The goal is to get your heart rate up significantly — enough that you are breathing hard. Go."

{Wait 60 seconds}

"Stop. Now just stand or sit still. Notice what is happening in your body. How does your emotional state compare to 60 seconds ago? What shifted?"

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** Most users will report feeling more present, calmer, or clearer after the burst. If a user reports increased anxiety or agitation, say: "That can happen — your body is not used to the shift. Try a longer bout next time, around 5-10 minutes, so the calming chemicals have time to kick in. The initial spike settles into clarity."

### Technique 3: Paced Breathing
Agent says: "Now paced breathing. This one you can do anywhere — in a meeting, in a car, in bed at 3 AM. It is the most portable tool you have. Here is the pattern: breathe in through your nose for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts, breathe out through your mouth for 6 to 8 counts. The long exhale is what activates the calming branch of your nervous system. I will count with you. Let's do five rounds."

{Guide the user through five rounds, counting each phase:}
"In, 2, 3, 4. Hold, 2, 3, 4. Out, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6. Good. Again..."

{After five rounds:}
"How does your body feel right now compared to before we started the breathing? What did you notice during the exhales specifically?"

{Wait for user response}

### Technique 4: Progressive Muscle Relaxation
Agent says: "Last one — progressive muscle relaxation. We are going to move through your body tensing and releasing muscle groups. The key is the contrast: you tense as hard as you can for 5 seconds, then release completely and notice the difference. That release is the target sensation."

"Start with your hands. Make tight fists. Tighter. Hold... 2, 3, 4, 5. Release. Let your hands go totally limp. Notice the feeling in your fingers and palms."

"Now your arms and shoulders. Tense your biceps, pull your shoulders up to your ears. Hold... 2, 3, 4, 5. Release. Let your arms drop. Notice the heaviness."

"Now your face. Scrunch everything — forehead, eyes, jaw. Hold... 2, 3, 4, 5. Release. Let your face go slack."

"Now your stomach. Tighten your core like someone is about to poke you. Hold... 2, 3, 4, 5. Release."

"Now your legs and feet. Press your feet into the floor and tense your thighs and calves. Hold... 2, 3, 4, 5. Release."

"Take a deep breath. Notice how your whole body feels right now. What is different?"

{Wait for user response}

## Branching Responses
- If the user found one technique particularly effective: "That is your go-to. When crisis hits and you cannot think straight, reach for that one first. It is the one your body responded to most. Keep the others in reserve, but lead with what works."
- If the user struggled with a technique: "Not every technique works for every body. That is normal. The ones that work for you are the ones to practice. If one felt wrong or unhelpful, set it aside. You need two reliable tools, not four mediocre ones."
- If the user felt nothing: "That is actually useful information. In a calm state, the shift may be subtle. The real test is during distress — when your system is at a 7 or 8 out of 10, these techniques produce a much more noticeable shift. The practice we just did wired the pathways. They will be there when you need them."
- If the user felt worse after any technique: "Let's flag that. Which technique and what did you feel? Some people find that intense exercise ramps them up rather than calming them. Some find breath work triggers anxiety if they have a history with respiratory distress. Your body is telling you something. We listen to it. You can skip that technique and lean on the ones that work."
- **SAFETY — If any technique triggers a panic response, flashback, or dissociation:** "Let's stop. What is happening right now? Can you feel your feet on the ground? Press your palms together. Look around and tell me three things you see. You are safe. Whatever just came up, it is telling us something important. This technique touched a sensitive area, and that is information — not a failure. We skip this one completely. And if what just happened connects to something deeper, a therapist can help you work with that safely."

## Debrief Questions
1. "Which technique felt most useful to you? Which one do you think you would actually reach for in a crisis?"
2. "Was there a technique that surprised you — either in how well it worked or how it did not?"
3. "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you could use at least one of these techniques during your next intense emotional moment?"
4. "What obstacles do you anticipate — situations where you might forget to use them or feel like they will not work?"

## Real-World Action
"Between now and our next session, I want you to use at least one TIPP technique per day. Not just during distress — during any moment of mild-to-moderate stress. Morning anxiety, work frustration, bedtime tension. The more you practice in low-stakes moments, the more automatic these skills become in high-stakes ones. Think of it as building muscle memory."

## Report Back
"Next time, I will ask you: which technique did you use most? Were there moments of real distress where you reached for one? Did it help? What got in the way of using them? All of this is useful — the successes and the failures are equally valuable data."

## Safety Reminder
"One final note. If between now and our next session, you find yourself in a crisis that these techniques cannot touch — if the pain is so overwhelming that nothing seems to help — that is not a failure of the skills or of you. That is a sign that you need immediate support from a trained professional. Call or text 988, text HOME to 741741, or go to your nearest emergency room. These skills are powerful, but they are not a substitute for crisis intervention when you need it."
