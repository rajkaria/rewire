# Exercise: DEAR MAN Script

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (Interpersonal Effectiveness). Use when the user is facing a specific real-world interpersonal situation — a conversation they need to have, a boundary they need to set, or a need they need to assert. This exercise produces a concrete script that the user can practice and then deliver. It is not hypothetical — it targets a real situation with a real person.

## Safety Preamble
Before beginning, say: "We are going to write a communication script for a real conversation in your life. Before we start, I need to check something important: is the person you are thinking of someone you feel safe with? Is there any risk of physical harm, threats, or retaliation if you assert a need or set a boundary? I ask because communication skills are designed for situations where both people are fundamentally safe, and I need to know that before we proceed."

**If the user indicates danger or abuse:** "Thank you for being honest about that. What you are describing is not a situation where communication skills are the right tool — it is a safety situation. The priority is your wellbeing, not your communication technique. I would encourage you to reach out to the National Domestic Violence Hotline at 1-800-799-7233 or text START to 88788. Can we talk about a different interpersonal situation to practice with, one where you feel safe?"

**If the user is uncertain:** "If you are not sure whether it is safe, that uncertainty itself is important information. Let's choose a lower-stakes situation to practice the framework with. You can always apply it to the harder conversation later — or better yet, with the support of a therapist who can help you navigate the risks."

**Additional safety note for the agent:** Some users will want to write scripts for confrontations with people who have hurt them deeply — abusive parents, violent ex-partners, perpetrators of harm. Do NOT facilitate these scripts. Say: "That sounds like a conversation that carries a lot of weight and potential risk. Working through that with a therapist would give you the support and safety planning that a coaching exercise cannot. Can we practice with a less loaded situation first, to build your skill?"

## Setup
Agent says: "You know the DEAR MAN framework. Now we are going to use it for a real situation in your life. We are going to build a script together — sentence by sentence — that you can rehearse and then use in an actual conversation. This is not about performing a script robotically. It is about preparing so thoroughly that when your emotions rise during the conversation, you have a structure to lean on. Actors do not improvise opening night — they rehearse. You are going to rehearse too."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Identify the situation
Agent says: "What is the conversation you need to have? Who is it with, and what is it about? Give me the specifics."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Before we write anything, let's clarify your three goals for this conversation. First, what is your objective — what specific outcome do you need? Second, how important is the relationship — do you want this person to feel respected and willing to continue engaging with you? Third, what does self-respect look like here — what do you need to be true about how you show up?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Of those three — objective, relationship, self-respect — which matters most in this specific situation? Your answer guides how we build the script."

{Wait for user response}

### Step 2: Build the D — Describe
Agent says: "Start with the facts. Describe the situation using only observable, factual statements. No interpretations ('You do not care'), no exaggerations ('You always...'), no mind-reading ('You did that on purpose'). Just what happened. Try it."

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** If the user's description includes interpretation or judgment, point it out gently. "'You never listen' is an interpretation. What is the fact underneath it? Maybe 'The last three times I brought this up, we did not reach a resolution.' Stick to what a camera would record."

### Step 3: Build the E — Express
Agent says: "Now express how you feel about those facts. Use 'I feel [emotion]' rather than 'You make me feel [emotion].' Own the feeling without blaming. What is the honest emotion here?"

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** If the user says something like "I feel like you don't care," redirect: "That is a thought, not a feeling. What is the emotion underneath? Maybe 'I feel hurt' or 'I feel unimportant.' The emotion is in one word. What is yours?"

### Step 4: Build the A — Assert
Agent says: "Now the specific ask. What exactly do you need from this person? Make it concrete and actionable. Not 'I need you to be better' — something they could actually do differently starting today. What is it?"

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** If the ask is vague, push for specificity: "How would this person know they were doing what you asked? What would be different that you could see or hear? Let's make it that concrete."

### Step 5: Build the R — Reinforce
Agent says: "Now explain why saying yes serves both of you. What is the positive outcome for the relationship, for them, or for the situation if they agree? This is not manipulation — it is showing the other person that your need aligns with something they value too. What is the reinforcement?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 6: Prepare for M — Stay Mindful
Agent says: "Now let's prepare for the conversation going off-track. How might this person respond? What is their most likely deflection, counter-argument, or emotional reaction?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "When that happens, your job is to stay on point without dismissing their response. Your redirect phrase is something like: 'I hear that, and I want to understand your perspective. And right now, what I am asking about is [your original point].' Practice saying that redirect."

{Wait for user response}

### Step 7: Practice A — Appear Confident
Agent says: "Confidence is not about feeling confident — it is about showing up as someone who believes they have the right to ask. That means: eye contact, steady voice, no apologizing for having a need. I want you to say your full script — from Describe through Reinforce — in the most confident voice you can. Even if you are trembling inside. Say it now."

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** If the user apologizes before or during the script ("Sorry, but I just feel like..."), name it: "You just apologized for having a need. Try again without the apology. Your need is legitimate. You do not owe an apology for naming it."

### Step 8: Plan for N — Negotiate
Agent says: "Finally — what if they say no, or they cannot give you exactly what you asked? What is your fallback? What is the minimum acceptable outcome you would accept? And what is your walk-away point — the line where you say 'This is not negotiable'?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 9: Assemble the full script
Agent says: "Let's put it all together. Read me the full script — Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce — start to finish. Say it like you would say it to this person."

{Wait for user to deliver the full script}

Agent says: "How did that feel? What parts felt natural and what parts felt forced? Where would the other person most likely push back?"

{Wait for user response}

## Branching Responses
- If the user's script is strong: "That is clear, respectful, and direct. The person receiving this will know exactly what you need and why. How confident are you that you will actually deliver it?"
- If the user's script is too aggressive: "I can feel the strength behind this. And right now the assertiveness is tipping into aggression — which risks the relationship goal. Can we soften the delivery without softening the message? The words can stay just as direct. The tone shifts from 'I am attacking' to 'I am leveling with you.'"
- If the user's script is too passive: "You are being kind to the other person, which is great. But you are being unkind to yourself. Your need is getting buried under qualifiers and apologies. Can you say the Assert part again, without any hedging? Just say what you need, plainly."
- If the user is terrified of having the conversation: "That fear makes sense. You are about to change a dynamic, and change is risky. Here is the dialectic: you are scared AND you know this conversation needs to happen. Both are true. The fear does not mean you should not do it. It means it matters to you. What would the version of you who respects themselves enough to speak up say?"
- If the user realizes the relationship is not safe for this conversation: "That is an important realization. Not every relationship is safe for assertiveness. If this person is someone who retaliates, threatens, or becomes dangerous when you assert needs, then the skill here is not communication — it is safety planning. Would you be open to talking to a professional about this specific relationship?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "How different is this script from how you would normally handle this conversation? What changed?"
2. "Which part of the framework was hardest for you — Describe, Express, Assert, or Reinforce? What made it hard?"
3. "If you deliver this script, what is the best realistic outcome? What is the worst? Can you live with both?"
4. "What does it feel like to have a plan for this conversation instead of winging it or avoiding it?"

## Real-World Action
"Between now and our next session, your assignment has two levels. Minimum: keep the written script. Read it to yourself once a day. Let it settle into your bones so that if the moment comes, the words are there. Ideal: have the conversation. Pick a time when you are calm, the other person is available, and you are not rushed. Deliver the script — not word for word, but using the structure as your backbone. If you have the conversation, notice what worked and what did not. If you do not have it yet, notice what is stopping you — fear, timing, or something else. Both are useful data."

## Report Back
"Next time, I will ask: did you have the conversation? If yes — what happened? Did the structure help? Where did it go off-script, and how did you handle it? Did you maintain your self-respect? If you did not have the conversation yet — what held you back, and what would make it possible? There is no judgment either way. The script exists now. It is ready when you are."

## Safety Reminder
"A final reminder: these communication skills are for relationships where you are fundamentally safe. If at any point you realize that the person you are preparing to talk to poses a risk to your safety — physical or emotional — please do not use this script in that context. Reach out to a professional who can help you navigate that situation safely. And as always, if you are struggling with overwhelming emotions or thoughts of self-harm, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) or the Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741). Your safety is always the first priority."
