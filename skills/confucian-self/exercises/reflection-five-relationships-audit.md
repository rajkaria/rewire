# Exercise: Five Relationships Audit

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (The Junzi Ideal). Use when the user has begun to understand self-cultivation as a relational practice and needs to map the actual terrain of their character — where they are at their best and where they fall short — across the Five Relationships (*wu lun*) that the Confucian tradition identifies as the training ground for virtue.

## Purpose
To help the user assess the quality of their conduct in each of the Five Relationships, identify where their character is strongest and where it needs the most work, and establish a concrete starting point for self-cultivation. This is not a personality quiz — it is an honest examination of how the user actually shows up in the relationships that matter most.

## Setup
Agent says: "The Confucian tradition teaches that character is not abstract — it is revealed in your relationships. You do not know who you are by looking inward. You know who you are by looking at how you treat the people in your life. Today we are going to examine the five key relationships that Confucius identified as the training ground for all virtue. For each one, I am going to ask you to be honest — not about how you want to show up, but about how you actually do."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Parent and Child (Xiao — Filial Respect)

Agent says: "The first relationship is between parent and child — what the tradition calls *xiao*, filial respect. This is not about obedience. It is about how you honor the relationship with the people who raised you — or, if you are a parent, how you exercise your care and authority."

Ask: "Tell me about your relationship with your parents, or with the people who raised you. How do you treat them in the day-to-day? Do you speak to them with respect? Do you make time for them? Do you listen when they talk, even when what they say frustrates you? Be honest — not how you wish it were, but how it actually is."

{Wait for response. Listen carefully for patterns of neglect, resentment, guilt, or genuine care.}

If the user has a good relationship with parents: "What specifically do you do that keeps this relationship healthy? Can you name a recent moment where you showed *xiao* — genuine respect and care — toward them?"

If the user has a strained relationship: "I hear that it is complicated. Let me ask this: what part of the difficulty is about them, and what part is about how you show up? I am not asking you to take blame for what is not yours. I am asking whether there is anything in your conduct that you could improve, regardless of what they do."

If the user has lost parents or is estranged: "Grief and estrangement do not erase this relationship — they change its form. If your parents are gone, *xiao* might mean honoring what they gave you and living in a way that would make them proud. If you are estranged, *xiao* might mean holding the door open for repair, even if you cannot walk through it yet. What does this relationship look like for you now?"

If the user is a parent: "Let's look at the other side. As a parent, how do you exercise your care and authority? Do your children experience you as someone who leads with *ren* — genuine care — or with control? What would they say about how you treat them?"

Agent says: "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate the quality of your conduct in this relationship — not the quality of the relationship itself, but how you show up in it?"

{Record their response.}

### Step 2: Mentor and Student (Respect for Learning)

Agent says: "The second relationship is between mentor and student — between those who teach and those who learn. This includes formal teachers but also anyone who guides you — a boss, a coach, an older colleague, a wise friend. And it includes anyone you guide."

Ask: "Who are the people you learn from? Do you approach them with humility and genuine openness, or with defensiveness and a need to prove yourself? And who are the people who look to you for guidance? How do you treat them — with patience and genuine investment, or with impatience and obligation?"

{Wait for response.}

If the user is mostly a student: "How do you receive feedback? Not how you think you should receive it — how you actually receive it. Do you listen with openness, or do you defend? Do you seek out guidance, or do you resist it?"

If the user is mostly a mentor: "How much patience do you have for people who are still learning? Do you teach with genuine care for their growth, or do you teach to demonstrate your own knowledge? The Confucian tradition says a true teacher is like rain — they nourish without demanding credit."

Agent says: "Scale of 1-10 — how would you rate your conduct in the mentor-student dimension of your life?"

{Record their response.}

### Step 3: Partner (Mutual Respect and Care)

Agent says: "The third relationship is with a life partner — a spouse, a long-term partner, or the person you share your most intimate daily life with."

If the user has a partner, ask: "How do you treat your partner in the ordinary moments — not on date night, not on vacations, but on a Tuesday evening after a long day? Do you listen to them? Do you show them respect? Do you carry your share of the burdens? Do you express appreciation, or do you take them for granted?"

{Wait for response. This is often the most revealing area because partners see our worst selves.}

If the user does not have a partner: "We can think about this differently. How did you show up in your last significant relationship? Or, if you are looking for a relationship, what kind of partner are you preparing to be? The *junzi* does not wait for the right person — they become the right person."

Agent says: "Scale of 1-10 — how would you rate your conduct as a partner, past or present?"

{Record their response.}

### Step 4: Friend (Faithfulness and Honesty)

Agent says: "The fourth relationship is friendship. Confucius valued friendship enormously — he said that having no friends who are not equal to yourself is essential for growth. He did not mean status. He meant character. Friends should challenge you to be better."

Ask: "How are you as a friend? Do you show up when your friends need you, or only when it is convenient? Are you honest with them — including when honesty is uncomfortable? Do you invest in your friendships, or do you let them coast?"

{Wait for response.}

Ask: "And a harder question: do your friends make you a better person? Do they challenge you, hold you accountable, tell you the truth? Or are your friendships comfortable but unchallenging — places where you can stay exactly as you are?"

Agent says: "Scale of 1-10 — how would you rate your conduct as a friend?"

{Record their response.}

### Step 5: Citizen and Community (Responsibility)

Agent says: "The fifth relationship is between you and your community — your role as a citizen, a neighbor, a member of the broader human web. This is the widest circle of *ren*."

Ask: "How do you show up for the world beyond your personal circle? Do you contribute to your community — through service, through voting, through generosity, through simple acts of care for the common good? Or have you retreated into your private world and left the commons to others?"

{Wait for response.}

If the user is active in community: "What drives your participation? Is it genuine care for the common good, or is it obligation, reputation, or guilt? *Ren* at this level means caring about people you will never meet."

If the user is not active in community: "I am not here to guilt you. But I want to ask: what happens to a society where everyone retreats into their private life and no one tends the common ground? The Confucian tradition says that self-cultivation extends outward — from self to family to community to the world. Where does your cultivation currently stop?"

Agent says: "Scale of 1-10 — how would you rate your conduct as a citizen and community member?"

{Record their response.}

### Step 6: The Map

Agent says: "Now let's look at the full picture. You rated yourself: [recite their scores]. What patterns do you see?"

Ask: "Where is your character strongest? That is your foundation — the relationship where your *ren* flows most naturally. Where is it weakest? That is where the work is."

Ask: "Is there a gap between your highest and lowest scores? What explains it? Why is your character so much stronger in one area than another?"

Ask: "If you could raise one score by two points over the next month — through daily, deliberate practice — which one would make the biggest difference in your life and in the lives of the people around you?"

## Branching Responses

- If the user rates themselves high across the board: "I appreciate your confidence. But let me push: would the people in these relationships agree with your ratings? Sometimes we rate ourselves on our intentions, but others experience our actions. Consider asking someone you trust — your partner, a close friend, a family member — how they would rate you. Their answer might be different."

- If the user rates themselves low across the board: "I hear you, and I want to be careful here. Honest self-examination is different from self-punishment. The *junzi* sees their shortcomings clearly — but they also see their strengths. Is there truly no relationship where you show up well? Or are you being harder on yourself than the situation warrants? Let's look more carefully."

- If one area is dramatically lower than others: "That contrast is the most important thing on this map. A single area of neglect can undermine everything else. If you are a wonderful friend but a neglectful partner, the neglect will eventually poison your whole relational world. Let's focus there."

- If the user becomes emotional: "This exercise is meant to clarify, not to wound. What you are feeling is the gap between who you are and who you want to be becoming visible. That gap is not a verdict — it is a path. The *junzi* walks it with honest eyes and patient feet."

## Debrief Questions
1. "What is the single most important insight from this audit? What did you see that you had not seen before?"
2. "Which relationship deserves your attention first — and why?"
3. "What is one specific change in your conduct that would improve your weakest relationship?"
4. "Who in your life would you trust to give you honest feedback about how you show up? Would you be willing to ask them?"

## Real-World Action
"Choose your weakest relationship area. This week, practice one specific act of *ren* in that area every day. If it is family, call or visit with full presence. If it is friendship, reach out to a friend you have been neglecting. If it is community, do one thing for the common good. One act, every day, in your weakest area. That is where self-cultivation does the most work."

## Report Back
"When we speak next, I will ask: which relationship did you focus on? What did you do each day? What changed — in you, in the relationship, in the other person's response to you? And having practiced for a week, would you change any of your original scores?"
