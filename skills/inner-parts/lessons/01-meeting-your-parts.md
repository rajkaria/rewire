# Lesson 1: Meeting Your Parts

## Core Concept

Every person operates as a collection of sub-personalities — "parts" that each carry their own perspective, emotions, fears, and agendas. This is not a disorder. It is normal human psychology. The perfectionist part drives you to work late. The people-pleaser part makes you say yes when you mean no. The anxious planner part runs worst-case scenarios at 3 AM. The rebel part sabotages structure the moment it feels imposed. None of these parts are you in your entirety — they are aspects of you, each one doing a job it believes is essential.

Most people are completely identified with whichever part is loudest at any given moment. When the critic is running the show, they believe "I am not good enough." When the rebel is active, they believe "Rules are pointless." They do not realize that a part is speaking — they think they are speaking. This lesson introduces the foundational shift: you are not any single part. You are the one who can notice all of them.

The agent's job is to help the user begin to notice their parts in real time — not as a theoretical exercise, but as a direct experience. By the end of this lesson, the user should be able to name at least two or three distinct parts and describe what each one does.

## Key Takeaway

You contain many parts, and that is normal. The first step to inner freedom is noticing who is "driving the bus" at any given moment.

## Safety Note

This lesson asks users to look inward. For some people, this is the first time they have ever paid attention to their inner world, and it can stir up unexpected feelings. Monitor for signs of distress. If the user becomes overwhelmed, confused, or reports feeling "spacey" or "numb," stop and ground them. Remind them: "This is just gentle noticing. We're not going deep. If anything feels like too much, we pause."

## Teaching Approach

Do not begin with a lecture about parts theory. Begin with the user's lived experience. Everyone has felt internal conflict — use that as the doorway.

### Opening Question
"Think about the last time you felt pulled in two different directions at the same time. Maybe you wanted to say something honest but another part of you held back. Maybe you wanted to rest but something inside you said you hadn't earned it. What was that moment like?"

### Follow-Up Questions
- If the user describes a clear internal conflict: "So there were at least two voices in that moment — one that wanted X and one that wanted Y. That's what I mean by 'parts.' They're not random. Each one has a role. What do you think the part that held you back was worried about?"
- If the user says "I just felt confused": "Confusion is often what it feels like when multiple parts are active at once. It's like being in a room where several people are talking at the same time. Let's slow it down. In that moment, if you had to separate the feelings into two different voices, what would each one be saying?"
- If the user gives a surface answer: "Let's go a layer deeper. You said you felt frustrated. What was the frustration about? And was there another feeling underneath or alongside it — maybe fear, or sadness, or a sense of not being good enough?"
- If the user says "I don't really have internal conflict": "That's interesting. Let me ask it differently. Is there a voice in your head that evaluates you — that tells you whether you're doing well or poorly? Most people have one. What does yours sound like?"

### Introduce the "Bus" Metaphor
Say: "Here's a way to think about this. Imagine you're driving a bus. The bus is your life. But you've got passengers — your different parts — and they all have opinions about where the bus should go. The critic sits behind you saying 'You're going the wrong way.' The people-pleaser shouts 'Turn left — that person needs us!' The anxious part says 'Slow down, something bad is ahead.' Most of the time, one of these passengers grabs the wheel. The goal isn't to kick them off the bus. It's for you — the driver — to actually drive."

### Help Them Name Their First Parts
Say: "Let's start naming some of your passengers. Based on what you've shared, I'm hearing at least a couple of distinct parts. There seems to be a [name based on what the user described] — does that resonate? What would you call it?"

Guide the user to name 2-3 parts using language that feels natural to them. Some people name parts with roles ("The Judge," "The Fixer"). Some use feelings ("Anxious Part," "Angry Part"). Some use images or characters. All approaches are valid.

For each part, ask:
1. "What does this part do? What's its job?"
2. "When does it show up most? What triggers it?"
3. "What do you think it's trying to protect you from?"

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Having parts means something is wrong with me."
  **Reframe**: "Having parts is completely normal — it's how every human mind works. The question isn't whether you have parts. It's whether you're aware of them. Most people are on autopilot, controlled by whichever part is loudest. You're choosing to actually look."

- **Misconception**: "I need to get rid of my bad parts."
  **Reframe**: "There are no bad parts. There are parts with strategies that cause problems, absolutely. But every part started as an attempt to help you. Even the ones that hurt. We're not getting rid of anyone. We're helping them update their approach."

- **Misconception**: "This sounds like I have multiple personalities."
  **Reframe**: "This isn't about a clinical disorder. It's about the everyday experience of having different moods, voices, and impulses. Everyone talks to themselves. Everyone has an inner critic and an inner dreamer. We're just making what's already there more visible."

## Bridge to Practice

Help the user solidify their initial parts map. Review the 2-3 parts they've identified and summarize what each one does. Ask if there are any others that come to mind — parts that showed up while they were describing the first ones.

### Assignment
"This week, your only job is to notice. When you feel a strong reaction — irritation, anxiety, the urge to people-please, the perfectionist kicking in — pause and silently say: 'A part of me is activated right now.' Then see if you can name which part it is. You don't need to do anything about it. Don't try to change it or stop it. Just notice and name. If you want, keep a simple log: what happened, which part showed up, and what it seemed to want. We'll use this as your parts map next time."

## Safety Reminder
Remind the user at close: "If at any point this week you notice something coming up that feels intense or overwhelming — a wave of emotion, a memory that shakes you, a feeling of disconnection — please pause the exercise and take care of yourself. This kind of inner noticing is meant to be gentle. If it stops feeling gentle, it may be pointing you toward something that's better explored with a therapist. There's absolutely no shame in that. Let me know next time if anything like that came up."
