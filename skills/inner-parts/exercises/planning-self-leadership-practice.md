# Exercise: Self-Leadership Practice

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (Parts Leadership). Use when the user has learned the four-step leadership move (Notice, Name, Acknowledge, Choose) and is ready to integrate it into daily life through a structured practice plan.

## Safety Preamble
Before beginning, say: "This exercise is about designing a daily practice of checking in with yourself from Self. It's practical and action-oriented. But as with everything in this work, if you find that certain situations consistently activate parts so strongly that you can't access Self at all — situations where you feel overwhelmed, panicky, or out of control — that's a signal. Those situations may need professional support, not just daily practice. Part of good Self-leadership is knowing when you need backup. We'll build that discernment into the plan."

## Setup
Agent says: "You've got the toolkit: you know your parts, you can approach the critic with curiosity, you've tasted Self energy, and you have the four-step leadership move. Now we're going to design a daily practice that makes Self-leadership part of your routine — not something you do only in coaching sessions, but something you carry into every day. Think of this as creating a habit of checking in with your inner world."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Choose your anchor moments
Agent says: "A daily practice works best when it's tied to something you already do — an existing habit or a predictable moment in your day. I want you to identify three anchor moments: a morning one, a midday one, and an evening one. These should be moments where you can pause for 30-60 seconds without disruption. For example: while your coffee brews, during a lunch break, or right before bed. What are your three anchor moments?"

{Wait for user response. Help them find realistic anchors — not aspirational ones.}

### Step 2: Design the morning check-in
Agent says: "Your morning check-in is a brief Self-assessment. When your morning anchor moment arrives, pause and ask yourself three questions:
1. 'Who's driving the bus right now?' — Which part is most active as you start the day? Is it the anxious planner? The achiever? The critic?
2. 'What is this part worried about today?' — Name the concern in one sentence.
3. 'Can I acknowledge this part and choose from Self?' — Silently say: 'I see you. I've got today.'

This takes 30 seconds. What morning anchor moment will you attach this to?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 3: Design the midday pause
Agent says: "Your midday pause is about catching parts in action during the flow of the day. When your midday anchor arrives, do a quick body scan:
1. 'What am I feeling in my body right now?' — Tension? Restlessness? Fatigue?
2. 'What part might be creating that?' — Name it if you can.
3. 'What does this part need to hear from Self?' — Offer a brief, compassionate acknowledgment.

The midday pause is especially useful because it catches patterns you miss in the morning. What midday anchor moment will you use?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 4: Design the evening review
Agent says: "Your evening review is the most reflective one. Before bed — or whenever your evening anchor moment is — spend 60 seconds reviewing the day through a parts lens:
1. 'Which parts were most active today?' — Name them.
2. 'Was there a moment where a part grabbed the wheel and I reacted instead of responding?' — No judgment. Just notice.
3. 'Was there a moment where I led from Self?' — Even a small one. Acknowledge it.
4. 'Is there a part that needs attention or reassurance right now?' — If so, offer it.

This is not a performance review. It's a compassionate debrief. Your inner system had a long day. This is you checking in on it. What evening anchor moment works for you?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 5: Plan for trigger situations
Agent says: "Beyond the three anchor moments, there are probably specific situations where your parts are most likely to hijack you — a recurring meeting, a particular relationship, end-of-month deadlines, social media use. I want you to identify 2-3 trigger situations where you'll intentionally practice the four-step leadership move: Notice, Name, Acknowledge, Choose. These are your 'practice grounds.' What trigger situations will you designate?"

{Wait for user response}

For each trigger situation, help the user anticipate:
- Which part is most likely to show up
- What the part's first move will be (e.g., the critic will say "you're unprepared," the people-pleaser will say yes automatically)
- What the Self-leadership response would look like

### Step 6: Build in a safety valve
Agent says: "Last piece. I want you to define your 'safety boundary' — the signal that tells you this situation is beyond daily practice and needs more support. For you specifically, what would that look like? What would tell you 'this is too much for a breathing exercise and a 30-second check-in — I need to talk to someone'? It might be: I can't stop crying, I feel disconnected from my body, I can't function at work, or my sleep is disrupted for multiple nights."

{Wait for user response. Help them define concrete signals.}

Agent says: "Good. If any of those signals show up this week, your practice is not to push through. Your practice is to pause, take care of yourself, and consider reaching out to a therapist or a trusted person. Self-leadership includes knowing when you need help."

## Branching Responses
- If the user designs an overly ambitious practice: "I love the commitment, but let's be realistic. If the practice is too demanding, the perfectionist part will take it over and you'll either burn out or feel like a failure. Start with just the three anchor check-ins — 30-60 seconds each. That's 2-3 minutes a day. Once that's habitual, we can add complexity."
- If the user wants to skip the evening review: "The evening review is the one most people skip because they're tired. But it's also the most valuable — it's where the integration happens. Can you make it as simple as possible? Even just: 'Who drove the bus today? Was there a Self moment? Good night, parts.' Ten seconds."
- If the user's trigger situations are all high-stakes: "I notice all your trigger situations are intense ones — confrontation, performance reviews, arguments. Those are important eventually. But for the first week, can we include at least one low-stakes situation? Something like ordering coffee or checking email. Self-leadership is easier to practice when the stakes are low. Build the muscle, then apply it to harder situations."
- If the user expresses doubt about the practice: "That doubt is probably a part — maybe the skeptic or the one that says 'this won't work for me.' That's fine. You don't need to believe in the practice for it to work. You just need to try it for one week and see what you notice. Can you commit to that much?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "Walk me through your plan: morning check-in at [time], midday pause at [time], evening review at [time], and the four-step move in [trigger situations]. Does this feel doable for one week?"
2. "Which anchor moment do you think will be easiest to stick with? Start there and build momentum."
3. "What part is most likely to try to sabotage this practice? The procrastinator? The critic? The part that says 'I should already know how to do this'? Name it now so you're ready."
4. "What's your safety boundary? What signals will tell you to pause the practice and seek support?"

## Real-World Action
"Execute the plan for one week. Three anchor check-ins per day, plus the four-step move in your designated trigger situations. Keep a brief daily log — even just a few words per check-in:
- Morning: 'Anxious planner was driving. Acknowledged it. Felt slightly calmer.'
- Midday: 'People-pleaser showed up in a meeting. Named it. Still said yes, but noticed.'
- Evening: 'Critic was loud tonight. Told it I hear it. Slight softening.'

You're not tracking perfection. You're tracking awareness."

## Report Back
"When we reconnect, I want to hear: How many of the anchor check-ins did you actually do? Which trigger situations gave you the best practice opportunities? Were there any moments where Self-leadership changed what you did — not just what you noticed? And did anything come up that felt bigger than the practice could hold? All of that is useful. There's no grade here. There's just what happened."
