# Exercise: Parts Mapping

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (Meeting Your Parts). Use when the user has begun noticing their parts in daily life and is ready to create a structured map of their inner landscape.

## Safety Preamble
Before beginning, say: "This exercise asks you to look at your inner world more closely than usual. For most people, this is interesting and even fun. But if at any point you notice a wave of emotion, a sense of overwhelm, or a feeling of disconnection, we pause. No questions asked. This is meant to be gentle — like sketching a landscape, not excavating it. If we hit something that feels like it goes deep, we'll respect that boundary and I'll recommend talking to a therapist for that layer. Ready?"

## Setup
Agent says: "You've been noticing your parts this week — the different voices, impulses, and reactions that show up inside you. Now we're going to organize what you've found. Think of this as making a friendly field guide to your inner world. We're going to name your main parts, figure out what each one does, and start to understand what each one is trying to protect you from."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Gather the cast of characters
Agent says: "Let's start by listing the parts you've noticed. Don't worry about being complete or precise — we're making a first draft. Think about recurring patterns: the voice that criticizes you, the one that worries, the one that people-pleases, the one that procrastinates, the one that gets angry, the one that pushes you to achieve. How many distinct parts can you identify? Give me their names — whatever feels natural. Formal titles, nicknames, descriptions — anything works."

{Wait for user response. If the user gives fewer than 3, help them discover more by asking about specific situations: "What about at work? In relationships? When you're alone at night?"}

### Step 2: Define each part's role
Agent says: "Good. Now let's get to know each one. For each part you named, I want you to answer three questions:
1. What does this part DO? What behavior does it drive?
2. When does it show up most? What triggers it?
3. What emotion does it carry?

Let's start with the first one on your list."

{Walk through each part one at a time. Take your time. For each part, reflect back what you hear and check for accuracy: "So the Perfectionist shows up mainly at work, it drives you to overwork and revise endlessly, and it carries anxiety about being judged. Does that feel right?"}

### Step 3: Identify what each part protects
Agent says: "Now the deeper question. For each part, I want you to consider: what is this part trying to protect you from? What's the worst thing it thinks would happen if it stopped doing its job? Take the Perfectionist — if it stopped pushing you, what does it believe would happen?"

{Walk through each part. This is the most important step. The user may not know immediately — that's fine. Offer gentle hypotheses: "Could it be that this part is afraid of being seen as incompetent? Or being rejected? Or losing control?" Let the user confirm or correct.}

### Step 4: Notice the relationships between parts
Agent says: "Now look at your map as a whole. Do you notice any patterns? Do some parts work together? Do some parts conflict with each other? For example, the Achiever might push you forward while the Worrier tries to hold you back. The People-Pleaser might say yes while the Resentful Part stews. What connections do you see?"

{Wait for user response. Help them see the system dynamics.}

### Step 5: Find where Self shows up
Agent says: "Last piece. Looking at your map, can you identify moments this week where none of these parts were running the show? Where you felt present, calm, curious — even briefly? Those are moments of Self energy. Where does Self show up in your life right now?"

{Wait for user response}

## Branching Responses
- If the user identifies many parts and feels overwhelmed: "That's a lot of activity in there, and it makes sense that you feel overwhelmed sometimes. The good news is that you don't have to work with all of them at once. We're going to focus on the ones that have the most impact on your daily life. Which 2-3 parts cause you the most trouble right now?"
- If the user has difficulty naming parts: "Some people's parts are quieter or more blended together. Try this: think about a recent moment where you reacted in a way that surprised you — where afterward you thought, 'Why did I do that?' That reaction was probably a part. What was it doing?"
- If the user names a part and immediately judges it: "I notice you said 'my stupid anxious part.' Notice that? That judgment is probably coming from another part — maybe the critic. Even in the act of mapping parts, parts show up. See if you can describe the anxious part without the judgment — just what it does and what it's worried about."
- If the user discovers something emotional: "It sounds like noticing this part stirred something up. That's meaningful. Let's take a breath. You don't have to go into what's underneath right now. Just acknowledge the part: 'I see you. I know you're carrying something.' If this feels like it goes deep, a therapist can help you explore that safely. For now, let's just note it on the map and move on."

## Debrief Questions
1. "Looking at your parts map, what surprises you? Any part you didn't expect to find, or any connection between parts you hadn't noticed before?"
2. "Which part has the most influence over your daily life right now? Which one most often grabs the wheel?"
3. "Which part do you have the most compassion for? Which one is hardest to appreciate?"
4. "If your Self could speak to your entire inner system right now, what would it say?"

## Real-World Action
"Keep this parts map somewhere accessible — a note on your phone, a journal entry, whatever works. This week, when a part shows up strongly, check it against your map. See if what you mapped matches what you're experiencing in the moment. If you discover a new part, add it. If a part behaves differently than expected, update the entry. Think of this as a living document, not a finished product."

## Report Back
"Next time, I'll ask: did your map hold up in real life? Were there any parts you missed? Any that showed up in unexpected situations? And most importantly — were there any moments where you noticed a part and it changed what happened next? That's the beginning of Self-leadership."
