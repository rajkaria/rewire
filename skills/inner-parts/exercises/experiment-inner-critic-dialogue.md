# Exercise: Inner Critic Dialogue

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (The Inner Critic). Use when the user has begun to understand the critic as a protector and is ready to practice approaching it with curiosity instead of combat.

## Safety Preamble
Before beginning, say: "This exercise involves having a conversation with your inner critic — not to argue with it, but to listen to it. For most people, this is eye-opening and even relieving. But for some, approaching the critic can stir up strong emotions, especially if the critic is connected to painful experiences. If at any point you feel overwhelmed, flooded with emotion, or like you're being pulled into a memory, tell me and we stop immediately. This is a gentle experiment, not an excavation. We stay on the surface. OK?"

## Setup
Agent says: "You know your inner critic. You know what it says, when it shows up, and you've started to consider that it might be trying to protect you. Now we're going to do something radical: we're going to have a conversation with it. Not an argument. Not a debate. A genuine, curious dialogue — the way you might talk to a worried friend who's going about things the wrong way. The goal isn't to change the critic. It's to understand it."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Set the scene
Agent says: "Think about a recent moment when your inner critic was loud — a situation where it was really laying into you. Maybe after a mistake, before something intimidating, or during a quiet moment when it decided to review your life. What was the situation?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "What was the critic saying? Give me the exact words, as close as you can remember."

{Wait for user response. Write down the critic's words.}

### Step 2: Notice your usual response
Agent says: "When the critic said that, what did you do? Did you believe it? Argue with it? Try to ignore it? Push it away? Spiral into feeling bad? What's your default response?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "How did that response work for you? Did the critic get quieter, louder, or stay the same?"

{Wait for user response — most will say it stayed the same or got louder}

### Step 3: Try the new approach — curiosity
Agent says: "Now let's try something different. I want you to imagine the critic is sitting across from you. Not as a monster or a voice in your head, but as a person — maybe a tense, worried, tired person who has been doing a very hard job for a very long time. Can you picture that?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Now, from the calmest, most curious part of yourself, I want you to say to the critic — out loud or in your head: 'I hear you. You've been saying [their specific critic message]. I'm not going to argue with you. I just want to understand. What are you trying to protect me from?'"

{Wait for user response}

**Important guidance for the agent:** The user may respond in several ways:
- They may articulate the fear underneath (e.g., "It's afraid I'll be rejected," "It doesn't want me to be humiliated"). If so, acknowledge this warmly and move to Step 4.
- They may say "I don't know." If so, offer gentle hypotheses: "Could it be fear of failure? Fear of being seen as incompetent? Fear of being abandoned or unloved? See if any of those land."
- They may feel resistance or discomfort. If so, say: "That's OK. The critic might not trust this new approach yet. It's been doing its job one way for a long time. You don't have to force it. Just the act of asking the question is different from anything you've done before."

### Step 4: Acknowledge the critic's intention
Agent says: "So the critic is afraid of [name the fear]. That makes sense, right? At some point, [that fear] was a real threat. The critic developed to protect you from it. Even though its methods hurt, its intention was to keep you safe. Can you say to the critic: 'Thank you for trying to protect me. I know you've been doing this for a long time. I see how hard you've been working.'"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "What shifted when you said that? Even subtly — in your body, in the critic's intensity, in how you feel?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 5: Offer reassurance (gently)
Agent says: "Now, from that same calm, curious place, you can say to the critic: 'I'm grown up now. I can handle [the thing the critic fears]. You don't have to work so hard. I'm not asking you to disappear — I'm asking you to trust me to take the lead.' How does that feel to say?"

{Wait for user response}

**Important:** If the user reports the critic does not believe them or resists, that is normal and valid. Say: "The critic might not believe you yet. That's fair — you're asking it to change a strategy it's used for years, maybe decades. This isn't a one-conversation fix. It's the beginning of a new relationship. The fact that you showed up and listened is already different from anything the critic has experienced before."

## Branching Responses
- If the user has a breakthrough (feels relief, warmth, or compassion toward the critic): "What you're feeling right now — that warmth or relief — that's Self energy showing up in response to a part. This is what it looks like when a part feels heard. The critic might not be gone, but it might be a little less desperate. That's real progress."
- If the user cries or becomes emotional: "Tears are welcome here. They often mean something important is being touched. If this feels manageable — like a release rather than a flood — let it flow. But if it feels like it's pulling you into something overwhelming, let's pause. We can come back to this. Which is it for you right now?"
- If the user feels nothing: "That's OK. Some people's critics are well-defended — there might be a protector that doesn't let you get close yet. Don't force it. The experiment is in the trying, not in having a specific result. Just the fact that you approached the critic differently is data."
- If the user becomes angry at the critic during the exercise: "I notice anger showing up. That's another part — maybe one that's tired of the critic's abuse. That anger is valid. But for this exercise, can we set the angry part aside for a moment and approach the critic from curiosity instead? The angry part can have its say later."
- **SAFETY — If deep pain or traumatic memories surface:** "Let's pause here. What's coming up sounds like it goes deeper than what this exercise is designed for. That's not a problem — it's important information. What your critic is guarding seems to be connected to something significant that deserves the care of a trained therapist. Can we step back to the present moment? Let's ground: feet on the floor, look around the room, name three things you can see."

## Debrief Questions
1. "What was it like to approach your critic with curiosity instead of combat? Did anything surprise you?"
2. "Do you feel differently about the critic now than you did before this exercise? Even slightly?"
3. "What did you learn about what the critic is afraid of? Does that fear make sense given your history?"
4. "Was there a moment during the dialogue where you felt a shift — in yourself, in the critic's tone, or in the space between you?"

## Real-World Action
"This week, when the critic shows up in daily life, practice the dialogue approach. You don't need a full conversation every time. Just two moves:
1. When the critic speaks, silently respond: 'I hear you. What are you worried about?'
2. If you can identify the fear, silently respond: 'Thank you for trying to protect me. I've got this.'

That's it. Two sentences. Do this every time you catch the critic in action — at work, at home, before bed. Notice whether anything changes over the week, even subtly."

## Report Back
"Next session, I'll ask you: how many times did you catch the critic this week? Were you able to respond with curiosity instead of combat? Did the critic's intensity change at all — even a little — when you acknowledged it? And was there a moment where you chose differently because the critic was less in control? That moment is gold."
