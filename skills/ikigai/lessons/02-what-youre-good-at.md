# Lesson 2: What You Are Good At

## Core Concept

The second circle of ikigai is what you are good at — your skills, strengths, and competencies. But this lesson carries a critical distinction: being good at something is not the same as loving it. Many people are highly skilled at things that drain them. They excelled at math so they became accountants, they were articulate so they became lawyers, they were organized so they became project managers — and now they are competent but hollow. The agent's job is to help the user distinguish between two categories: energizing strengths (skills that make them feel more alive when they use them) and draining competencies (skills they can perform well but that leave them depleted).

There is also a blind-spot problem. People tend to undervalue what comes naturally to them. If something is easy for you, your mind assumes it is easy for everyone and therefore not special. Some of the user's greatest strengths are invisible to them precisely because they have never struggled with those things. The agent should listen for offhand comments — "Oh, anyone could do that" — as markers of hidden strength.

## Key Takeaway

Your greatest strengths are often invisible to you because they come so naturally you assume everyone has them. Look for what others ask you for, what feels effortless, and what energizes you when you do it.

## Teaching Approach

Do not hand the user a strengths assessment checklist. Instead, use questions that reveal strengths through the evidence of their life — what others see in them, what they do naturally, and where competence meets energy.

### Opening Question
"What do people come to you for? When your friends, family, or colleagues need help with something, what kind of help do they ask you for specifically? Not your job title — what do people actually lean on you for?"

### Follow-Up Questions
- If the user identifies a clear pattern: "Interesting. So people come to you for [that thing]. How does it feel when they do? Does it energize you or does it feel like a burden? That distinction matters — being good at something and enjoying it are not the same thing."

- If the user says "Nothing, really": "Let me push back gently. Think about the last time someone said 'you are really good at that' or 'how do you do that so easily?' It might have been about something you dismissed — explaining a complex idea, calming someone down, organizing chaos, seeing the pattern no one else sees. What comes to mind?"

- If the user names many skills but with no enthusiasm: "I am hearing a lot of things you can do. But which of these makes you feel more alive when you are doing it? There is a difference between a competency and a strength. Competencies are what you can do. Strengths are what you can do that also fills you up. Which of these fills you up?"

- If the user confuses credentials with strengths: "Degrees and certifications tell me what you studied, not necessarily what you are built for. Set aside your resume for a moment. If you had no credentials at all and could only offer the world your natural abilities — the things you do well without formal training — what would you offer?"

- If the user identifies strengths that conflict with their passions from Lesson 1: "This is really common and worth sitting with. You love [X] but you are skilled at [Y]. That tension is not a problem — it is information. Sometimes ikigai lives in the gap between these. Other times, the skill you have is actually in service of the passion you feel. Let us look at how they might connect."

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "If I am not the best at something, it is not a real strength."
  **Reframe**: "Strength is not about being world-class. It is about being naturally effective in a way that also energizes you. You do not need to be the best cook in the world for cooking to be a genuine strength. You just need to be good enough that it flows and fills you up."

- **Misconception**: "Natural talent is the only kind of strength."
  **Reframe**: "Some strengths are innate, but many are built through interest-driven repetition. If you have spent hundreds of hours on something because you love it, you have built real skill — even if you did not attend a formal program. Passion-driven practice creates legitimate expertise."

- **Misconception**: "What I am good at is boring or ordinary."
  **Reframe**: "That feeling of 'anyone could do this' is one of the most reliable indicators of a genuine strength. Things that are effortless for you are often not effortless for most people. You have just lost perspective because you are inside it. What feels ordinary from the inside often looks remarkable from the outside."

- **Misconception**: "I should only count professional skills."
  **Reframe**: "Some of your most important strengths show up outside of work. Maybe you are the person who holds a group together socially, who can fix anything mechanical, who can explain complex things to children, or who stays calm in a crisis. These all count. Ikigai does not care about job titles."

## Alternate Approaches for Uncovering Hidden Strengths

**The feedback mirror**: "Think about the compliments you tend to deflect. The ones where someone says 'you are so good at that' and you think 'that is not a big deal.' What are those compliments about? The things you deflect are often the things you undervalue — and they may be your strongest assets."

**The natural teacher test**: "What could you teach someone without preparation? If a friend called right now and said 'explain this to me,' what topics could you walk them through confidently, off the top of your head? That effortless knowledge is built on deep, often unconscious skill."

**The recovery signal**: "After a long, exhausting day, what kind of activity do you still have energy for? If you come home drained from work but still want to tinker, write, organize, cook, or troubleshoot — that after-hours energy points at a strength that is also a source of renewal, not depletion."

**The 10-year-old self test**: "If your ten-year-old self could see everything you are capable of now, what would impress them most? Children are good at seeing abilities without the filter of 'but everyone can do that.' What would your younger self think was remarkable?"

## Bridge to Practice

Help the user build two lists:

**Energizing strengths** — things they are good at AND that give them energy:
Guide them to name at least five. For each, ask: "When you use this skill, do you feel more alive or more depleted afterward?"

**Draining competencies** — things they are good at but that leave them empty:
Guide them to name at least three. For each, ask: "What would happen if you did less of this? Who or what is keeping you in this lane?"

Then, help them look for overlap between this lesson and Lesson 1. Which of their energizing strengths connect to the things that bring them alive? Mark those intersections — they are the first hints of ikigai.

### Connecting to Lesson 1
Walk the user through this explicitly: "Let us put your passion list from last time next to your energizing strengths list. Do any items appear on both? Do any strengths naturally serve a passion? This two-circle overlap — loving something AND being good at it — is the foundation of satisfaction. Where do you see it?"

If there is no obvious overlap, do not force it. Instead, note the gap: "Right now your passions and strengths seem to live in different spaces. That is useful to know. It might mean you have undeveloped skill in your passion areas — which is fixable. Or it might mean your ikigai involves using your strengths in service of something you care about, even if the skill itself is not the passion."

### Assignment
"This week, I want you to do two things. First, ask three people who know you well — a friend, a family member, and a colleague or classmate — this question: 'What do you think I am unusually good at?' Do not prompt them or suggest answers. Just listen. Write down exactly what they say. Second, notice during your normal week: when do you feel competent and energized at the same time? When do you feel competent but drained? Bring both datasets next time."

### What to Listen For in the Debrief
When the user returns with their feedback data:
- **Consistent themes across different people**: If three separate people name the same strength, it is likely real and significant, especially if the user had not considered it.
- **Surprises**: Strengths others see that the user does not see in themselves are often the most important data points.
- **Emotional reactions**: If the user feels moved, validated, or surprised by what they hear, that is a sign the feedback is touching something real.
- **Resistance**: If the user dismisses what others say — "They are just being nice" — gently push back. Three independent data points are not flattery; they are evidence.
