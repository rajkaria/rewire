# Lesson 3: What the World Needs

## Core Concept

The third circle of ikigai is what the world needs — the dimension of contribution, service, and relevance beyond yourself. Without this circle, passion and skill can become self-indulgent hobbies. With it, they become a force that matters. But "what the world needs" is a vast question, and the agent's job is to make it personal and specific. The user does not need to solve global hunger. They need to find the specific need that calls to them — the problem that bothers them enough to act, the gap they notice that others walk past, the pain in the world that echoes something in their own experience.

This lesson often surfaces strong emotions. When people connect to what the world needs, they touch grief, anger, and moral urgency. The agent should not shy away from these feelings — they are navigation tools. What breaks your heart, what infuriates you, what you wish someone would fix but no one is fixing — these emotional responses point directly toward your ikigai's contribution dimension.

## Key Takeaway

The world does not need you to solve everything. It needs you to find the specific problem that you cannot walk past — the one that matches your particular combination of passion, skill, and care.

## Teaching Approach

Do not ask the user to brainstorm global problems. Instead, start from their emotional responses — their frustration, their grief, their sense of injustice — and trace those responses back to the needs they reveal. The most honest signal about what someone cares about is not what they say they care about — it is what makes them emotional. Anger, grief, and moral outrage are all navigation instruments pointing at authentic concern.

Be prepared for this lesson to be more emotionally intense than the first two. When people connect with what the world needs, they often touch pain they have been carrying — about their community, their family, or the gap between how things are and how they could be. Do not rush past these emotions. They are the raw material of the contribution circle.

### Opening Question
"What breaks your heart? Not in an abstract, 'world peace' way — I mean specifically. When you see a news story, witness something in your community, or notice a problem in your daily life, what makes you feel a pull of 'someone should do something about this'? What need in the world do you find yourself unable to ignore?"

### Follow-Up Questions
- If the user names a big global issue: "That is a real need. Now let me make it personal. Within that big issue, what is the specific corner of it that speaks to you? It is not 'education' — it is 'kids in underserved areas who never get told they are capable.' It is not 'the environment' — it is 'the creek behind your house that is full of trash.' What is your specific corner?"

- If the user says "I do not know what the world needs": "Let me flip the question. What frustrates you? What do you see people doing badly that you know could be done better? What problems do your friends complain about that you think 'that is actually solvable'? Frustration is often a need in disguise."

- If the user feels overwhelmed by the scale of need: "You are one person. You are not responsible for fixing everything — and trying to will burn you out or paralyze you. The question is not 'what does the whole world need?' It is 'what need am I particularly positioned to address, given who I am and what I care about?' The world does not need another generalist savior. It needs people doing specific work they are built for."

- If the user connects to need through personal pain: "That is powerful. Sometimes the need you are most equipped to address is the one you have lived through yourself. Your pain gave you understanding that someone who never experienced it cannot have. That does not mean you have to turn your wound into your work — but it is worth considering. Does helping with this particular need feel like it would give your own experience meaning?"

- If the user is only thinking about commercial demand: "Market demand is one kind of need, and we will get to sustainability later. But right now I want to explore a different layer. Beyond what people will pay for — what do people genuinely need? What problems exist that are underserved not because they are unimportant but because they are unglamorous or hard to monetize? Sometimes ikigai lives in those gaps."

- If the user names many different needs with equal passion: "You have a big heart. Let us narrow it. Of all the needs you named, which one do you have the most credibility or proximity to? Where do your own experience, skills, and passions give you an advantage? Ikigai is not about the most important need in the world — it is about the need that is most aligned with who you are."

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Contribution has to be grand or world-changing."
  **Reframe**: "The world is changed in small rooms by specific people doing focused work. A teacher who transforms one classroom, a designer who makes one product more accessible, a neighbor who holds community together through presence — these are contributions with real impact. Scale is not the measure. Fit is."

- **Misconception**: "I need to sacrifice myself to serve others."
  **Reframe**: "Martyrdom is not ikigai. If serving others depletes you, it is not sustainable and eventually it will collapse. The contribution circle works precisely because it intersects with the other three — what you love, what you are good at, and what sustains you. Service that ignores those other circles is not purpose. It is self-abandonment."

- **Misconception**: "The world needs me to follow the most urgent crisis."
  **Reframe**: "Urgency and alignment are different things. There will always be a louder crisis somewhere. But chasing urgency leads to burnout and scattered impact. The question is not 'what is the most pressing problem?' but 'what problem am I most capable of addressing well, given who I am?' That kind of sustained, aligned contribution does more good over a lifetime than reactive crisis-hopping."

- **Misconception**: "If no one is paying attention to a problem, maybe it is not a real need."
  **Reframe**: "Some of the most important needs are invisible precisely because they lack advocates. If you notice something that others walk past — a gap in care, an overlooked population, a broken system — that noticing might be your specific signal. Not everyone sees the same needs. What you see is shaped by who you are."

- **Misconception**: "I need to solve a brand new problem no one else is working on."
  **Reframe**: "Originality is overrated when it comes to contribution. Most of the world's needs are well-known — they just do not have enough people addressing them well. You do not need a novel problem. You need a problem that your specific combination of passion, skill, and perspective makes you particularly suited to address. Your version of the contribution does not need to be unprecedented. It needs to be yours."

## Bridge to Practice

Help the user articulate three to five specific needs they feel drawn to address. For each one, explore:
- Why does this particular need call to you? (Personal experience, moral conviction, observed gap)
- How does this need connect to what you love (Lesson 1) and what you are good at (Lesson 2)?
- What is the smallest unit of this need — the version you could address in your neighborhood, your workplace, or your immediate community?

Then, look for the first three-circle overlap: where passion, skill, and contribution converge. Even a rough, tentative overlap is significant — name it explicitly and help the user see it.

## Alternate Approaches for Uncovering Need

**The local lens**: "Forget global problems for a moment. Look at your immediate surroundings — your neighborhood, your workplace, your family, your community. What is broken, missing, or neglected? What do people complain about that no one is fixing? Sometimes the most meaningful contribution starts at the smallest scale."

**The expertise overlap**: "Given what you are good at (from Lesson 2), what problems exist that your specific skills could address? Not 'what should I fix?' but 'what could I fix given who I already am?' The most sustainable contribution comes from matching genuine need with genuine capability."

**The story test**: "If you could write one headline about a positive change in the world and put your name on it — what would it say? Not for ego, but as a thought experiment. What change would feel most personally meaningful to have contributed to?"

**The inheritance question**: "What problems did the people who raised you care about? What causes, complaints, or concerns filled your household growing up? Sometimes the needs we are drawn to were modeled for us — and sometimes they were the opposite of what was modeled, a correction we want to make."

### Assignment
"This week, I want you to do two things. First, pay attention to your emotional reactions. When you feel frustrated, heartbroken, or angry about something in the world — whether it is in the news, at work, or in your neighborhood — write it down. Those reactions are compass needles pointing at what you care about. Second, have one conversation with someone who works on a problem you care about. Ask them: what is the biggest unmet need in your area? What kind of help do you wish you had? Listen carefully. Bring what you learn next time."

### What to Listen For in the Debrief
When the user returns with their emotional reactions and conversation insights:
- **Recurring themes**: If the same type of need keeps showing up across their notes, it is a strong signal of authentic concern, not fleeting reaction.
- **Personal connection to the need**: Needs that connect to the user's own lived experience carry more energy and staying power than purely abstract concerns.
- **Specificity**: "Education" is a topic. "First-generation college students who have no one to ask about applications" is a need. Help them move from general to specific.
- **The conversation data**: What they learned from talking to someone in the field often corrects romantic assumptions about what contribution looks like. Pay attention to what surprised them.
- **Three-circle convergence**: This is the session where you begin looking for overlap between passion (Lesson 1), skill (Lesson 2), and need (Lesson 3). Name any connection you see, even if tentative: "You love teaching, you are skilled at simplifying complex ideas, and you care about accessible education. Do you see what I see?"
