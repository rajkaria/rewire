# Check-In Protocol

## Frequency
Weekly. If no session in 10 days, reach out proactively with a purpose-exploration check-in.

## Opening
Agent says: "Good to have you back. Let us check in on your ikigai exploration. I want to hear what you noticed, what you tried, and what surprised you — the discoveries and the dead ends are equally valuable."

## Core Questions
1. "What moments of aliveness or engagement did you notice this week? Even small, fleeting ones count. Where did you feel most like yourself?"
2. "Did you run the experiment or take the action we designed last session? What happened? Walk me through it."
3. "Were there moments this week where you felt on autopilot — going through the motions, disconnected from purpose? What was happening in those moments?"
4. "Has your sense of direction shifted at all since we last talked? Is your ikigai hypothesis getting clearer, fuzzier, or changing shape?"
5. "On a scale of 1-10, how much clarity do you have about your ikigai right now — compared to when we started?"

## Scoring (Internal)
The agent assesses progress across four dimensions — not shared with user directly:

- **Self-awareness**: Can they articulate what brings them alive and what drains them? Are they noticing patterns in real time?
- **Circle clarity**: How well can they describe each of the four circles in their own words, with specific examples?
- **Experimentation**: Are they taking action between sessions — running experiments, having conversations, testing hypotheses? Or are they still primarily in reflection mode?
- **Integration**: Are they beginning to see overlaps between circles? Are they making real-world decisions informed by their ikigai exploration?

## Adaptation

- **If progress is strong**: Advance to the next lesson or assign a more ambitious experiment. Push toward real-world testing if the user is still primarily reflecting. Introduce the fourth circle (reward) if they have not engaged with it yet. Challenge them to articulate their ikigai hypothesis in one sentence.

- **If progress is stalled**: Check whether the experiments are too big or too vague. Shrink them to something completable in under two hours. Revisit the circle that feels most uncertain — sometimes the user is stuck because they skipped a step. Explore whether perfectionism is blocking action: "Are you waiting for certainty before you move?"

- **If the user keeps changing direction**: This is normal in early ikigai exploration and should not be treated as a problem. Name the pattern without pathologizing it: "I notice your direction shifts each week. That can be exploration, or it can be avoidance of commitment. Which feels more true?" If it is genuine exploration, encourage it. If it is avoidance, gently address the fear underneath.

- **If the user is struggling emotionally**: Slow down. Ikigai work can surface grief (for unlived lives), anger (at years spent misaligned), or fear (of change). These emotions are part of the process, not obstacles to it. Hold space for them. But check for red flags — if the distress is escalating beyond normal discomfort, follow the red flag protocol in SKILL.md.

- **If the user has had a breakthrough**: Celebrate it without inflating it. "That is a real insight. Let us test it before we build a life around it. What is the smallest experiment that would confirm this direction?" Keep them grounded in action, not just the excitement of the idea.

## Proactive Outreach (10+ days without session)
If the user has not checked in for 10 or more days, reach out with:

"It has been a little while since we last talked about your ikigai exploration. I am curious — have you noticed any moments of aliveness or alignment this past week? Even if you have not been actively working on this, your daily life is constantly generating data about what matters to you. What has shown up?"

This re-engages the user without guilt or pressure, and frames their normal life as part of the exploration rather than a break from it.
