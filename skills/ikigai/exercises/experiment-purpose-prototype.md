# Exercise: Purpose Prototype

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (The Sweet Spot), when the user has an ikigai hypothesis and needs to move from reflection to real-world testing. This is the most action-oriented exercise in the skill — it takes the user from thinking about purpose to making contact with it.

## Setup
Agent says: "You have an ikigai hypothesis — a direction where your passion, skill, need, and reward might converge. Now we need to test it. Not with more thinking. With action. We are going to design a purpose prototype: a small, low-stakes, time-bound experiment that puts your hypothesis in contact with reality. Think of this like a scientist designing an experiment, not an entrepreneur launching a business. We want data, not commitment."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: State the hypothesis
Agent says: "First, say your ikigai hypothesis out loud. Make it specific. Not 'I want to help people' — more like 'I think my ikigai lives at the intersection of teaching, creative problem-solving, and helping young people who lack access to mentorship.' What is yours?"

{Wait for user response. If it is too vague, help them sharpen it by referencing specific elements from their four circles.}

### Step 2: Design the experiment
Agent says: "Now we design the test. A good purpose prototype has five elements. Let us build yours."

**Element 1 — The action:**
"What is one concrete thing you can do in the next one to two weeks that puts you in direct contact with this direction? Not reading about it, not planning — actually doing a version of it. It should be small enough that it does not require permission, money, or a major schedule change. What comes to mind?"

{Wait for user response}

If the user is stuck, offer these categories of prototypes:
- **Conversation prototype**: Talk to someone who is already doing this work. Ask them what it is actually like, not what it looks like from the outside.
- **Immersion prototype**: Spend a few hours doing the work in a low-stakes way. Volunteer, shadow someone, or try a small version on your own.
- **Creation prototype**: Make something that represents this direction. Write the first article. Design the first workshop. Build the first prototype. Teach one person.
- **Contribution prototype**: Find one person or group with the need you identified and do something useful for them, even informally. See how it feels to serve that need with your particular skills.

**Element 2 — The timeline:**
"When exactly will you do this? Pick a specific day and time. Vague intentions do not get tested."

{Wait for user response}

**Element 3 — What you are testing:**
"What specific question does this experiment answer? It might be: 'Do I actually enjoy this work when I am doing it, not just thinking about it?' Or: 'Is there real demand for this?' Or: 'Can I do this well enough to be useful?' What is your experiment testing?"

{Wait for user response}

**Element 4 — Success signal:**
"How will you know the experiment went well? What would you feel, observe, or hear that would tell you to keep going in this direction? Be specific."

{Wait for user response}

**Element 5 — Pivot signal:**
"And equally important — what would tell you to adjust or change direction? What would you feel, observe, or hear that would mean this particular hypothesis is not quite right? Knowing your exit criteria in advance keeps you honest."

{Wait for user response}

### Step 3: Anticipate the resistance
Agent says: "Here is what usually happens. You will design a great experiment, and then between now and the time to run it, your mind will offer you reasons not to. 'I am not ready.' 'I need more information first.' 'Next week would be better.' What is the most likely thought or feeling that will try to stop you from running this experiment?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Good. Name it now so it does not ambush you later. When that thought shows up, your plan is simple: notice it, acknowledge it, and do the experiment anyway. You do not have to feel ready. You just have to be willing."

### Step 4: Document the design
Agent says: "Let us capture this so it is concrete. Your purpose prototype is:
- **Hypothesis**: [their ikigai hypothesis]
- **Experiment**: [their specific action]
- **When**: [specific day and time]
- **Testing**: [the question they are answering]
- **Success looks like**: [their success signal]
- **Pivot looks like**: [their pivot signal]
- **Likely resistance**: [the thought or feeling that will try to stop them]

Does this feel right? Anything to adjust?"

{Wait for confirmation or adjustment}

## Branching Responses
- If the user designs an experiment that is too big: "I love the ambition, but I want to protect this experiment from failing due to scope. What is the smallest version of this that would still give you useful data? If you can shrink it to something you could do in two hours, you are much more likely to actually do it — and the data will be just as valuable."

- If the user designs an experiment that is too safe: "This is comfortable, which means it probably will not teach you much. A good experiment has a little bit of risk — not financial risk, but emotional risk. The risk of being seen, of being a beginner, of finding out your hypothesis is wrong. Can we push this one step further outside your comfort zone?"

- If the user is excited and ready: "That energy is data. Pay attention to it during the experiment — does it sustain, or does it fade once you are in the middle of the work? Both are useful information."

- If the user is anxious about the experiment: "Anxiety about testing a purpose direction is normal and actually a good sign. It means this matters to you. The anxiety is not a stop sign — it is a signal that you are approaching something real. Can you carry the anxiety with you into the experiment rather than letting it stop you?"

- If the user has already done something relevant without realizing it: "You have already started prototyping without calling it that. Let us build on what you have already learned. What did that experience tell you? What question is still unanswered? Design the next experiment to answer that question."

## Debrief Questions
After the experiment is run, use these to extract maximum learning:
1. "What happened? Walk me through the experiment. What did you actually do?"
2. "How did you feel during it — not what you think you should have felt, but what actually showed up? Alive? Drained? Curious? Anxious? Bored?"
3. "Did the world respond? Was there interest, need, receptivity? Or indifference?"
4. "Did your ikigai hypothesis get stronger or weaker based on this experiment? What would you adjust?"
5. "What do you want to test next?"

## Real-World Action
"Run the experiment. Do not revise it, do not optimize it, do not postpone it. The prototype does not need to be good — it needs to happen. Contact with reality will teach you more in two hours than two more weeks of reflection. Go."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I want to hear three things: what you did, what you felt, and what you learned. From there, we will decide whether to double down on this direction, adjust the hypothesis, or design a new experiment entirely. This is an iterative process — every experiment gets you closer, even the ones that do not go as planned."
