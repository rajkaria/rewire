# Exercise: Life Audit

## Type
assessment

## When to Use
Anytime after Lesson 1. Best used early in the ikigai process to establish a baseline, or later as a progress check. This exercise takes an honest inventory of how the user's current life aligns with each of the four ikigai circles. It is diagnostic, not prescriptive — the goal is to see clearly, not to fix immediately.

## Setup
Agent says: "Before we design your future, let us get honest about your present. We are going to audit your current life against the four circles of ikigai. This is not about judging yourself — it is about seeing clearly where you already have alignment and where the biggest gaps are. Think of it as taking a snapshot so you know your starting point."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Map your current time
Agent says: "Think about a typical week. Roughly how do you spend your waking hours? Do not worry about being exact. Just give me the broad categories: work, commuting, household tasks, family time, hobbies, socializing, consuming media, exercise, and whatever else shows up. Estimate the hours for each."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Good. Now let us look at each category through the four-circle lens."

### Step 2: Audit each circle

**Circle 1 — Love / Passion alignment:**
Agent says: "Looking at your typical week, how many of those hours involve activities you genuinely love — where you feel engaged, curious, or absorbed? Not just tolerable, but actively energizing. Give me an honest percentage."

{Wait for user response}

Follow up: "Which specific activities in your week fall into that category? And which ones feel like obligation, routine, or numbness?"

{Wait for user response}

**Circle 2 — Skill / Strength alignment:**
Agent says: "How many hours in your week are you using your genuine strengths — the skills that both come naturally and energize you? Not your draining competencies, just the ones where you feel effective and alive at the same time."

{Wait for user response}

Follow up: "Where are you overusing draining competencies — doing work you are good at but that leaves you depleted? Name the specific activities."

{Wait for user response}

**Circle 3 — Need / Contribution alignment:**
Agent says: "How much of your week involves contributing to something you believe matters — addressing a need in the world that you actually care about? This could be through work, volunteering, parenting, community involvement, or any other form of service."

{Wait for user response}

Follow up: "Is the contribution in your life right now connected to needs that genuinely matter to you? Or are you serving out of obligation to needs you were assigned rather than chose?"

{Wait for user response}

**Circle 4 — Reward / Sustainability alignment:**
Agent says: "Are you being adequately rewarded for the work you do — not just financially, but in terms of recognition, growth, autonomy, and satisfaction? And is the current reward structure sustainable, or are you running on fumes?"

{Wait for user response}

Follow up: "If you look at the things you love, are good at, and want to contribute — are any of those currently generating reward? Or is reward coming primarily from activities that do not align with your other three circles?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 3: Score the alignment
Agent says: "Let us put a number on it. For each circle, rate your current life alignment on a scale of 1 to 10. One means completely misaligned — your daily life has almost nothing to do with this circle. Ten means deeply aligned — you are living this circle consistently."

- Love / Passion: ___
- Skill / Strength: ___
- Need / Contribution: ___
- Reward / Sustainability: ___

{Wait for user to score all four}

Agent says: "Now look at the scores together. What pattern do you see?"

### Step 4: Identify the leverage point
Agent says: "Here is the key question: which circle, if you increased its score by just two points, would have the biggest positive impact on your overall sense of purpose and satisfaction? Not the lowest score necessarily — the one with the most leverage. Where would a small shift create the most meaning?"

{Wait for user response}

Follow up: "What would raising that score by two points actually look like in your weekly routine? What would change? What would you start, stop, or adjust?"

{Wait for user response}

## Branching Responses
- If scores are generally low (most below 4): "These scores tell an important story: your current life has significant misalignment with what matters to you. That is uncomfortable to see, but it is also clarifying. You are not broken — you are living a life that was designed by default rather than by intention. The good news is that small, deliberate changes compound. We do not need to overhaul everything. We need to find the first lever."

- If scores are mixed (one or two high, others low): "This is a very common pattern. You have real alignment in some areas and significant gaps in others. The high scores tell you something is working — protect those. The low scores point to where the work is. Let us focus on raising the lowest score that has the most leverage."

- If scores are generally high (most above 7): "Let me check — are these scores honest, or aspirational? I ask because if your alignment is genuinely this strong, you may not need purpose-finding work. You may need optimization work — fine-tuning what is already good. Or there might be a specific area of dissatisfaction that the averages are masking. What drove you to explore ikigai in the first place?"

- If the user becomes discouraged by low scores: "I want to reframe what just happened. You did something most people avoid their entire lives — you looked honestly at the gap between what matters and how you are living. That takes courage. And the fact that you can feel the gap means your compass is working. You know what alignment feels like, which is exactly what you need to build toward it."

- If the user realizes their reward comes from misaligned activities: "This is one of the most common traps: being well-compensated for work that does not align with your passion, skill, or contribution values. It creates golden handcuffs. The question is not 'how do I quit tomorrow?' It is 'how do I gradually build reward around more aligned activities while maintaining stability?'"

## Debrief Questions
1. "What was the most honest thing you admitted to yourself during this audit?"
2. "Were any of your scores surprising — higher or lower than you expected?"
3. "If you zoom out from the individual scores, what is the overall story of your life right now? Is it a story of alignment, misalignment, or transition?"
4. "What is one specific change you could make in the next week that would move one score up by even half a point?"

## Real-World Action
"Pick the circle with the most leverage — the one where a small change would create the most meaning. Design one concrete action this week that moves that score up. Not a big life change — a specific, doable action. Maybe it is spending two hours on something you love. Maybe it is having a conversation about a need you care about. Maybe it is using an energizing strength you have been neglecting. Pick the action and put it on your calendar with a specific time."

## Report Back
"Next time, I will ask: did you take the action? What happened to your sense of alignment when you did? And do you want to audit again in a few weeks to see if the numbers are shifting? This audit is a tool you can return to anytime you feel drift."
