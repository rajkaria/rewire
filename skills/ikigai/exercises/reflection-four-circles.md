# Exercise: Four Circles Mapping

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 3 (What the World Needs), once the user has explored passion, skill, and contribution. This exercise synthesizes all three dimensions and begins introducing the fourth (reward) to create a complete ikigai map. Can also be revisited at any point to update the map as understanding deepens.

## Setup
Agent says: "You have been exploring three big questions: what you love, what you are good at, and what the world needs. Now we are going to lay all of that out side by side and look for where the circles overlap. This is not about finding the perfect answer — it is about seeing the landscape clearly so you can spot the most promising direction to test."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Populate each circle
Agent says: "Let us fill in your four circles. I am going to ask you to give me your top entries for each one. Do not overthink it — go with what feels most true right now. We can always revise."

**Circle 1 — What You Love:**
"From our first conversation, what are the three to five things that genuinely bring you alive? The activities, topics, or experiences where you feel most engaged and energized. List them."

{Wait for user response}

**Circle 2 — What You Are Good At:**
"Now your energizing strengths — the skills you have that also give you energy when you use them. Not your draining competencies, just the ones that light you up. Give me three to five."

{Wait for user response}

**Circle 3 — What the World Needs:**
"The needs that call to you. The specific problems, gaps, or unserved populations that you feel pulled toward. Three to five."

{Wait for user response}

**Circle 4 — What You Can Be Rewarded For:**
"This one might be newer territory. What are you currently rewarded for — financially or otherwise? And looking at your first three circles, what could you plausibly be rewarded for? Think broadly: salary, freelance income, recognition, community standing, opportunities, grants, partnerships. Three to five."

{Wait for user response}

### Step 2: Find the overlaps
Agent says: "Now the interesting part. Look across your four lists. Where do you see the same theme, activity, or direction showing up in more than one circle? I am going to walk through the key intersections."

Guide the user through each intersection:

- **Passion + Skill** (what you love AND are good at): "Where does something you love meet something you are skilled at? This is your zone of satisfaction — work that is fulfilling but might lack external relevance."
- **Skill + Need** (what you are good at AND the world needs): "Where does a strength of yours meet a real need? This is your zone of contribution — useful work, but it might not light you up."
- **Need + Reward** (what the world needs AND what pays): "Where is there a need you care about that also has a viable reward structure? This is your zone of sustainability."
- **Passion + Need** (what you love AND the world needs): "Where does what excites you also address something the world genuinely lacks? This is your zone of mission."

For each intersection, ask: "What shows up here? Even a rough connection counts."

{Wait for responses at each intersection}

### Step 3: Identify the center
Agent says: "Now look at the center — where all four circles overlap, or at least where three of them converge. Is there a direction, a role, a kind of work, or a way of living that touches all of these? It does not have to be a job title. It could be a description like 'helping young people develop creative confidence through hands-on workshops' or 'building tools that make complex information accessible to non-experts.' Try to say it in one or two sentences."

{Wait for user response. If they struggle, help them construct it from the overlaps they identified.}

### Step 4: Name the gaps
Agent says: "It is equally important to see where the circles do not overlap. Looking at your map, which circle is weakest? Where is the biggest gap? Maybe your passion and skill align beautifully but you have not found the need or the reward yet. Maybe you see the need clearly but are not sure how your skills connect. Name the gap."

{Wait for user response}

## Branching Responses
- If the user sees a clear four-circle overlap: "That is a strong ikigai hypothesis. Hold it lightly — we still need to test it in the real world. But the fact that it shows up across all four dimensions means it is worth a serious experiment. How does it feel to say it out loud?"

- If the user sees overlap in two or three circles but not all four: "That is exactly where most people start. A three-circle overlap is a direction, not a dead end. The missing circle is your next frontier. If reward is missing, we design a sustainability experiment. If need is missing, we explore who this serves. If skill is missing, we look at what you need to learn. Which circle needs the most attention?"

- If the user feels no overlap at all: "Let us look at this differently. Sometimes the overlap is not in a single activity but in a quality — a way of being that runs through different areas. Maybe the thread is 'bringing clarity to confusion' or 'making people feel safe' or 'turning ideas into tangible things.' Is there a quality like that running through your lists, even if the specific activities differ?"

- If the user is overwhelmed: "This is a lot to hold at once. Let us simplify. Forget the complete map for a moment. Just tell me: what is the one direction that feels most alive to you right now? The one that, when you imagine pursuing it, makes you lean forward even a little? Let us start there and fill in the rest over time."

## Debrief Questions
1. "What surprised you when you laid all four circles side by side? Any unexpected connections?"
2. "Which circle feels strongest for you right now? Which feels weakest or most uncertain?"
3. "If you had to describe your ikigai direction in one sentence — even a rough, imperfect one — what would you say?"
4. "What would need to be true for you to move further in this direction? What is the first obstacle?"

## Real-World Action
"Take your ikigai hypothesis — even if it is rough — and write it down somewhere you will see it this week. Then do one thing that moves you closer to testing it. Not planning, not researching — doing. Talk to someone in that space. Try the work for an hour. Make something. Volunteer. Contact with reality is the only way to refine the map."

## Report Back
"Next time, I will ask: did you test your hypothesis? What did contact with reality reveal that reflection alone could not? And has your map shifted at all based on what you learned?"
