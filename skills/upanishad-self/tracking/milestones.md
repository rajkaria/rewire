# Milestones

## Stage 1: Questioning
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: User begins to genuinely question their habitual identifications. They move from assuming they know who they are to recognizing that the question deserves investigation. The neti neti method has unsettled their certainty without yet providing a new ground to stand on.
**The user can**: Articulate the difference between the contents of experience (body, thoughts, emotions, roles) and the awareness in which those contents appear. Describe at least one specific moment where they caught themselves identified with something impermanent and recognized -- even briefly -- that they are not that thing. Apply the neti neti inquiry to at least one layer of identity and report what happened when they did -- not what they thought about it, but what they experienced. Tolerate the discomfort of not knowing who they are without rushing to a comfortable answer. Describe the fundamental question of the Upanishads in their own words, grounded in their own experience rather than reciting the teaching.

## Stage 2: Discrimination
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User develops the capacity for viveka -- the ability to discriminate between the Self and what the Self is not, in real time. They can apply the chariot metaphor to their daily experience and name which faculty is in charge at any given moment. The distinction between awareness and the contents of awareness is becoming practical rather than theoretical.
**The user can**: Map the chariot metaphor onto their own life with specificity -- naming their dominant horses (senses and desires), assessing the state of their charioteer (buddhi/intellect), and recognizing moments when the rider (Atman) is remembered versus forgotten. Catch identification as it happens -- the moment of absorbing into a role, a reaction, a story -- and step back to the witness, at least some of the time. Describe the difference between the charioteer being awake and the charioteer being asleep, based on their own direct observation. Begin to identify the ways maya operates in their life -- the specific misperceptions, projections, and habitual stories that overlay reality. Maintain a daily witness practice for at least several consecutive days and report what the practice reveals about the patterns of their mind.

## Stage 3: Witnessing
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User has developed a stable witness capacity. They can access the observer position -- the awareness that watches without being absorbed -- with increasing ease and frequency. Viveka and vairagya are beginning to operate naturally, not just as exercises. Maya patterns are recognized in real time rather than only in retrospect.
**The user can**: Access the witness position in daily life -- not just during formal practice -- and describe what it is like to observe experience without being fully identified with it. Name their primary maya patterns with precision -- the specific stories, projections, and misidentifications that habitually distort their perception -- and catch them in operation. Describe experiences of vairagya -- moments where clinging or identification loosened naturally, not through force but through clear seeing. Maintain a consistent daily practice of self-inquiry and report how the quality of inquiry has deepened over time. Distinguish between genuine witness consciousness and the ego's simulation of it -- between actually resting as awareness and the mind's commentary about resting as awareness. Apply the inquiry to at least one relationship or challenging situation and describe how it changed their response.

## Stage 4: Recognition / Graduation
**Marker**: User has a living, embodied relationship with the inquiry. The question "who am I?" is no longer an exercise but a quality of attention they carry into daily life. They hold their identities lightly -- not abandoning them, but wearing them with awareness. They have touched, at least partially, the recognition that the Self is not bounded by the personal -- that awareness is not a private possession but the ground of all experience.
**The user can**: Turn attention toward the subject of experience -- the one who is aware -- and rest there, even briefly, without requiring any special state or condition. Hold their roles, personality, beliefs, and emotions as things they carry rather than things they are -- wearing them like clothing, participating in them fully, while knowing they are not defined by them. Describe what they have discovered through inquiry in their own words, grounded in their own experience, without relying on Sanskrit terminology or traditional formulations. The teaching has become personal. Point to specific changes in how they relate to other people, to difficulty, and to their own inner life as a result of the inquiry -- not as self-improvement but as a natural consequence of seeing more clearly. Recognize when the ego co-opts the inquiry -- turning it into spiritual pride, making it a new identity, using non-attachment as emotional avoidance -- and gently return to genuine looking. Hold the teaching with both reverence and lightness. They respect the depth of the Upanishadic tradition while knowing that the tradition itself points beyond all traditions, all teachings, all words -- to what is simply here, always, before any tradition names it.
**Next step**: Suggest `yoga-sutras` for systematic mind-training that deepens the concentration and discipline underlying self-inquiry. Suggest `karma-yoga` if the user feels called to express self-knowledge through selfless action in the world. Suggest `mindful-path` if they want practical attention-training techniques to complement the philosophical inquiry. Suggest `epictetus-manual` if they are drawn to the Stoic tradition's parallel investigation of what is within our control and what is not. Or suggest that the user seek a living teacher in the Vedantic tradition if the inquiry has deepened to the point where a human relationship with the teaching would serve better than a structured coaching format.
