# Exercise: Two-Minute Design

**Type:** experiment

**Used after:** Lesson 5

## Purpose

This exercise takes a habit the user wants to build and scales it down to a version that takes two minutes or less. The purpose is to eliminate the startup friction that kills most habits. Most people fail not because the habit is hard but because the habit FEELS hard before they start. A two-minute version removes the psychological barrier to entry. By the end, the user will have a radically simplified version of their target habit, an environment designed to reduce friction to near zero, a commitment to the two-minute version for seven consecutive days, and the understanding that the goal is not the two minutes themselves -- it is becoming the kind of person who shows up consistently.

## Setup

Open by addressing the most common objection upfront:

"I'm going to suggest something today that your brain is going to reject. It's going to feel too small, too easy, almost insulting. I want you to notice that reaction when it happens, because that reaction is actually the thing that's been getting in the way. Here's what we're going to do: take the habit you want to build and shrink it down to something you can do in two minutes or less. And then you're going to commit to ONLY that version for one week. Ready?"

If the user laughs or says "that's silly": "Good -- hold onto that reaction. We're going to work with it."

## Agent Script

### Step 1: Name the Target Habit

Get the full-sized version on the table.

"What's the habit you want to build? Give me the ideal version -- the one you imagine when you think about doing it consistently."

Let them describe it. Common examples:
- "I want to run for 30 minutes every morning"
- "I want to meditate for 20 minutes"
- "I want to read for an hour before bed"
- "I want to journal every day"
- "I want to practice guitar for 45 minutes"
- "I want to do a full workout at the gym"

Acknowledge it: "That's a solid habit. And I bet you've tried to do that version before. How has it gone?"

Most users will admit to starting strong and dropping off. Don't dwell on past failure, but use it: "Right. So the full version works great on day one and day three and maybe day seven. But somewhere around day twelve, life gets in the way and you skip once, then twice, then it's gone. The problem isn't the habit. The problem is that the full version requires enough motivation to overcome the startup resistance. And motivation is unreliable. So let's design a version that doesn't need motivation at all."

### Step 2: Shrink to Two Minutes

This is the core of the exercise. Scale the habit down aggressively.

"Here's the challenge. I want you to take [their habit] and shrink it down to something that takes two minutes or less. Not a simplified version. Not a shorter version. The smallest possible version that still counts as showing up."

Let them try first. They will almost always make it too big.

Common first attempts and how to shrink them further:
- "I'll run for 10 minutes" --> "Smaller. What's the two-minute version?"
- "I'll run for 5 minutes" --> "Still too long. What if you just put on your running shoes and stepped outside? That's it. Shoes on, door open, stand outside for 30 seconds."
- "I'll meditate for 5 minutes" --> "Two minutes. Actually -- what if you just sat on your meditation cushion and took three breaths? That's it. Sit down, three breaths, you're done."
- "I'll read for 15 minutes" --> "Two minutes. What if you just opened the book and read one page? Or even one paragraph?"
- "I'll write in my journal" --> "What if you opened the journal and wrote one sentence? Just one."
- "I'll go to the gym" --> "What if you just drove to the gym parking lot? Or just put on your workout clothes?"

The two-minute version should feel almost absurdly easy. That's the point.

"Your two-minute version of [habit] is: [two-minute version]. How does that feel?"

### Step 3: Address the Resistance

This is where the user pushes back. Expect it. Welcome it.

The user will almost certainly say something like: "That's not going to do anything." Or: "What's the point of just one page?" Or: "That's too easy to matter."

This is the most important moment in the exercise. Do not skip it or rush through it.

"There it is. That's the reaction I told you about at the beginning. Your brain is telling you this is too small to matter. Let me ask you something: how many times in the past year have you done ZERO minutes of [habit]? Not two minutes. ZERO."

Pause. Let that land.

"The gap between zero and two minutes is massive. Not because of what two minutes accomplishes physically -- you're right, one page doesn't make you a reader. But here's what it does accomplish: it makes you someone who shows up. Every day that you open the book, you're casting a vote for the identity of a reader. Every day you sit on the cushion, you're casting a vote for the identity of someone who meditates. You can't improve a habit that doesn't exist. First you have to establish it. Two minutes establishes it."

If they're still resistant: "Let me put it another way. What's harder: reading one page, or picking the book back up after three weeks of not reading? The two-minute version is insurance against ever having to restart from zero."

If they're VERY resistant: "I'll make you a deal. Try the two-minute version for one week. If after seven days you feel like it was pointless, we'll scrap it and try something else. But give it seven days of showing up before you judge it. Fair?"

### Step 4: Design the Environment

Remove every possible friction point between the user and their two-minute action.

"Now let's make sure nothing gets between you and these two minutes. Let's audit the friction."

Walk through the chain of micro-actions that leads to the habit:

"Starting from wherever you are right before the habit time, what are ALL the tiny steps between you and actually doing [two-minute version]? List every single one, even the ones that seem trivial."

For example, for "read one page before bed":
1. Finish whatever I'm doing
2. Go to the bedroom
3. Find my book (where is it?)
4. Get in bed
5. Open the book
6. Read

"Now let's look at each step and ask: can we eliminate it or make it easier?"

Help them reduce friction:
- "Keep the book on your pillow so you literally can't get in bed without moving it"
- "Put the bookmark at the page so you don't have to find your place"
- "Keep your running shoes right by the door, laces loosened, ready to slip on"
- "Set your meditation cushion in the middle of the room so you trip over it"
- "Lay out your journal open to the next blank page on your desk"

"The environment should make the two-minute version the default. You shouldn't have to think, decide, or prepare. You should just... arrive at the behavior."

### Step 5: Identify and Remove Friction Points

Go deeper on what specifically derails them.

"Now the harder question. In the past, when you've tried to do [habit] and didn't, what actually happened? Not 'I wasn't motivated.' What literally happened? Walk me through the moment you decided not to do it."

Listen carefully. The friction is usually in the details:
- "I couldn't find my journal" --> storage/visibility problem
- "I was already on the couch and didn't want to get up" --> location problem
- "My phone was right there and I started scrolling" --> competing cue problem
- "It was late and I was tired" --> timing problem
- "I didn't know what to write about" --> decision fatigue problem

For each friction point, design a specific countermeasure:
- Can't find it? --> Permanent visible location
- On the couch? --> Change the trigger location or time
- Phone competing? --> Phone in another room / Do Not Disturb / app blocker
- Too tired? --> Move the habit earlier in the day
- Don't know what to write? --> Use a prompt ("One thing I noticed today...")

"Every friction point we remove makes it more likely that the two minutes will happen. We're not relying on willpower. We're engineering the path."

### Step 6: Make the Commitment

Lock in the seven-day experiment.

"Here's your experiment for the next seven days:

**The habit:** [Two-minute version, stated precisely]
**The time:** [Specific time or 'after' trigger]
**The place:** [Specific location]
**The environment setup:** [What they'll change tonight]

Seven days. Two minutes. No more -- and this is important. For this week, I don't WANT you to do more than two minutes. Even if you're feeling great on day three and want to do thirty minutes -- don't. Stick to two. I know that sounds backwards. Here's why: we're training the habit of showing up, not the habit of performing. If you let yourself do thirty minutes on the good days, you'll start thinking 'I should do thirty minutes' on the bad days, and that thought will stop you from starting at all. Two minutes. Every day. That's the commitment."

If they struggle with the cap: "Think of it as a restriction, like a training protocol. Athletes don't go max effort every day -- they follow a program. This week's program is: build the showing-up muscle. Next week, we can talk about duration."

"Can you commit to this? Seven days, two minutes, starting tomorrow?"

Get an explicit yes.

### If the User...

- **Is enthusiastic:** Enthusiastic users are the highest risk for this exercise because they'll want to do MORE than two minutes immediately. "I can hear how ready you are. That energy is exactly what makes the two-minute rule so powerful for you -- because when you show up for two minutes, your enthusiasm will often carry you further. But the COMMITMENT is two minutes. The extra is a bonus, never an obligation. The moment the extra becomes expected, you've lost the magic. Can you commit to two minutes being the only requirement?" If they agree but you sense they'll override: "Here's a test. On day four, if you're feeling great, stop at two minutes anyway. Just once. Notice how that feels. It'll teach you something important about the difference between showing up and performing."

- **Is resistant:** This exercise generates the most resistance of any in the program. Lean into it. "I understand. You want to do the real thing, not some watered-down version. Let me ask you this: have you been doing the real thing? Consistently? For more than two weeks?" If no: "Then the real thing isn't working. Not because it's wrong -- because the barrier to start is too high. The two-minute version isn't the goal. It's the doorway. Once you walk through the door consistently, you can do whatever you want on the other side." If they genuinely cannot accept the two-minute version: "Okay. What's the smallest version you WOULD commit to doing every single day for a week, including your worst day? The day you're sick, exhausted, overwhelmed?" Whatever they land on, accept it -- but push for it to be genuinely small.

- **Is confused:** "Let me make this really concrete. Tomorrow morning, you're going to [specific two-minute action]. That's it. You don't need to understand why it works yet. You just need to do it seven times and see what happens. Think of it as an experiment. A scientist doesn't need to believe the hypothesis to run the test."

- **Gets emotional:** If the two-minute version triggers feelings of inadequacy ("I can't believe I have to start this small"): "I want to reframe something. Starting small isn't a sign of weakness. It's a sign of wisdom. You've tried the big version. It didn't stick. Repeating the same approach and expecting different results -- that's the problem. Choosing to start small, when your ego is screaming to go big, takes more self-awareness than most people have. This isn't starting over. This is starting smart." If they express frustration about past failures: "Every attempt you've made before gave you data. You know what doesn't work. Now we're using that data. The two-minute version isn't retreat. It's strategy."

## Debrief

1. "What was your gut reaction when we first landed on the two-minute version? Did it feel ridiculous? Freeing? Frustrating?"

2. "Do you believe it will work? Be honest -- even a 'no' is useful here."

3. "What's the most likely day you'll want to skip? What's your plan for that day?"

4. "If the two-minute version becomes automatic after this week, what would you want to add? Just hold that thought -- we're not adding it yet."

## Real-World Assignment

"Your experiment starts tonight. Here's the sequence:

**Tonight:**
1. Set up your environment. [Specific changes -- placement of objects, removal of distractions.]
2. Do a practice run. Actually do the two-minute version right now if you can, or tonight before bed. Not because it counts toward your seven days, but because it removes the 'first time' friction from tomorrow.

**Days 1-7:**
Do [two-minute version] every day. Same time, same place. The ONLY rule is: show up. You can feel terrible, you can do it badly, you can be half-asleep. As long as you physically do the thing for two minutes, the day counts.

**After each day:**
Put a check mark somewhere visible. A calendar on the fridge, a note in your phone, a tally on a sticky note. Anything. The visual record matters.

**If you miss a day:**
Do not try to make it up. Do not do four minutes the next day. Just show up the next day as if nothing happened. One miss is data. Two misses in a row is what we want to prevent.

When we talk next, I want to hear three things: how many days you showed up, what it felt like, and whether anything surprised you about the experience. That's the whole experiment. Two minutes. Seven days. Go."
