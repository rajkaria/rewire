# Exercise: Identity Mapping

**Type:** reflection

**Used after:** Lesson 1

## Purpose

This exercise helps the user shift from outcome-based thinking ("I want to lose 20 pounds") to identity-based thinking ("I want to become a person who moves their body daily"). By the end, the user will have a clear identity statement for 1-3 areas of life and one micro-action that serves as a "vote" for that new identity. The shift from "what do I want to achieve?" to "who do I want to become?" is the foundation everything else builds on.

## Setup

Introduce the exercise with warmth and low pressure:

"I want to walk you through something that might change how you think about the changes you're trying to make. It takes about 10 minutes, and there's nothing to get wrong here. We're just going to explore what's underneath your goals. Sound good?"

If the user seems impatient or wants to skip ahead, say: "I get it -- you want to get to the action. This IS the action. The clarity we get here shapes everything that comes after. Give me 10 minutes."

## Agent Script

### Step 1: Surface the Desired Change

Ask the user to name a change they want to make.

"Let's start simple. What's one change you've been wanting to make in your life? Don't overthink it -- just the first thing that comes to mind."

**What to listen for:** Most users will answer with an outcome: "I want to read more," "I want to get in shape," "I want to be less stressed," "I want to save money." This is expected. Do NOT correct them yet. Just acknowledge it.

"Got it -- [repeat what they said back to them]. That's a clear goal. Let me ask you something about it."

### Step 2: Expose the Outcome Frame

Gently surface the fact that they framed their answer as an outcome.

"Notice how you phrased that -- it's about a result. Something you want to HAVE or ACHIEVE. That's how most of us think about change. But here's what I want to explore with you: what kind of person naturally gets that result? Not what do you want to accomplish -- who do you want to BECOME?"

Pause. Let them think.

"If someone were already living the way you described -- what would you call them? Not their job title. Their character. What kind of person are they?"

**What to listen for:** This is where the reframe happens. Listen for identity language: "a healthy person," "a reader," "someone who's disciplined," "a calm person." If they stay stuck in outcomes ("someone who has read 50 books"), guide them: "That's still an outcome. Go deeper -- what kind of person reads 50 books in a year? What's true about them as a person?"

### Step 3: Explore Three Life Areas

Expand beyond the first answer to map identity across their life.

"That's great. Now I want to zoom out. Let's look at three areas of your life where you want growth. They can be anything -- health, relationships, career, creativity, finances, peace of mind, whatever matters to you. What are three areas?"

Wait for them to name three areas. Write them down (repeat them back).

For EACH area, ask: "In [area], what kind of person do you want to be? Not what do you want to achieve there -- who do you want to become? Describe them."

**Guide each response toward identity language:**
- If they say "I want to make more money" --> "What kind of person earns well? What's true about their character?"
- If they say "I want a better relationship" --> "What kind of partner do you want to be? How would they show up?"
- If they say "I want to be healthier" --> "Describe the healthiest version of you. Not their body -- their habits, their relationship with their body."

Reflect back each identity statement: "So in [area], you want to become someone who [identity description]. Does that feel right?"

### Step 4: Synthesize the Identity Statement

Pull the three areas together into one cohesive identity.

"Now let's do something interesting. Look at these three identities together:
1. [Area 1]: [Identity]
2. [Area 2]: [Identity]
3. [Area 3]: [Identity]

Is there a common thread? A word or phrase that captures the person you're becoming across all of these?"

Help them find it. Common threads might be: discipline, presence, self-respect, intentionality, courage, consistency, care.

"Let me try putting it together: 'I am becoming the kind of person who [thread].' Does that resonate? Let's refine it until it feels like something you'd actually say to yourself."

Iterate with the user until the statement feels authentic. It should be:
- Short enough to remember
- Specific enough to mean something
- Aspirational but believable

### Step 5: Design One Identity Vote

Connect the identity statement to a single micro-action.

"Here's where this gets practical. Every action you take is a vote for the person you're becoming. You don't need a landslide -- you just need a majority. So let's design ONE tiny action that casts a vote for this identity. Something you could do tomorrow. Something so small it almost feels too easy."

"What's one thing the person you described would do today -- something that takes less than five minutes?"

**What to listen for:** If they go too big ("I'd work out for an hour"), scale it down: "That's the full version. What's the five-minute vote? The one that says 'I'm the kind of person who moves their body' even on a terrible day?"

If they go too vague ("I'd be more mindful"), make it concrete: "What does that look like in action? Give me the physical behavior. What would I see you doing?"

Land on something specific: "Put on running shoes and step outside for two minutes." "Open my journal and write one sentence." "Put my phone in the other room during dinner."

"So your identity vote is: [specific action]. Every time you do that, you're casting a vote for [identity statement]. Not because it transforms your life in one day, but because it starts to change what you believe about yourself."

### If the User...

- **Is enthusiastic:** Build on their energy. "I can feel how much this resonates. Let's make it even sharper -- what if we designed identity votes for all three areas? Just one tiny action each." Let them run with it. Enthusiastic users often want to do too much -- gently remind them that one consistent vote beats ten sporadic ones.

- **Is resistant:** Meet them without pushing. Common resistance: "This feels like fluffy affirmation stuff." Response: "I hear you. This isn't about positive thinking. It's about strategy. When you know who you're becoming, every decision gets simpler -- you just ask 'what would that person do?' It's a filter, not a fantasy." If they remain resistant, ask: "What feels off about this approach? Let's talk about that."

- **Is confused:** Simplify and redirect. "Let me make this simpler. Forget goals for a second. If I asked your best friend to describe the version of you that you're most proud of -- not the richest or most successful version, but the one you'd respect the most -- what would they say?" This reframes identity through a familiar lens. Then build from there.

- **Gets emotional:** Hold space. Identity work can surface grief about the gap between who someone is and who they want to be. If this happens: "I notice this is hitting something real. That's not a problem -- it means we're in the right place. The gap between where you are and who you want to become? That's not failure. That's awareness. And awareness is where change starts." Do not rush past the emotion. Let them process. Only move forward when they signal readiness.

## Debrief

After completing the exercise, ask these reflection questions:

1. "What surprised you about how you were originally framing your goals versus the identity we landed on?"

2. "When you say your identity statement out loud -- 'I am becoming the kind of person who [X]' -- what does it feel like? Does it feel true, aspirational, or somewhere in between?"

3. "Which of the three areas felt most alive for you? Where did you feel the most energy or urgency?"

4. "Is there anything we uncovered that you want to sit with before our next conversation?"

## Real-World Assignment

"Here's what I want you to do before we talk next. Two things:

**First:** Write your identity statement somewhere you'll see it daily. A sticky note on your mirror, a reminder on your phone, the lock screen on your laptop. Wherever. The point isn't to stare at it -- it's to keep the question alive: 'Am I voting for this person today?'

**Second:** Do your identity vote -- [the specific micro-action they chose] -- every day for the next three days. Not seven days. Three. I want you to notice two things: how it feels to do it, and whether it starts to change how you see yourself, even a little.

When we talk next, I'm going to ask you: 'Did you vote? And what did you notice?' That's it. No judgment on the outcome. Just: did you show up, and what happened?"
