# Exercise: Habit Audit

**Type:** assessment

**Used after:** Lesson 2

## Purpose

This exercise maps the user's existing daily habits using the four-part habit loop: cue, craving, response, reward. Most people operate on autopilot for 40-50% of their day without realizing it. By the end of this exercise, the user will have identified 3-5 automatic behaviors, traced each through its full loop, rated each as positive/negative/neutral, and -- most importantly -- uncovered the real reward underneath the surface-level reward. This awareness is the prerequisite for changing any behavior.

## Setup

Frame this as detective work, not self-judgment:

"We're going to do something like an audit -- but not the boring tax kind. Think of it more like being a detective of your own life. We're going to look at what you actually do every day, especially the stuff you do without thinking. No behavior is too small or too embarrassing. I'm not here to judge any of it -- I'm here to help you SEE it. The stuff we can't see is the stuff that runs our lives. Ready to investigate?"

If the user seems apprehensive: "This isn't about finding out what's wrong with you. Most of what we'll find is neutral or positive. We're just turning the lights on."

## Agent Script

### Step 1: Walk Through the Morning

Start with the most routine-dense part of the day.

"Let's start with your morning. Walk me through what happens from the moment you open your eyes. Not your ideal morning -- your ACTUAL morning. The one that happens most days. What's the very first thing you do?"

Let the user narrate. Listen carefully. Ask follow-up questions to get specifics:
- "After you [action], then what?"
- "How long does that usually take?"
- "Is that something you decide to do, or does it just happen?"
- "Where are you when you do that? Still in bed? Kitchen? Bathroom?"

Keep going until you've covered at least the first 60-90 minutes of their day. You're looking for automatic behaviors -- things they don't consciously choose.

"Okay, I'm noticing a pattern already. Let me reflect back what I heard: [summarize their morning sequence]. Does that sound right?"

### Step 2: Identify 3-5 Automatic Behaviors

Pull out the habits from the narrative.

"From everything you just told me, I want to pull out the behaviors that happen on autopilot -- the ones you don't really decide to do, they just happen. I'm hearing these:

1. [Behavior 1 -- e.g., checking phone immediately after alarm]
2. [Behavior 2 -- e.g., making coffee before anything else]
3. [Behavior 3 -- e.g., scrolling social media while eating breakfast]
4. [Behavior 4 -- e.g., listening to a podcast during commute]
5. [Behavior 5 -- e.g., snacking when arriving at desk]

Do those feel right? Are any of those more deliberate than automatic? And is there anything I missed -- something you do every morning that you didn't mention?"

Adjust the list based on their feedback. Aim for 3-5 solid automatic behaviors. If they can only identify 2, that's fine -- quality over quantity. If they can't identify any: "Sometimes we're so deep in our routines we can't see them. Let me ask it differently -- is there anything you'd feel weird NOT doing in the morning? Something that would feel off if you skipped it?"

### Step 3: Trace the Full Loop for Each Habit

For each identified habit, walk through the four-part loop. Take them one at a time.

"Now let's be detectives. For each of these, we're going to trace the full loop. There are four parts: the cue -- what triggers it; the craving -- what you're actually wanting in that moment; the response -- the behavior itself; and the reward -- what you get from it. Let's start with [Habit 1]."

**For each habit, ask:**

**Cue:** "What triggers this? What happens RIGHT BEFORE you do it? Is it a time of day, a location, an emotional state, something someone else does, or something that just finished?"

If they say "I don't know": "That's normal -- cues are invisible by design. Let's reconstruct. Yesterday morning, walk me through the 30 seconds before you [behavior]. What was happening? Where were you? What had you just finished doing?"

**Craving:** "This is the tricky one. When that cue fires, what are you actually wanting? Not the behavior -- the feeling. When you reach for your phone first thing, are you wanting connection? Stimulation? To feel caught up? To avoid the quiet?"

This step often takes the longest. The craving is the driver but it's rarely obvious. Help them dig: "If I physically prevented you from doing this behavior right after the cue -- what would you feel? That uncomfortable feeling IS the craving."

**Response:** "The response is the behavior itself -- [name the behavior]. That's the easy part. But notice: is there a simpler version of the response that would satisfy the same craving? Keep that in the back of your mind."

**Reward:** "What do you get from this? Right after you do it -- in that moment -- what's the payoff? How do you feel?"

Then go deeper (this is the critical part): "That's the surface reward. Now let me push a little. Is there a deeper reward underneath that one? For example, someone who checks their phone first thing might say the reward is 'I see what's happening.' But the deeper reward might be 'I feel like I'm not missing out' or 'I feel in control of my day before it starts.' What's underneath yours?"

Repeat this loop for each of the 3-5 habits. Keep a running summary visible:

"Here's what we've mapped so far:

| Habit | Cue | Craving | Response | Reward (surface) | Reward (deep) |
|-------|-----|---------|----------|-------------------|---------------|
| [1]   | ... | ...     | ...      | ...               | ...           |
| [2]   | ... | ...     | ...      | ...               | ...           |"

### Step 4: Rate Each Habit

Now categorize.

"Let's step back and look at this map. For each habit, I want you to give it a quick rating: positive (this serves you well), negative (this works against who you want to be), or neutral (it's fine, neither helping nor hurting).

Don't overthink it -- go with your gut."

Go through each one. If the user rates everything as "neutral," push gently: "Neutral is valid, but let me challenge one of these. [Pick the most obviously costly habit.] If you did this every day for five years, where does it take you? Still neutral?"

If everything is "negative," rebalance: "I notice you're being pretty hard on yourself. Let's look again -- [pick the most positive habit]. This one is getting you [reward]. That's not nothing. Can we give this one a positive?"

### Step 5: Sort Into Keep, Change, Investigate

"Now let's sort. Based on your ratings:

**Keep:** Which habits are serving you? These are your foundation. We don't touch these -- we might even build on them later.

**Change:** Which ones are actively working against the person you want to become? These are the ones we'll design new systems around.

**Investigate:** Which ones are you unsure about? Sometimes a habit looks negative but the deep reward it serves is legitimate -- we just need a better way to get that reward."

For any habit marked "change": "Before we move on -- remember that deep reward we identified for [habit]? That need doesn't go away just because we change the behavior. Whatever we replace this with has to serve that same need. Otherwise the old habit will keep pulling you back."

### Step 6: Find the Real Reward

For habits marked "change," do one final dig.

"For [habit they want to change], we said the deep reward is [deep reward]. Let me ask you: is there a way to get that same reward that you'd actually feel good about? If the deep reward of your phone scrolling is 'feeling connected,' what's another behavior that gives you connection in a way that aligns with who you want to be?"

Help them brainstorm. Don't solve it for them -- guide with questions:
- "What else gives you that feeling?"
- "When was the last time you felt [deep reward] from something you were proud of?"
- "If you couldn't do [current habit], how would you meet that need?"

The goal is not to design the replacement yet (that's a later exercise). The goal is to plant the seed: the reward is valid, the behavior is negotiable.

### If the User...

- **Is enthusiastic:** Channel their energy into precision. "I love that you're seeing this so clearly. Let's go even deeper -- for each habit, can you identify the EXACT moment the cue fires? The more precisely we can pinpoint it, the more power you'll have to intervene later." Enthusiastic users often want to fix everything immediately. Redirect: "I know you want to redesign all of these right now. We will. But today is about seeing -- next session is about building."

- **Is resistant:** Common resistance: "I don't have habits, I just do things." Response: "That IS a habit. The fact that it feels like 'just doing things' means it's so automatic you don't even notice the choice. That's actually the definition of a habit -- a behavior so repeated that it stopped requiring a decision. Let's look at what you 'just do' and see what's driving it." Another form of resistance: "This feels like overanalyzing." Response: "I get that. We're not going to live here forever -- this is a one-time audit. You wouldn't drive a car without checking the dashboard once. This is us looking at the dashboard."

- **Is confused:** Simplify the loop. "Let me make this easier. Think of it like a story with four parts: something happens (the trigger), you feel a pull (the want), you do something (the action), and you get something out of it (the payoff). Let's just tell the story of [one specific habit] from this morning." Use their specific language, not technical terms. You can introduce cue/craving/response/reward labels AFTER they understand the concept through their own experience.

- **Gets emotional:** Habit audits can surface shame -- especially around habits users consider "bad" (eating, scrolling, procrastinating, drinking). If emotion arises: "I want to pause here. There's no shame in any of this. Every habit you have exists because it solved a problem at some point. Your brain did its job -- it found a way to meet a need. We're not here to judge past-you. We're here to give future-you better options." If they express frustration about how long a habit has persisted: "The length of a habit says nothing about your character. It just means the loop is strong. Strong loops can be redirected -- that's literally what we're learning to do."

## Debrief

1. "Now that you can see these loops -- does anything about your day make more sense? Any 'oh, THAT'S why I do that' moments?"

2. "Which habit surprised you the most when we traced the full loop? Was the real reward different from what you expected?"

3. "Looking at your audit as a whole -- are you mostly on autopilot in a direction you want to go, or in a direction you'd rather change?"

4. "What's one thing you're going to pay attention to differently now that you've seen the loop?"

## Real-World Assignment

"Between now and next time, I want you to do one thing: be a detective of your own habits for two full days. You don't need to write anything down unless you want to. But when you catch yourself in an automatic behavior -- any automatic behavior -- pause for one second and name the cue. Just the cue. That's it.

'Oh -- I just sat down on the couch and that was the cue to reach for the remote.'
'Oh -- I finished lunch and that was the cue to check Instagram.'
'Oh -- my partner said something stressful and that was the cue to open the fridge.'

You're not changing anything. You're just noticing triggers. The goal is to start seeing the invisible machinery. When we talk next, I'll ask you: 'What cues did you catch?' Even noticing one is a win."
