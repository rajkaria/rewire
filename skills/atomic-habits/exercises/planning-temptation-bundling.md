# Exercise: Temptation Bundling

**Type:** planning

**Used after:** Lesson 4

## Purpose

This exercise pairs behaviors the user needs to do (but resists) with activities they genuinely enjoy (but might feel guilty about). By linking obligation with pleasure, the new habit borrows motivation from the reward of the enjoyable activity. By the end, the user will have a list of genuine pleasures, a list of target habits, 1-2 practical bundles designed and sequenced, and a plan to implement them starting the next day. The core insight: you don't need more discipline -- you need better pairing.

## Setup

Open with honesty about how motivation actually works:

"Let's be real about something. The habits you want to build? You probably already know you should do them. Knowing isn't the problem. The problem is that in the moment, they don't feel appealing enough to beat whatever else is competing for your attention. Today we're going to fix that -- not by making you more disciplined, but by making the habit more attractive. We're going to pair the things you need to do with the things you love to do. Think of it like making a deal with yourself -- and it's a deal where everybody wins. This should take about 10 minutes."

## Agent Script

### Step 1: List Genuine Pleasures

Surface what the user actually enjoys -- without judgment.

"First, I want you to list things you genuinely enjoy doing. Not things you think you should enjoy. Things that actually pull you in. The guilty pleasures count. The mindless stuff counts. The things you'd never put on a resume but you'd do every day if you could. What do you actually love doing?"

Let them list. Prompt if needed:
- "What do you do to unwind after a hard day?"
- "What would you do for an hour if nobody was watching or judging?"
- "What are you always making time for even when you're busy?"
- "Is there something you look forward to every day?"

Common answers: watching a specific show, listening to podcasts, scrolling social media, playing video games, drinking good coffee, taking long showers, snacking on something specific, calling a friend, online shopping/browsing, listening to music.

Aim for 5-8 items. Write them down.

"Great list. No judgment on any of these. These are your motivational assets. Most people treat their pleasures like vices to eliminate. We're going to treat them like fuel."

### Step 2: List Target Habits

Now surface the behaviors they want to build or maintain.

"Now the other list. What are the habits you want to do but keep avoiding, procrastinating on, or dropping? The things you know serve you but that feel like a chore in the moment."

Let them list. Prompt if needed:
- "What keeps showing up on your to-do list without getting done?"
- "What do you start strong on Monday and abandon by Wednesday?"
- "If your future self could force current-you to do one thing daily, what would it be?"

Common answers: exercising, meditating, reading, studying, cleaning, meal prep, stretching, journaling, practicing a skill, doing admin/financial tasks, flossing, taking supplements.

Aim for 3-5 items. Write them down.

"Good. Now we have two lists: what pulls you forward and what you keep pushing away. Let's introduce them to each other."

### Step 3: Brainstorm Bundles

Pair items from the two lists creatively.

"Here's the game. For each habit you want to build, I want us to find a pleasure from your list that could pair with it. The formula is:

'I only get to [PLEASURE] while/after I [HABIT].'

The key word is ONLY. The pleasure becomes the reward -- or the companion -- for the habit. Let's brainstorm. No filter yet. Some of these will be impractical. That's fine. Let's just generate ideas."

Go through each target habit and explore pairings:

"Let's take [Target Habit 1]. Look at your pleasure list. Is there anything there that could happen during or right after this habit?"

Guide the brainstorm with examples of pairing logic:
- **During pairings** (habit and pleasure happen simultaneously): Podcasts/audiobooks while exercising, favorite music while cleaning, a great coffee while journaling, a TV show while stretching/folding laundry
- **After pairings** (pleasure is the reward for completing the habit): Social media only after 10 minutes of reading, video games only after studying for 30 minutes, favorite snack only after a workout, calling a friend only after completing admin tasks

For each pair, just note it -- don't evaluate yet:

"Here's what we've got so far:
1. [Habit A] + [Pleasure X] -- [during/after]
2. [Habit A] + [Pleasure Y] -- [during/after]
3. [Habit B] + [Pleasure Z] -- [during/after]
..."

Generate at least 4-6 possible bundles.

### Step 4: Evaluate Practicality

Now filter the list.

"Let's get real about which of these actually work. For each bundle, I want to test three things:"

Go through each bundle and ask:

**Compatibility test:** "Can these two things actually happen together (or in sequence) without one ruining the other? Watching TV while meditating doesn't work. Listening to a podcast while running does."

**Sustainability test:** "Would you actually stick with this pairing, or would it start to feel forced after a few days? The pleasure has to stay pleasurable even when it's paired."

**Integrity test:** "Does this pairing undermine the quality of the habit? If listening to a podcast during exercise makes you phone it in on the workout, the bundle isn't serving you."

Cross off bundles that fail any test. For surviving bundles, rank them: "Which of these remaining bundles excites you the most? Which one makes you think 'I could actually look forward to that'?"

### Step 5: Pick 1-2 Bundles to Implement

Select and commit.

"Let's pick your top one or two. These are the bundles you're going to implement starting tomorrow."

For each selected bundle, get specific:

"Let's spell it out precisely:

**Bundle 1:**
- Habit: [specific behavior]
- Paired with: [specific pleasure]
- Timing: [during / immediately after]
- Rule: 'I only get to [PLEASURE] while/after I [HABIT].'

Can you say that rule out loud? Does it feel like a deal you'd actually keep?"

If they pick two bundles, make sure they're at different times of day to avoid overwhelm.

### Step 6: Design the Specific Sequence

Map out exactly how the bundle plays out in real time.

"Let's walk through tomorrow. It's [TIME]. You're in [LOCATION]. Walk me through exactly how this bundle happens, minute by minute."

Help them script the sequence:
1. What triggers the start? (Cue from their existing routine)
2. What do they do first -- the habit or the pleasure setup?
3. How long does the habit portion last?
4. When does the pleasure kick in?
5. How does it end?

"For example: 'It's 6:30am. I walk to the living room. I set up my yoga mat (that I left out last night). I open my podcast app and queue up the episode I've been saving. I press play and start my stretching routine. The podcast is my reward for being on the mat. When the episode is over, I'm done.'

Now build yours."

Address the "only" rule: "The hardest part of this is the restriction -- the 'ONLY.' That means no podcast at other times of day. No TV show unless you've done the paired habit. This is what creates the pull. If you can access the pleasure anytime, the bundle loses its power. Can you commit to the 'only' rule for at least two weeks?"

If they can't commit to full restriction: "That's okay. Let's try a softer version: you get [pleasure] anytime, but you get [premium version of pleasure] ONLY with the habit. For example, you can listen to any podcast anytime, but your ONE favorite podcast is reserved for workouts. Does that work?"

### If the User...

- **Is enthusiastic:** They'll want to bundle everything. "I love this energy. Here's the risk: if you create too many 'only' rules at once, your life starts to feel like a negotiation. Pick two bundles max. Make them work for two weeks. Then we expand." If they're excited about a creative pairing that's slightly impractical, let them try it: "I'm not sure that'll work perfectly, but I love that you're thinking creatively. Try it for three days and we'll evaluate."

- **Is resistant:** Common resistance: "I don't want to ruin my pleasure by attaching it to a chore." Response: "That's a fair concern. Here's what actually happens in practice -- the habit starts to absorb some of the positive feeling from the pleasure. Your brain starts associating the habit with enjoyment. You're not ruining the pleasure; you're upgrading the habit. But if a specific pairing feels wrong, trust that instinct. We'll find a better match." Another form: "This feels like bribery." Response: "It IS a form of negotiation with yourself. But so is everything else you do. You go to work because you get paid. You eat because food tastes good. Every behavior is sustained by something. We're just being intentional about what sustains this one."

- **Is confused:** Simplify: "Think of it this way. You have something you love doing. You have something you keep avoiding. What if you could only do the fun thing while doing the hard thing? Like a kid who can only eat dessert if they eat their vegetables. Except you're the kid AND the parent, and you get to design the deal. What's your favorite 'dessert' and what's your 'vegetables'?" Build from that concrete frame.

- **Gets emotional:** If emotion arises around the pleasure list (guilt about "wasted time," shame about "unproductive" habits): "I want to name something. You seem uncomfortable about enjoying [pleasure]. There's no shame in what you enjoy. Pleasure isn't the opposite of productivity -- it's the fuel for it. The fact that you love [pleasure] is a RESOURCE, not a weakness. We're going to use it, not judge it." If they struggle to list pleasures at all (possible sign of anhedonia or depression): "I notice it's hard to name things you enjoy. That's worth paying attention to. For now, let's work with 'things you gravitate toward even if you wouldn't call them enjoyable.' What do you find yourself doing when you're on autopilot?"

## Debrief

1. "Looking at your bundles -- do they feel like something you'd actually look forward to? Or do they feel like homework with a bribe attached? Be honest."

2. "Which pleasure surprised you as a motivational tool? Did pairing it with a habit change how you think about it?"

3. "Is the 'only' rule going to be the hard part? What's your strategy for sticking to it when you want the pleasure without the habit?"

4. "How does it feel to use enjoyment as a tool for change instead of something to feel guilty about?"

## Real-World Assignment

"Here's your assignment for the next seven days:

**Day 1 (tonight or tomorrow):** Set up the bundle. Whatever you need to prepare -- download the podcast episodes, move the equipment, set the timer, put the supplies in the right place. Remove any friction between you and the bundle.

**Days 1-7:** Execute the bundle. Follow the 'only' rule as strictly as you can. [PLEASURE] happens only during/after [HABIT].

**Each day, notice one thing:** Is the habit getting easier? Is the pleasure still enjoyable? Are you starting to look forward to the habit because of the pairing?

**If the 'only' rule breaks** (and it might -- that's okay): Notice WHY it broke. Were you stressed? Bored? Did someone else trigger it? Don't judge it. Just note it and reset the next day.

When we talk next, I'll ask: 'What happened with your bundle? Did the pairing change how you felt about the habit?' We'll refine from there. Some bundles stick perfectly. Others need adjustment. Both are useful data."
