# Exercise: Implementation Intentions

**Type:** planning

**Used after:** Lesson 3

## Purpose

This exercise takes the user from vague good intentions ("I should meditate more") to precise behavioral plans locked to specific times, locations, and existing habits. By the end, the user will have 1-2 fully specified implementation intentions in the format "I will [behavior] at [time] in [location]," at least one habit stack linking a new behavior to an existing routine, an environment audit identifying where to place cues, and a stress-tested plan that accounts for likely failure points. Vague plans fail. Specific plans succeed. This exercise is where the specificity happens.

## Setup

Frame this as engineering, not willpower:

"Today we're going to build something. Not brainstorm, not reflect -- actually design a specific plan for your habit. The difference between people who follow through and people who don't usually isn't motivation or discipline. It's specificity. People who say 'I'll work out more' almost never do. People who say 'I'll do pushups in my bedroom at 7:15am right after I brush my teeth' follow through at dramatically higher rates. We're going to build that level of specificity for you. This should take about 15 minutes."

If the user has already been through the Habit Audit: "Remember those habits we mapped out? Now we're going to use that knowledge to build something new."

## Agent Script

### Step 1: Choose the Target Habit

Help the user select one habit to plan around.

"First, let's pick the habit you want to build. What's one behavior you want to make a consistent part of your life? It could be something from our earlier conversation, or something new."

**What to listen for:**
- If they pick something enormous ("I want to start running marathons"), scale it down: "I love the ambition. For this exercise, let's pick the smallest meaningful version of that. What's the entry point? Not the marathon -- the first two minutes."
- If they pick something vague ("I want to be more productive"), make it behavioral: "Productivity is an outcome. What's the specific behavior? What would I literally see you doing?"
- If they can't choose, help: "What's the one thing that, if you did it consistently, would have the biggest positive ripple effect on your life right now?"

Land on a clear, specific behavior. Confirm: "So the habit we're designing for is: [behavior]. Yes?"

### Step 2: Build the Implementation Intention

Construct the explicit plan.

"Now let's lock this into your life. We need three things: the exact behavior, the exact time, and the exact location. Let's start with time."

**Time:**
"When in your day does this habit fit most naturally? Not when you WISH you'd do it -- when does your schedule actually have space? And be honest about your energy. If you're a zombie before 8am, scheduling a creative habit at 6am is setting yourself up for failure."

If they say "whenever I have time": "That means never. 'Whenever' is where habits go to die. Let's pick a specific time. Even if it's approximate -- 'after lunch, around 12:30' is much better than 'sometime in the afternoon.'"

**Location:**
"Where will you be at [time]? Be specific -- not just 'at home' but 'in my kitchen' or 'at my desk in the bedroom.'"

**Behavior (refined):**
"And the behavior itself -- let's make it crisp. How long does it take? What does it look like from the outside?"

Now assemble it: "Let's put it together. Say it out loud or type it out:

'I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION].'

For example: 'I will write in my journal for 5 minutes at 7:00am at the kitchen table.'

What's yours?"

Read it back to them. "Does this feel doable? Not aspirational -- genuinely doable on your worst day?"

If it doesn't feel doable, shrink something: the duration, the complexity, or the frequency. The plan must pass the "bad day" test.

### Step 3: Build a Habit Stack

Layer the new habit onto an existing one.

"Now let's make this even stickier. You already have habits that happen on autopilot every day -- we found some in your habit audit. We're going to attach your new habit to one of those existing routines. This is called stacking -- you use the momentum of something you already do to carry you into the new thing."

"The formula is: 'After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT].'

What's something you do every single day, without fail, right around the time you planned your new habit?"

Help them find a reliable anchor:
- Morning: making coffee, brushing teeth, getting out of bed, showering, getting dressed
- Midday: eating lunch, returning from a break, closing the laptop for the day
- Evening: getting home, eating dinner, putting kids to bed, brushing teeth at night

"So the stack becomes: 'After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT].' Say it."

Test it: "Does this flow naturally? Can you picture yourself doing [current habit] and then immediately moving into [new habit] without any friction?"

If there's a gap (e.g., the current habit happens in the bathroom and the new habit requires being at a desk), find a better anchor or bridge the gap: "Is there something you do between [current habit] and being at [new location] that could serve as the link?"

### Step 4: Audit the Environment

Design the physical space to support the plan.

"Here's where most plans quietly fail: the environment isn't set up for success. Let's audit yours."

"When it's [TIME] and you're in [LOCATION], will your cue be visible? Will the thing you need be within arm's reach?"

Go through practical logistics:
- "What physical object do you need for this habit? Where is it right now?"
- "Could you put it somewhere more visible or accessible? What would that look like?"
- "Is there anything in your environment that will compete for your attention at that moment? Your phone? TV? Another person's routine?"

Design specific environmental changes:
- "What if you [laid out your journal on the kitchen table the night before / put your running shoes by the bed / set your guitar on its stand in the living room]?"
- "Is there something you could remove from the space to reduce distractions?"

"The goal is to make the right behavior the path of least resistance. When your environment is designed well, you don't need willpower -- you just follow the obvious next step."

### Step 5: Write the Full Plan

Consolidate everything into one clear plan.

"Let's write the whole thing out. Your complete habit plan:

**Implementation Intention:** I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION].

**Habit Stack:** After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT].

**Environment Design:** [Specific changes to make -- cue placement, friction reduction]

Read that back. How does it feel? Does it feel like something that could actually happen tomorrow morning?"

### Step 6: Stress-Test the Plan

Anticipate failure before it happens.

"Now let's break it. I want you to think about the most likely thing that would stop you from following through. Not the dramatic stuff -- the mundane stuff. The ordinary Tuesday morning where it all falls apart."

Ask these questions one at a time:

"What happens if you wake up late?"

"What happens if someone else disrupts your routine? A partner, a kid, a meeting?"

"What happens if you're just not feeling it -- low energy, bad mood, zero motivation?"

"What happens on weekends / travel days / unusual schedule days?"

For each scenario, design a fallback: "What's the 'never miss' version? The absolute minimum you could do that still counts as showing up? Even on the worst day?"

The fallback should be so small it's almost impossible to skip:
- Full version: 20 minutes of journaling --> Fallback: Open journal and write one sentence
- Full version: 30-minute workout --> Fallback: 5 pushups
- Full version: Meditate for 10 minutes --> Fallback: Take 3 conscious breaths

"Your plan now has two modes: the full version for good days, and the 'never miss' version for everything else. The rule is: you can scale down, but you never skip entirely. Missing once is an accident. Missing twice is the start of a new pattern."

### If the User...

- **Is enthusiastic:** Enthusiastic users often want to plan five habits at once. Channel this: "I can feel your momentum and I don't want to lose it. But here's what I've seen: people who plan one habit and nail it build more confidence than people who plan five and miss three. Let's make this one bulletproof. If it's still feeling easy after two weeks, we add the next one." If they push back, let them plan a second one but emphasize: "This is your primary. The second one is a bonus. If life gets hard, the primary is the one you protect."

- **Is resistant:** Common resistance: "I don't need to plan this specifically, I'll just do it when the moment feels right." Response: "I respect that instinct. Let me ask you something -- how many times have you tried to do this habit before without a specific plan? And how did that go?" Pause. Let the silence work. Then: "The plan isn't a straitjacket. It's a default. You can always flex. But without a default, the default becomes doing nothing." Another form: "This feels rigid and I don't like rigidity." Response: "Think of it less like a rule and more like a GPS. It gives you a route. You can take a detour anytime. But without the route, you just drive in circles."

- **Is confused:** If the implementation intention formula feels abstract: "Let me just ask you directly. Tomorrow morning, what time will you wake up? Okay. And what's the first thing you do after that? Good. Now, where in that sequence does [new habit] fit? Let's literally slot it in." Walk them through their actual tomorrow rather than building the formula first.

- **Gets emotional:** Planning exercises can trigger anxiety about failure ("What if I can't stick to this either?") or grief about past failed attempts. If this comes up: "I hear that this brings up past experiences. Every failed attempt taught your brain something -- you're not starting from scratch, you're starting from experience. This plan is different because it's designed differently. We're not relying on motivation. We're redesigning the system. And we built in a fallback specifically so that 'failure' isn't even possible -- the minimum is so low that you can always show up."

## Debrief

1. "How does it feel to have a plan this specific? Is that reassuring or anxiety-inducing? Be honest."

2. "What part of the plan do you trust the most? What part do you trust the least?"

3. "Is there anything about your environment or schedule we didn't account for?"

4. "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you'll follow through tomorrow? If it's below a 7, what would need to change to bring it up?"

If confidence is below 7: revisit the plan together. Shrink the habit, change the time, find a better anchor. The plan should leave them at 7+ confidence.

## Real-World Assignment

"Your assignment is straightforward: execute the plan for five days.

Here's exactly what I want you to do:

1. **Tonight:** Set up your environment. [Specific environmental changes they designed.] Make tomorrow's version of you walk into a space that's already set up for success.

2. **Tomorrow through Day 5:** Follow the plan. Full version when you can, 'never miss' version when you can't.

3. **After each day:** Take 10 seconds to note one thing. Just one. Did you follow the plan? If yes -- how did it feel? If no -- what actually happened? Not a journal entry. One sentence.

When we talk next, I'm going to ask you three questions: 'Did the plan work? What got in the way? And what would you change?' We'll refine from there. The first version of any plan is a hypothesis -- we're going to test it and improve it."
