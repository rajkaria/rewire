## Core Concept

The third law of behavior change is about reducing the effort required to perform the desired behavior. The central insight: humans are wired to follow the path of least resistance. Instead of fighting that tendency, harness it. The agent should teach the user to engineer their environment and routines so that the desired behavior requires less effort than the undesired one.

There are two key mechanisms here. First, friction reduction — systematically removing the steps, decisions, and obstacles between the user and the target behavior. Every extra step is a chance to quit. Second, the two-minute rule — any habit can be scaled down to a version that takes two minutes or less. "Read before bed" becomes "open the book." "Run three miles" becomes "put on running shoes." The point is not that two minutes of effort produces results. The point is that showing up consistently rewires identity. The entry point matters more than the finish line because you cannot optimize a behavior you are not doing.

The agent should help the user see that effort optimization is not laziness — it is strategic design. High performers do not rely on extraordinary discipline. They build systems where the right behavior is the easiest behavior.

## Key Takeaway

The best habit is the one you actually do. Make it so easy you cannot say no.

## Teaching Approach

1. **Start with their target habit.** Ask the user what habit they are trying to build (or pull from their earlier work in the skill). Get the specific behavior, not the aspiration.

2. **Audit the friction.** Walk through what currently happens between the moment they decide to do the habit and the moment they actually do it. Count the steps. Every step is a friction point. Ask: "What gets in the way? Where do you stall out?" Listen for logistical barriers (gear is in the closet, app is buried on phone, ingredients are not prepped) and decision barriers (which workout? what to write about? which book?).

3. **Apply the two-minute rule.** Take whatever habit they named and shrink it. Keep shrinking until it feels almost absurdly small. The user will resist this — they will feel like a two-minute version is pointless. This is the critical coaching moment. Explain that the two-minute version is not the habit. It is the entry point. The goal right now is to become the kind of person who shows up, not the kind of person who completes a perfect session. Showing up is the skill being trained.

4. **Design the path of least resistance.** Help the user rearrange their environment so the desired behavior is the default action. This means pre-decisions (lay out clothes the night before, pre-load the app, set the book on the pillow), removal of decision points (same workout every Monday, same writing prompt every morning), and physical placement (put the guitar where you sit, put the journal next to the coffee maker).

5. **Address perfectionism directly.** Many users will say some version of "but two minutes won't get me results." The agent should validate the desire for results and then reframe: doing it badly for two minutes beats not doing it perfectly. Consistency at a low level always outperforms sporadic heroics. The user is building the showing-up muscle first. Intensity can increase later — but only after the behavior is automatic.

6. **Flip it for bad habits.** Briefly introduce the inverse: if reducing friction builds good habits, adding friction disrupts bad ones. Move the phone charger out of the bedroom. Log out of social media after each session. Delete the app and only use the browser version. Do not make it impossible — just make it slightly harder. The extra seconds create a decision point where autopilot breaks.

7. **Bridge to the two-minute-design exercise.** Tell the user you are going to move into a hands-on exercise where they will design the two-minute version of their habit and map out the friction points to eliminate. This is where the concept becomes a concrete plan.

### Branching Guidance

- **If the user already has a simple habit:** Skip the shrinking step and focus on friction audit and environment design. Ask what still gets in the way even though the habit is simple.
- **If the user is a perfectionist or overachiever:** Spend more time on step 5. Use the analogy of a pilot's pre-flight checklist — the checklist is not the flight, but without it, the flight does not happen. Two minutes is their pre-flight checklist.
- **If the user is skeptical about small steps:** Ask them to recall a time they intended to do something but did not. Walk through the friction. Then ask: "What if every one of those barriers was already handled before you even thought about it?" Let them arrive at the conclusion themselves.
- **If the user is dealing with a bad habit, not building a good one:** Pivot to the friction-addition approach. Focus on making the unwanted behavior slightly harder rather than requiring willpower to resist it. This pairs well with Lesson 7 (Breaking Bad Habits).
- **If the user names a complex habit (e.g., "meal prep"):** Break it into sub-behaviors and apply the two-minute rule to the first domino in the chain. Meal prep starts with "open the recipe app" or "put one ingredient on the counter."

## Questions to Ask

1. "Walk me through exactly what happens between the moment you decide to do [habit] and the moment you actually start doing it. How many steps are there?"

2. "If you had to shrink this habit down to something you could do in two minutes or less — even on your worst, most exhausted day — what would that look like?"

3. "What is already in place in your environment that makes it easy to do the things you do not want to do? What does your environment make effortless right now?"

4. "Think about a habit you do consistently without thinking — brushing teeth, checking your phone, making coffee. What makes that so automatic? What friction has been completely removed?"

5. "When you hear 'just do two minutes,' what is your honest reaction? What comes up for you?"
   - If resistance: "Where does that resistance come from? What would it mean if two minutes was actually enough for now?"
   - If acceptance: "Good. Now what would it look like to make even that two-minute version require zero decision-making?"

6. "If someone secretly rearranged your home tonight to make [desired habit] the absolute easiest thing to do tomorrow morning, what would they change?"

7. "What is the very first physical action in your habit? Not the mental decision — the first thing your body does. How can we make that action unavoidable?"

8. "Here is a test: could you do your two-minute version right now, in the next sixty seconds, with zero preparation? If not, what is still in the way?"

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Two minutes is not enough to matter."** Redirect: Two minutes is not the habit. It is the gateway. Every consistent runner in the world started by putting on shoes. The two-minute version trains the most important skill in behavior change — showing up. Once you are showing up every day, extending the duration is trivial. But you cannot extend something that is not happening.

- **"I should aim higher than this."** Redirect: Aiming lower IS aiming higher, because it is the only way to actually build the showing-up muscle. Anyone can do something hard once. The question is whether you can do something small every single day for months. That is the real challenge, and it is the one that produces transformation. Meet yourself where you are, not where you think you should be.

- **"Easy means lazy."** Redirect: Easy means strategic. Think about how any well-designed product works — the best ones remove every unnecessary step. You are designing your behavior the way an engineer designs a product. The goal is not maximum effort. The goal is maximum consistency. And consistency requires removing unnecessary obstacles.

- **"I have done this before and it works for a week, then I stop."** Redirect: That is exactly the pattern this approach fixes. If you stop after a week, the habit was still too hard or too reliant on motivation. Shrink it further. Make the environment do more of the work. The fact that you stopped is information about the design, not about your character.

- **"My problem is not friction, it is motivation."** Redirect: Motivation fluctuates daily — it is unreliable fuel. Friction is constant and designable. On the days your motivation is high, you will do the habit regardless. The system you are building is for the days your motivation is at zero. On those days, only friction reduction saves you.

## Bridge to Practice

After this conversation, guide the user into the **two-minute-design** exercise. In that exercise, they will:
1. Write down their target habit in its full form
2. Scale it down to a two-minute version
3. Map every friction point between intention and action
4. Design a specific environment change for each friction point
5. Commit to the two-minute version for the next five days

If the user is not ready for a full exercise, give them a micro-assignment: "Before our next conversation, try one thing — take whatever habit you want to build and remove one physical barrier. Move something, set something up the night before, pre-decide something. Just one friction point. Then notice what happens."

Remind the user that the next lesson will cover how to make habits satisfying — adding immediate rewards that keep the behavior going. The foundation they are building here (making it easy to start) pairs with the next lesson's focus (making it rewarding to continue).
