## Core Concept

The agent needs to convey that every habit — whether helpful or destructive — operates through the same four-step loop: cue, craving, response, reward. This loop is not just a description of habits; it is the diagnostic tool users can apply to any behavior they want to understand, change, or build.

The four steps work like this. A **cue** is the trigger that initiates the behavior — a time, place, emotional state, preceding action, or the presence of other people. A **craving** is the motivational force; it is not the habit itself but the anticipation of the reward — the desire for the state change the habit delivers. The **response** is the actual behavior — the thing you do. And the **reward** is the satisfaction or relief that follows, which teaches the brain whether this loop is worth repeating. The agent's job is to help the user dissect a real habit from their own life using this framework, and — critically — to discover that the real reward driving a habit is often not the obvious one. Someone who checks their phone every ten minutes is not seeking information; they are seeking novelty, distraction from discomfort, or a micro-hit of social validation. Until the user sees the true reward, they cannot redesign the loop.

## Key Takeaway

You cannot change what you cannot see. Mapping the habit loop makes invisible, automatic behaviors visible — and visibility is the first step to redesign.

## Teaching Approach

1. **Connect to the previous lesson.** Briefly reference the identity work from Lesson 1: "Last time we talked about who you want to become. Today we're going to look at the machinery running underneath your daily behaviors — the loop that drives every habit. Once you see how the loop works, you can start rewiring it." This framing makes the lesson feel purposeful, not academic.

2. **Ask the user to pick a habit.** Give them a choice: a habit they want to build, a habit they want to break, or just a habit they find interesting. Starting with a "neutral" or positive habit (like their morning coffee ritual) can be less threatening than going straight for a destructive one. The important thing is that they have a concrete behavior to work with.

3. **Walk through the loop together — one step at a time.** Do not explain all four steps upfront and then ask the user to apply them. Instead, discover each step through questioning:
   - Start with the **response** (the behavior itself) since it is the most concrete: "Okay, the habit is checking your phone first thing in the morning. That's the behavior — step three. Now let's work backward."
   - Move to the **cue**: "What triggers it? Is it a time, a feeling, something you see? Think about the exact moment right before you reach for the phone." Push for specificity. "Waking up" is too vague. "Hearing the alarm and feeling the phone on the nightstand" is a cue.
   - Then the **craving**: "Right before you pick it up, what are you anticipating? What do you expect to feel or find?" This is where most insight happens. The user often discovers something they did not expect.
   - Finally, the **reward**: "After you've scrolled for a few minutes, how do you actually feel? Is it the feeling you were hoping for?" Often the answer is no — the anticipated reward and the actual reward are different, which is itself a revelatory moment.

4. **Highlight the gap between perceived and actual reward.** This is the deepest insight in the lesson. Many habits persist not because the reward is good, but because the craving is strong. The phone-checking habit often leaves people feeling worse (distracted, anxious, behind) — but the craving for novelty or relief from boredom keeps the loop firing. Ask the user to sit with this disconnect. It is uncomfortable but clarifying.

5. **Generalize the framework.** Once you have dissected one habit, invite the user to see that every behavior in their day follows this same loop. The framework is universal. This is not about judging habits as good or bad — it is about seeing the architecture so you can work with it.

6. **Bridge to practice.** Connect to the habit-audit exercise. Frame it as extending what you just did to a broader set of behaviors.

### Branching Guidance

**If the user picks a habit they want to break:**
Good — this is high-motivation territory. Be careful not to jump to solutions. The goal of this lesson is diagnosis, not prescription. When the user identifies the reward, they may immediately want to know how to stop. Say something like: "We're going to get there — the next lessons are all about redesigning the loop. But the most powerful thing you can do right now is just see it clearly. Most people try to break habits they've never actually understood. You're doing the understanding part first, and that changes everything."

**If the user picks a habit they want to build:**
Run the loop in the forward direction: "What would the cue be? What would make you crave it? What's the action? What's the reward?" This is lighter and more creative. The user is designing rather than diagnosing. You can still surface insight: "What reward would actually make this feel satisfying — not in theory, but in the moment right after you do it?"

**If the user struggles to identify the craving:**
This is the hardest step for most people because cravings are fast and subconscious. Help with prompts: "Think about the feeling right before the habit. Is it boredom? Anxiety? Restlessness? A sense of incompleteness?" You can also try the deletion test: "Imagine the habit is suddenly impossible — you physically cannot do it. What feeling comes up? That discomfort points at the craving."

**If the user says "I don't have habits, I'm just disorganized":**
Everyone has habits — most of them are just invisible because they are automated. Reframe: "Disorganization is itself a set of habits — the habit of leaving things where they land, the habit of deciding later, the habit of responding to whatever feels most urgent. Those are all loops running in the background. Want to pick one and trace it?" This normalizes their situation and makes the framework accessible.

**If the user has read about habit loops before:**
Skip the introductory explanation and go straight to application. "You know the framework — let's use it. Pick the habit that's been hardest for you to change, and let's trace the loop together. I bet the craving or the reward is not what you think it is." Challenge them to go deeper than their previous understanding.

## Questions to Ask

1. "Pick one habit from your daily life — it could be one you love, one you hate, or one you barely notice. What comes to mind?" *(Opening. Low pressure. Any habit works. If they hesitate, offer examples: morning phone check, afternoon coffee, evening Netflix, nail biting.)*

2. "Walk me through the moment right before you do it. Where are you? What just happened? What are you feeling? I want to find the exact trigger." *(Digging for the cue. Push for environmental and emotional specificity. "After lunch" is not enough — "sitting at my desk after lunch, feeling sluggish and unfocused" is a cue.)*

3. "In the split second before you act, what are you expecting or hoping to feel? Not what you actually feel afterward — what you're anticipating." *(Isolating the craving. This is the question that produces the most insight. Many users will need a few attempts. Be patient and rephrase if needed: "What would be missing if you couldn't do this?")*

4. "After you've done it, honestly — how do you feel? Is that the feeling you were chasing?" *(Revealing the gap between anticipated and actual reward. If the feelings match, the loop is well-calibrated. If they do not match — which is common for "bad" habits — that mismatch is the key to change.)*

5. "Now that you've mapped this loop, what surprises you? Is the real reward what you expected, or is it something else entirely?" *(Reflection. Let the user articulate their own insight. The agent should not tell them what to conclude — let them discover it.)*

6. "If you could keep the reward but change the response — get the same underlying feeling through a different action — what might that look like?" *(Forward-looking. Plants a seed for later lessons on substitution without solving the problem prematurely. If the user has good ideas, affirm them. If they are stuck, that is fine — say "We'll work on that. For now, just seeing the loop clearly is the win.")*

7. "Think about a habit you consider 'good' — something that's working for you. Can you trace the same four steps? What keeps that loop running smoothly?" *(Builds confidence by applying the framework to something positive. Also teaches that the same architecture powers both helpful and unhelpful behaviors — it is not about good vs. bad loops, it is about designed vs. accidental ones.)*

8. "If you had to explain this loop to a friend using one of your own habits as the example, how would you describe it?" *(Teaching is the best test of understanding. If they can explain it clearly, they own the concept. If they stumble, revisit the step they struggle with.)*

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Habits are just things I repeat. There's no deeper structure."** Redirect: Repetition is the visible surface, but underneath every repeated behavior is an architecture — a trigger, a motivation, an action, and a payoff. Understanding that architecture is what separates someone who white-knuckles through change from someone who redesigns the system. Ask: "If there were no structure to habits, why would the same behaviors happen at the same time, in the same place, in the same emotional state? That consistency is the loop."

- **"The reward is obvious — I eat the cookie because it tastes good."** Redirect: Surface rewards almost always mask deeper ones. The cookie might taste good, but the real reward might be relief from stress, a break from monotony, or a sense of comfort associated with childhood. Until you identify the actual reward, you will keep trying to replace the cookie with willpower instead of with a different behavior that delivers the same underlying satisfaction. Ask: "If the cookie is about taste, would an equally tasty healthy snack work? If not, what else is the cookie giving you?"

- **"If I just remove the cue, the habit will stop."** Redirect: Removing cues can help (and is a strategy in a later lesson), but if the craving is strong enough, the brain will find new cues. The real leverage is understanding and addressing the craving — the need the habit is serving. Otherwise you are playing whack-a-mole with triggers. Ask: "Have you ever tried removing a trigger and found the habit just moved somewhere else? That is the craving finding a new path."

- **"Knowing the loop should make it easy to change."** Redirect: Awareness is necessary but not sufficient. Knowing why you check your phone does not automatically stop you. What awareness does is shift you from unconscious repetition to conscious choice — and conscious choice is where redesign becomes possible. The next lessons provide the actual tools for change. This lesson provides the diagnostic lens. Ask: "Think of it like a doctor diagnosing a condition — the diagnosis does not cure you, but you cannot cure what you have not diagnosed."

## Bridge to Practice

After this conversation, guide the user to the **habit-audit exercise** (exercises/assessment-habit-audit.md). The transition: "You just traced one habit through the loop. Now let's zoom out — I want you to map several habits from your daily life using the same framework. It only takes a few minutes, but it will show you patterns you've never noticed. Want to try?"

If the user is not ready for a full audit, assign a **loop-spotting task**: "Between now and our next conversation, I want you to catch yourself in the middle of a habit — any habit — and mentally label the four steps in real time. Cue, craving, response, reward. Just notice. You do not have to change anything. The act of noticing is the practice." This builds the diagnostic reflex that makes all future lessons more effective.

If the user wants to move forward immediately, preview the next lesson: "Now that you can see the loop, the next step is learning how to redesign it — starting with the first lever: making the cue for a good habit impossible to miss. Ready, or should we let this settle?"
