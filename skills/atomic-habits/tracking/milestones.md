## Stage 1: Awareness — Understanding the Machine

**Description:** The user understands the core frameworks: identity-based habits, the habit loop (cue-craving-response-reward), and the four laws of behavior change. They can explain these concepts in their own words and have identified at least one real habit in their life they want to work on. They are seeing their existing behaviors through a new lens but have not yet started designing changes.

**Criteria:**
- [ ] Can explain the habit loop (cue-craving-response-reward) in their own words without prompting
- [ ] Can identify the cue, craving, response, and reward for at least one existing habit in their life
- [ ] Understands the difference between outcome-based and identity-based habit change and can articulate why identity matters
- [ ] Has named at least one specific habit they want to build or break
- [ ] Can describe what their "identity goal" is — the type of person they want to become, not just the outcome they want

**Agent approach:** More teaching, more guidance. Walk the user through concepts step by step. Use lots of questions to check understanding, but provide clear explanations when they're stuck. Be patient with confusion. Use concrete examples from their life to anchor abstract concepts. Do not rush to experiments — make sure the framework is solid first.

## Stage 2: Design — Architecting Change

**Description:** The user has moved from understanding to action. They have created specific implementation intentions ("I will [behavior] at [time] in [location]"), designed at least one habit stack, and made at least one deliberate environment change. They have run at least 2 real-world experiments and reported back on what happened. They are starting to see the architecture behind their own behavior — why they do what they do and how their environment shapes their choices.

**Criteria:**
- [ ] Has written at least one implementation intention with specific time, location, and behavior
- [ ] Has designed and attempted at least one habit stack (linking a new habit to an existing one)
- [ ] Has made at least one deliberate environment change to support a habit (adding a cue, reducing friction, increasing friction for bad habits)
- [ ] Has run at least 2 experiments and reported back with specific observations about what happened
- [ ] Can diagnose why an experiment didn't work using the framework (wrong cue, too much friction, no immediate reward, etc.) rather than blaming willpower
- [ ] Has applied the two-minute rule to at least one habit — scaling it down to an entry version

**Agent approach:** Start shifting from teaching to challenging. When the user encounters a problem, ask "What do you think is going on?" before offering a diagnosis. Start asking "What would you do?" before giving answers. Push them to design their own experiments rather than following agent suggestions. Be more direct about what's not working. Introduce the concept of optimizing and iterating on habits that are already started.

## Stage 3: Momentum — The System is Running

**Description:** The user has at least one habit that has been maintained for 2+ consecutive weeks. They are catching themselves mid-loop — noticing cues and cravings in real time and sometimes choosing a different response. When they miss a day, they self-correct without guilt and get back on track. They are starting to design new habits independently, applying the methodology without needing to be walked through each step. The behavior is shifting from deliberate to increasingly automatic.

**Criteria:**
- [ ] Has maintained at least 1 habit for 2+ consecutive weeks (not perfectly, but consistently)
- [ ] Catches themselves mid-loop — reports noticing cues or cravings in real time at least once
- [ ] Self-corrects after a missed day without prompting and without guilt-spiraling — applies the "never miss twice" principle on their own
- [ ] Designs new implementation intentions or habit stacks independently, without the agent walking them through the process
- [ ] Can distinguish between a system problem (bad cue, too much friction) and an effort problem without defaulting to "I need more willpower"
- [ ] Has started to notice habit loops in other areas of their life beyond the one they're actively working on
- [ ] Uses the vocabulary of the methodology naturally in conversation (cue, friction, environment design, habit stack, etc.)

**Agent approach:** Peer-level dialogue. Stop teaching and start collaborating. Ask challenging questions that push the user to think systemically: "You've got your morning routine working — what's the next domino?" Introduce stretch challenges: optimizing existing habits, tackling harder behaviors, stacking systems together. Start connecting to bigger-picture thinking — how does this habit connect to the person they're becoming? Challenge comfortable patterns: "You're good at adding positive habits. What about the ones you want to eliminate?"

## Stage 4: Mastery — Self-Coaching / Graduation

**Description:** The user has a working system of 2 or more habits maintained for 30+ days. They can articulate the entire methodology in their own words — not reciting definitions, but explaining the principles as they apply to their own life. They design and troubleshoot habits independently. When they hit a setback, they coach themselves through it: diagnosing the system failure, adjusting the design, and getting back on track. They no longer need the agent for this skill.

**Criteria:**
- [ ] Has maintained a working system of 2+ habits for 30+ consecutive days
- [ ] Can articulate the full methodology in their own words — identity-based change, the four laws, habit stacking, environment design, the two-minute rule, tracking principles — and explain how each applies to their life
- [ ] Designs and troubleshoots new habits independently — proposes the cue, stack, environment change, and reward without agent input
- [ ] Coaches themselves through setbacks — when something breaks, they diagnose and fix the system without needing the agent to intervene
- [ ] Has taught or explained the methodology to someone else (friend, partner, colleague)
- [ ] Recognizes when a problem is NOT a habit problem and can name what it actually is (motivation, identity conflict, environment beyond their control, emotional pattern) — demonstrates the limits of the framework, not just its applications

**Agent approach:** Celebrate. This is graduation. Deliver a graduation message that names specific growth the agent has observed across their sessions. Reference early struggles and how far they've come. Then suggest what's next: related skills that build on their habit foundation (deep-work for focus habits, feeling-good if emotional patterns emerged, karma-yoga if they showed interest in purpose and detachment from outcomes). Make it clear they can always return if they need a tune-up.

**Graduation message:** "You came in wanting to build better habits. You're leaving with something bigger — a system for changing who you are, one small behavior at a time. You've gone from understanding the habit loop to running your own experiments to maintaining real systems that work. That's not just habit change — that's self-mastery. The methodology is yours now. You don't need me for this anymore. If you want to keep growing, I'd suggest exploring [recommend specific next skill based on their journey]. And if you ever need a tune-up on habits, I'm here."

## Celebration Points

Progress reinforcement is critical for sustained change. Celebrate these moments explicitly when they occur — do not wait for the user to recognize them.

### Stage 1 Transitions
- **First habit loop identified** -> "You just mapped your first habit loop. That's not a small thing — most people go their entire lives without seeing the invisible machinery behind their behavior. You can see it now. That changes everything."
- **Identity goal articulated** -> "You just named who you want to become, not just what you want to do. That shift — from outcomes to identity — is where real change starts."

### Stage 2 Transitions
- **First experiment completed** -> "You did the thing. You took the framework off the whiteboard and into your actual life. That matters more than getting it perfect. What did you learn?"
- **First environment change made** -> "You reshaped your environment instead of relying on willpower. That's thinking like a behavior designer, not just someone trying harder."
- **First failed experiment analyzed** -> "The experiment didn't work — and you figured out why. That's not failure, that's iteration. You just learned something about your own behavior that most people never discover."

### Stage 3 Transitions
- **First mid-loop catch** -> "You caught yourself in the middle of the loop. You noticed the cue, felt the craving, and paused. That is the skill in action. Awareness in real-time is the turning point."
- **Two-week streak reached** -> "Two weeks. The behavior is starting to wire itself in. You're past the point where most people quit. This is where habits start to feel less like effort and more like identity."
- **First self-correction after a miss** -> "You missed a day, and instead of spiraling, you got back on it. You applied 'never miss twice' without me having to say it. That's the methodology working through you, not just for you."
- **First independently designed habit** -> "You just designed that habit stack on your own — the cue, the sequence, the environment change. You didn't need me to walk you through it. You own this methodology now."

### Stage 4 Transitions
- **30-day system maintained** -> "Thirty days with a working system. This isn't a streak — it's a lifestyle. The habits aren't something you do anymore. They're part of who you are."
- **Taught someone else** -> "You explained this to someone else. When you can teach it, you own it. The methodology isn't something you learned — it's something you know."
- **Graduation** -> Deliver the full graduation message (see Stage 4 above). Make it personal. Reference their specific journey, their early struggles, their breakthroughs. This moment matters.
