# Ecclesiastes Wisdom Coaching

> Everything is vapor. Therefore, enjoy.

## What This Skill Does

Coaches users through the wisdom of Kohelet (Ecclesiastes) — the ancient Hebrew teacher who examined wealth, achievement, pleasure, and knowledge with radical honesty, found that none of them deliver lasting satisfaction, and arrived not at despair but at liberation. Helps users stop chasing what cannot be held (hevel), accept the season they are in (et), release the demand for total understanding, and find genuine joy (simcha) in the ordinary moments that are actually available to them.

## Source

Ecclesiastes (Kohelet) from the Hebrew Bible / Tanakh. One of the wisdom literature books, attributed traditionally to King Solomon. Among the most philosophically rigorous and psychologically honest texts in any wisdom tradition. Presented here as universal philosophical wisdom accessible to anyone, regardless of spiritual background or belief.

## What You'll Work On

- Understanding hevel (vapor/breath) — not as nihilism but as radical acceptance of impermanence
- Recognizing the rhythm and seasons of life — and stop fighting the season you are in
- Accepting the limits of wisdom — you cannot know or control everything, and that is OK
- Practicing simcha — the deliberate enjoyment of bread, work, love, and ordinary daily life

## Structure

- **4 lessons** covering hevel (impermanence), et (seasons and timing), the limits of wisdom, and simcha (the imperative to enjoy)
- **3 exercises** including a hevel audit, a week of radical enjoyment, and a season-mapping practice
- **Weekly check-ins** with progress tracking
- **4 milestones** from awareness through mastery

## Sensitivity Note

This skill draws from Hebrew biblical wisdom literature. It uses original Hebrew terminology (hevel, yitron, simcha, et, Kohelet) alongside English to preserve nuance. The material is presented as universal philosophical wisdom accessible to anyone. It never evangelizes, never requires belief, and acknowledges the spiritual context without imposing it. Kohelet asked the hardest questions without pretending to have neat answers — this skill follows that example.
