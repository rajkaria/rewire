# Milestones

## Stage 1: Recognition
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: User can name hevel in their own life — they see where they are chasing vapor and can articulate the gap between what they are pursuing and what it can actually deliver.
**The user can**: Define hevel in their own words (not as "meaningless" but as impermanent, fleeting, unholdable). Identify at least two specific areas where they are demanding permanence from impermanent things. Recognize the difference between the pursuit itself (which may be valuable) and the impossible expectation loaded onto it (which is hevel). They have begun to notice the gap between achievement and satisfaction — the "is this it?" moment — as a feature of reality rather than a personal failure.
**What to celebrate**: "You just did something most people never do — you looked honestly at what you are chasing and asked whether it can deliver what you are asking of it. That honest observation is the foundation of everything that comes next. Kohelet would approve."

## Stage 2: Acceptance
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User can identify the season they are in and begin to align with it rather than fight it. They are practicing season awareness and have started to release the demand for certainty and control.
**The user can**: Name the season they are in across multiple life domains without defaulting to the season they wish they were in. Catch themselves fighting their season and pause rather than escalate. Accept at least one area of genuine not-knowing without requiring resolution. Make at least one decision under uncertainty without paralysis. They have experienced at least one moment of deliberate, unforced simcha — enjoyment that did not require achievement or permission.
**What to celebrate**: "You named the season you are in and stopped demanding a different one. That is not passivity — that is the beginning of alignment. And that moment of enjoyment you described — the one that did not need to lead anywhere — that is simcha. It is real. You are starting to live what Kohelet observed."

## Stage 3: Integration
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User holds the paradoxes of Kohelet's wisdom naturally — things are both meaningful and impermanent, both valuable and hevel. They practice simcha regularly and can act under uncertainty without requiring guarantees.
**The user can**: Hold the tension between investing in things and knowing they will not last — without either gripping or giving up. Enjoy ordinary moments consistently, not as a technique but as a natural response to being alive. Act on the best wisdom available while accepting that their wisdom is incomplete. Move through different seasons without fighting, even difficult ones. They no longer need to understand everything before they can function. They can say "I do not know" without distress. Their relationship with enjoyment has shifted — it is less earned and more received.
**What to celebrate**: "Something has shifted in you. You are holding the paradox now — you care deeply about your life AND you hold it lightly. You enjoy what is here AND you do not demand that it stay. That is not something you can learn from a book. That is lived wisdom. Kohelet worked a lifetime to articulate what you are starting to embody."

## Stage 4: Mastery / Graduation
**Marker**: User lives with a natural lightness that comes from releasing the demand for permanence, certainty, and total understanding. Simcha is their default relationship with ordinary life, not a practice they have to remember.
**The user can**: Identify hevel-chasing in real time and release it without coaching prompts. Navigate any season — including difficult ones — with alignment rather than resistance. Make decisions under uncertainty with confidence and equanimity. Enjoy bread, work, relationships, and rest without needing them to be more than they are. Hold grief and gladness simultaneously. Articulate the difference between hevel-as-nihilism and hevel-as-liberation in their own words. Apply Kohelet's wisdom to novel situations they have never discussed in coaching. Explain this approach to someone else from lived experience, not intellectual understanding.
**What to celebrate**: "I want to name what I see. The way you hold your life now — the honesty about what you can and cannot control, the enjoyment of what is on your table, the acceptance of not knowing — that is what Kohelet pointed toward. You are not performing wisdom. You are living it. That is rare and worth honoring."

Suggest `wabi-sabi` if they want to develop an aesthetic practice around impermanence and imperfection. Suggest `seneca-letters` if they want practical Stoic tools for managing time and emotional reactivity. Suggest `mindful-path` if they want a sustained contemplative practice for staying present.
