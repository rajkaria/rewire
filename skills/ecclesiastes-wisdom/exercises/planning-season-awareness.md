# Exercise: Season Awareness

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (A Time for Everything). Use when the user has understood the concept of life seasons (et) and needs to map where they actually are — and align their expectations and actions with the season rather than fighting it.

## Setup
Agent says: "You have been thinking about seasons — the idea that there is a time for everything and you cannot choose which time you are in. Now let's get practical. We are going to map the season you are in across different areas of your life, look at where you are aligned and where you are fighting, and design your next week to match reality rather than your wish list. This is not passive acceptance — it is strategic wisdom. A farmer who plants in the right season is not lazy. They are smart."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Name the life domains
Agent says: "We are going to look at five areas of your life. For each one, I want you to name the season you are in right now — not the season you want to be in. Use whatever language feels right: planting, harvesting, resting, mourning, building, tearing down, searching, waiting, growing, letting go. There is no wrong answer. Just honest observation. Let's start with work or career. What season are you in?"

{Walk through each domain one at a time:}
1. Work / career
2. Relationships / love / family
3. Health / body / energy
4. Inner life / personal growth / spirituality
5. Creativity / play / self-expression

{For each, listen carefully. Push gently if the user names the season they want rather than the season they are in. "Is that where you are, or where you wish you were? What does the evidence say?"}

### Step 2: Identify the fights
Agent says: "Now look at your map. In which areas are you fighting the season you are in? Where are you demanding summer when it is winter? Common fights look like: demanding productivity during a season of rest, demanding clarity during a season of confusion, demanding joy during a season of grief, demanding growth during a season of letting go. Where is the biggest mismatch between where you are and what you are demanding of yourself?"

{Wait for user response. This is the diagnostic moment. The mismatch between season and expectation is the primary source of unnecessary suffering.}

### Step 3: What the season is asking for
Agent says: "For the area with the biggest mismatch — the place where you are fighting hardest — let me ask this: if you stopped fighting and listened, what is this season actually asking of you? Every season has its own wisdom. Winter asks for rest and patience. A season of mourning asks for tears and tenderness. A season of uncertainty asks for faith without evidence. What is your current season asking for that you have been refusing to give?"

{Wait for user response. Help them articulate the unmet demand of their season. This often involves giving themselves permission to do something they consider weak or unproductive — rest, grieve, not know, wait.}

### Step 4: Align the week
Agent says: "Now let's design your coming week to match the season rather than fight it. I want three specific adjustments — things you will do differently because you are honoring the season you are actually in. These are not big overhauls. They are alignment moves. For example: if you are in a season of rest but have been pushing yourself to produce, your adjustment might be 'I will go to bed by 10pm three nights this week without guilt.' If you are in a season of uncertainty, your adjustment might be 'I will make one decision this week without requiring certainty.' What are your three adjustments?"

{Help the user make adjustments that are specific, concrete, and achievable. Push back if they are too vague ("be kinder to myself") or too ambitious. The adjustments should feel like relief, not like more homework.}

### Step 5: Anticipate the resistance
Agent says: "Last step. For each adjustment, what thought or feeling will try to pull you back into fighting the season? Name it specifically. 'Going to bed early feels lazy.' 'Making a decision without certainty feels reckless.' 'Grieving feels self-indulgent.' When that thought shows up, I want you to have a ready response. Something like: 'This is the season I am in. Fighting it is the real waste of energy.' What will you say to yourself?"

{Help the user craft a specific, personal response to the resistance thought for each adjustment.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user says "Everything is in winter right now": "Multiple areas in a difficult season at once is genuinely hard. But notice — that is information, not a verdict. If everything is in winter, then the wise response is to hunker down, conserve energy, and trust that seasons change. What is the smallest act of kindness you can do for yourself during this winter? Start there."
- If the user says "I do not know what season I am in": "That might mean you are between seasons — which is its own season. It is the season of not-knowing, of transition, of waiting. That is one of the hardest seasons because it has no clear action attached to it. The wisdom of this season is patience. Can you be patient with yourself for not knowing?"
- If the user identifies clear alignment — "I am in the right season in most areas": "Good. Then the work is subtler — it is about deepening your presence in the season you are in rather than getting ahead of it. When things are going well, the most common fight is the anxiety that it will end. Are you in the season, or are you already pre-grieving its end?"
- If the user resists the exercise — "I don't want to just accept a bad situation": "Naming your season is not the same as accepting a bad situation permanently. A farmer who recognizes winter does not pretend it is OK that crops are not growing — they prepare for spring. What preparation does your current season allow? What can you do now that positions you for the next season, without demanding that this season be something it is not?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "Looking at your season map — what surprised you? Was there an area where you realized the season was different than what you had been telling yourself?"
2. "For the area with the biggest mismatch — how long have you been fighting this season? What has the fight cost you?"
3. "Did naming the season bring any relief? Sometimes just saying 'this is where I am' releases tension you did not know you were holding."
4. "Are your three adjustments realistic for this week? Rate your confidence from 1-10 for each. If any are below 8, let's make them smaller."

## Real-World Action
"Execute your three season-aligned adjustments this week. Each morning, take 30 seconds to ask yourself: what season am I in today? And each evening, take 30 seconds to reflect: did I honor the season or fight it? Keep a brief note of what you notice — just a sentence or two. We are building the habit of season awareness, not adding another chore."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I will ask you three things. First: did you do your three adjustments? What happened? Second: did the resistance thought show up, and were you able to respond to it? Third: did your relationship with the season shift at all during the week — did it feel less like a fight and more like something you could be in? The shift does not have to be dramatic. Even a small movement from fighting to flowing is significant."
