# Milestones

## Stage 1: Awareness
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: User can identify when a conversation is crucial and can name their own default pattern (silence or violence) as it happens, even if they can't yet change it.
**The user can**: Distinguish between a normal disagreement and a crucial conversation (high stakes, strong emotions, differing opinions). Identify at least three conversations they've been avoiding. Name their primary default pattern and its specific flavor (masking, avoiding, withdrawing for silence; controlling, labeling, attacking for violence). Articulate the cost of not having these conversations.
**Celebrate**: "You can see it now. That's not a small thing — most people go their whole lives on autopilot in these moments. You're already ahead because you can name what's happening."

## Stage 2: Safety Building
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User can create and restore safety in conversations by establishing mutual purpose and mutual respect, and can spot the moment safety breaks down.
**The user can**: Identify when the other person (or they themselves) no longer feels safe in a conversation — before it crashes. Construct and deliver a contrasting statement that addresses misunderstanding. Establish mutual purpose before diving into content. Separate observable facts from the stories they tell about those facts. Identify victim, villain, and helpless stories in their own thinking and begin to rewrite them.
**Celebrate**: "You're no longer just reacting — you're reading the room and adjusting. That skill will serve you in every relationship you have."

## Stage 3: Active Practice
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User initiates crucial conversations they would have previously avoided, uses the STATE framework, and genuinely listens to perspectives they disagree with.
**The user can**: Open a crucial conversation with facts rather than conclusions or emotions. Share their story tentatively while owning it as their interpretation. Ask for the other person's perspective with genuine curiosity — not as a rhetorical move. Listen actively using the four moves (ask, mirror, paraphrase, prime). Navigate a conversation where the other person gets defensive or shuts down, and restore safety in real time. Debrief their own conversations honestly — identifying what worked and what didn't.
**Celebrate**: "You're having conversations you would have run from a month ago. The fact that they're not perfect doesn't matter — what matters is that you're in the room, in dialogue, doing the work."

## Stage 4: Mastery / Graduation
**Marker**: User uses crucial conversation skills as a natural part of how they communicate, without needing to consciously apply a framework. Their important relationships are measurably stronger.
**The user can**: Initiate crucial conversations promptly rather than letting issues fester. Create safety instinctively — reading the other person's state and adjusting before being asked. Separate facts from stories automatically, catching interpretive leaps in real time. Use the STATE framework fluidly without thinking about the steps. Listen to perspectives that challenge their own without getting defensive. Navigate complex, multi-issue conversations where emotions run high. Help OTHERS stay in dialogue when they observe conversations going sideways. Report concrete improvements in at least two important relationships as a direct result of how they communicate.
**Celebrate**: "You've changed how you show up in the conversations that matter most. This isn't just a skill — it's a way of being in relationship with other people. The people around you are benefiting from the work you've done, even if they don't know why things feel different."
