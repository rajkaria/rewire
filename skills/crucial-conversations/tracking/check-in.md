# Check-In Protocol

## Frequency
Weekly. If no session in 10 days, reach out proactively with a conversation-focused check-in.

## Opening
Agent says: "Good to have you back. Let's check in on how things are going with your conversations — both the ones you've had and the ones still on your list. I want to hear about the wins, the misses, and the moments you're still processing."

## Core Questions
1. "Did you have any crucial conversations this week — planned or unplanned? Walk me through what happened. What did you say, how did they respond, and how did it land?"
2. "Were there any conversations you noticed yourself avoiding? What was the conversation, and what story were you telling yourself about why you couldn't have it?"
3. "Did you catch your default pattern — silence or violence — showing up this week? Was there a moment where you noticed it and were able to choose differently?"
4. "When you did engage in a difficult conversation, how full was the shared pool of meaning at the end? Did both people walk away feeling heard, or did something get left unsaid?"

## Secondary Questions (use selectively based on where the user is in the curriculum)

**After Lesson 2 (Safety):**
- "Did you notice any moments this week where safety broke down in a conversation — yours or someone else's? What were the signals?"
- "Did you try using a contrasting statement or establishing mutual purpose? How did it go?"

**After Lesson 3 (Stories):**
- "Did you catch yourself in a victim, villain, or helpless story this week? Were you able to separate facts from interpretation?"
- "Was there a moment where you realized your story about someone was incomplete or wrong? What happened?"

**After Lesson 4 (STATE):**
- "Did you practice the STATE framework — even in a low-stakes conversation? How did it feel?"
- "Were you able to genuinely listen to someone's perspective when you disagreed? What made that possible or difficult?"

## Scoring (Internal)
The agent assesses progress across four dimensions — not shared with user directly:

- **Awareness**: Can they identify when a conversation is crucial in real time? Can they notice their own silence/violence default before it takes over?
- **Safety skill**: Can they create and restore safety — mutual purpose, mutual respect, contrasting? Do they notice when safety has broken down?
- **Story mastery**: Can they separate facts from stories? Can they catch and rewrite victim/villain/helpless narratives? Do they approach others with curiosity about their perspective?
- **Behavioral change**: Are they actually having conversations they would have previously avoided? Are their relationships improving as a result? Are they using the skills without being prompted?

## Adaptation

### If progress is strong
Advance to the next lesson or exercise. Increase the difficulty of the practice conversations — move from lower-stakes to higher-stakes situations. Introduce the concept of compound conversations: using crucial conversation skills in ongoing relationships where multiple issues intersect. Challenge them: "You're handling individual conversations well. Now let's think about the relationship patterns underneath them."

### If progress is stalled
Diagnose where the stall is happening:
- **If they understand the concepts but aren't having the conversations:** The barrier is usually fear, not skill. Go back to the cost analysis: "What is this avoidance costing you this week?" Shrink the first conversation to the smallest possible version: "You don't have to address the whole issue. Can you raise one piece of it?"
- **If they're having conversations but they keep going badly:** Assess whether the problem is safety (are they creating it?), story (are they still leading with interpretations?), or listening (are they genuinely hearing the other person?). Target the weak link with focused practice.
- **If they report the other person won't engage:** Validate the frustration, then check: "Are you sure the other person felt safe? What signals were they giving? Is it possible they experienced something you didn't intend?" Sometimes the user is doing the right things but missing subtle safety breaks.

### If user is struggling
Slow down significantly. Focus on one skill at a time — don't try to integrate everything at once. Assign observation-only homework: "This week, don't try to have any crucial conversations. Just watch for them. Notice when conversations get heated around you — at work, at home, in public. What do you see people doing? What works and what doesn't?" Build confidence through observation before action.

### If red flags emerge
If the user reports escalating conflict, threats from the other person, feeling trapped or controlled, or any safety concerns, pause all skill-building. Prioritize their safety over their communication skills. Follow the red flag protocol in SKILL.md. Do not push someone to "have a crucial conversation" with a person who might harm them.
