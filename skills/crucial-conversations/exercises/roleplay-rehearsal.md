# Exercise: Conversation Rehearsal

## Type
roleplay

## When to Use
After Lesson 3 (Mastering Your Story) or Lesson 4 (Speaking and Listening). Use when the user has a specific upcoming conversation and wants to practice it before doing it for real. This is the highest-value exercise in the skill — practicing with words they'll actually use.

## Safety Preamble
Before beginning, say: "We're going to rehearse your conversation. I'll play the other person so you can practice what you'd actually say and how you'd handle their likely responses. Two important things: First, this is practice, not performance. You'll stumble, rephrase, try different approaches — that's the point. Second, if at any point this starts to feel overwhelming or triggering — like it's pulling up something bigger than just this conversation — tell me and we stop immediately. This is meant to build confidence, not create distress. OK?"

**Red flag check before starting:** If the conversation involves a partner, family member, or authority figure, briefly assess safety: "Before we rehearse, I want to make sure: when you imagine having this conversation for real, do you feel safe? Is there any risk that the other person could respond with threats, intimidation, or harm?" If indicators of abuse or danger emerge, do NOT rehearse — follow the red flag protocol in SKILL.md.

## Setup
Agent says: "Here's how this works. You tell me about the person and the situation. I'll play the other person — as realistically as I can. You practice opening the conversation using what we've learned: facts first, then your story, then asking for their path. I'll respond the way the other person is likely to respond. We'll pause along the way to debrief what's working and what's not. Think of this like a flight simulator — better to practice here than to crash for real."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Brief the agent
Agent says: "Tell me everything I need to know to play this person. What's their personality like? How do they typically react under pressure — do they get defensive, shut down, attack, deflect? What's their relationship to you — boss, partner, friend, parent? And what's the specific topic you need to discuss? The more detail you give me, the more realistic I can make this."

{Wait for user response. Ask follow-up questions to understand:}
- The other person's communication style
- Their likely concerns and fears
- What they might say to push back
- The history between them on this topic
- What outcome the user is hoping for

### Step 2: Set the opening line
Agent says: "Now let's design your opening. Remember the framework: establish mutual purpose, share facts, tell your story tentatively, and ask for their perspective. What are you going to say first? Give me the actual words — not a summary of what you want to communicate, but the words that would come out of your mouth."

{Wait for user response}

**Guidance for the agent:** Before starting the roleplay, coach the opening. Common issues to fix:
- Starting with the story instead of facts: "You jumped straight to your interpretation. Let's start with what happened — the facts — before you share what you think it means."
- Starting with accusations: "If they hear that as their first line, where does their brain go? Defense mode. Can we reframe this so they feel you're on the same team?"
- Starting too tentatively: "I barely heard what you actually need to say. You softened it so much the message disappeared. Can you be clearer while still being respectful?"
- Missing the mutual purpose: "They don't know why you're bringing this up. Before you get into the issue, can you establish what you both want?"

### Step 3: Run the roleplay
Agent says: "OK, I'm going to be [the other person's name/role] now. Say your opening line as if you're saying it to them. I'll respond as they might. When we hit a tricky moment, I'll pause and we'll coach through it. Ready? Go."

{Switch into the other person's character. Respond realistically based on the briefing.}

**Guidance for the agent during roleplay:**

Phase 1 — Let them practice the opening (1-2 exchanges):
Play the other person realistically. If the other person would likely be defensive, be defensive. If they'd deflect, deflect. If they'd get quiet, get quiet. Don't make it easy — the user needs to practice handling real reactions.

Phase 2 — Pause and coach (after the first tricky moment):
Step out of character. Say: "Let's pause here. [Name what just happened.] They got defensive when you said X. What could you have done differently? Remember — when safety breaks, step out of content and restore safety. What would a contrasting statement sound like right now? Try it."

{Wait for user to try a different approach}

Say: "Good. Let's rewind and try that section again." Resume the roleplay from the point where it went off track.

Phase 3 — Continue and escalate:
Resume the roleplay, this time introducing the other person's most likely difficult response. Common patterns to simulate:
- **The deflector:** "Oh so now I'm the problem? What about when YOU did [counterattack]?"
- **The shutdown:** Long silence, minimal responses, "Fine. Whatever you say."
- **The emotional escalation:** Raised voice, tears, "I can't believe you're bringing this up now."
- **The rationalizer:** "That's not what happened. You're remembering it wrong."

After each difficult moment, pause and coach: "What just happened? What are your options right now? How do you restore safety?"

Phase 4 — Practice the landing:
After navigating the difficult section, guide them toward closing. Say: "OK, we're in the home stretch. You've shared your perspective, you've heard theirs. How do you move toward a shared understanding or a concrete next step? What would you say to wrap this up in a way that both of you walk away feeling heard?"

### Step 4: Full run-through
Agent says: "Now let's do it from the top, straight through, no stopping. You've practiced the hard parts. Now run the whole conversation as if it were real. I'll stay in character the whole time. Ready?"

{Run the full roleplay without interruption. Let the user apply everything they've practiced.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user freezes during the roleplay: Step out of character immediately. "Take a breath. You're practicing, not performing. What's happening right now — what are you feeling? If you were watching someone else in this situation, what would you tell them to say?" Help them re-enter with lower pressure.
- If the user reverts to their old pattern (silence or violence): Pause. "I noticed something just happened. You went to [silence/violence] right there. You started [masking/avoiding/controlling/labeling]. That's the old pattern. Can you try that moment again, but this time stay in dialogue? What would the new version sound like?"
- If the user gets frustrated that it's hard: "Of course it's hard. If this conversation were easy, you would have had it already. The fact that you're struggling in practice means you're actually engaging with the difficulty rather than avoiding it. That's progress. Let's keep going."
- If the user nails it: "That was strong. Did you feel the difference? You stayed curious, you kept the safety high, and you said what needed to be said. How did that feel compared to how this conversation usually plays out in your head?"
- If the user becomes emotionally distressed or triggered: Step out of character immediately. "Let's stop the roleplay. What you're feeling right now is important. Sometimes rehearsing a conversation brings up the underlying pain that made it crucial in the first place. Are you OK? Do you want to continue, take a break, or talk about what came up?" If the distress seems connected to trauma, follow the red flag protocol.

## Debrief Questions
1. "What was the hardest moment in the rehearsal? What triggered you, and how did you handle it?"
2. "Did anything surprise you about how the other person might respond? Did hearing it out loud change your expectations?"
3. "On a scale of 1-10, how ready do you feel to have this conversation for real? What would move you one point higher?"
4. "What's the one thing you want to make sure you do when you have this conversation? And what's the one thing you want to make sure you DON'T do?"

## Real-World Action
"You've rehearsed. Now schedule the conversation. Pick a specific time and place within the next week. Choose a time when neither of you is rushed, stressed, or distracted. If possible, choose a private, comfortable setting. Before you walk in, review your opening line, your mutual purpose statement, and your contrasting statement. After the conversation, debrief with yourself: what worked, what didn't, where did safety break, and how did you recover?"

## Report Back
"Next session, I want the full debrief. Tell me: Did you have the conversation? What happened? Where did it go well? Where did it go off script? How did the other person respond — was it what you expected? How do you feel about it now? And most importantly — was the pool of shared meaning fuller at the end than it was at the beginning? Even if the conversation wasn't perfect, did both people get heard? That's the measure of success."
