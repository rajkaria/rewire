# Exercise: Pre-Conversation Preparation

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (Speaking and Listening). Use when the user is preparing for a specific crucial conversation and needs a structured ritual for getting ready. This exercise builds a reusable preparation template they can apply to any crucial conversation going forward.

## Setup
Agent says: "You've learned the skills. Now we're going to build your pre-game ritual — a structured preparation process you can use before any crucial conversation, for the rest of your life. Think of this like an athlete's warm-up routine. They don't wing it before a big game, and you shouldn't wing it before a big conversation. By the end of this exercise, you'll have a preparation template that's customized to how your mind works and the specific challenges you face in dialogue."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Choose the conversation
Agent says: "First, pick the conversation you're preparing for. It can be the one we've been working on, or a new one. Tell me: who is it with, what's the topic, and when do you plan to have it?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 2: Master your story FIRST
Agent says: "Before you plan what to say, you need to examine what you're bringing into the room. Your internal state will shape everything. Let's clean up your story."

Walk through each question:
- "What are the raw facts — only what a camera would record?"
- "What is the story you've been telling yourself about these facts? Don't censor it — let the unfiltered version out."
- "Is there a victim element in your story? Are you casting yourself as purely innocent?"
- "Is there a villain element? Are you reducing the other person to a one-dimensional bad actor?"
- "Is there a helpless element? Are you telling yourself there's nothing to be done?"
- "What's your role in this situation? What did you do — or not do — that contributed?"
- "What would a reasonable, rational, decent person have to believe to behave the way the other person did?"

{Wait for user response to each question}

Agent says: "Now rewrite the story. Same facts, but this time include your role, humanize the other person, and open up possibilities. What's the more useful version?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 3: Define your purpose and desired outcome
Agent says: "Now let's get clear on what you actually want. Three levels:"

- "What do you want for yourself from this conversation?"
- "What do you want for the other person?"
- "What do you want for the relationship between you?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Now — what's the mutual purpose? What do you both care about that can serve as common ground? This is what you'll lead with."

{Wait for user response}

### Step 4: Design your opening
Agent says: "Let's write your opening. This is the first 30 seconds of the conversation — the most important 30 seconds. It needs to accomplish three things: establish mutual purpose, share the facts, and signal that you want dialogue, not a fight."

Walk through the STATE framework for their specific situation:
- "What facts will you share? Give me the specific words."
- "How will you tell your story — tentatively, owned as your perspective?"
- "How will you ask for their path? What's your specific question?"
- "Do you need a contrasting statement? What might they misunderstand, and how will you address it?"

{Wait for user response to each. Refine collaboratively.}

Agent says: "Say your full opening out loud or type it as if you're saying it to them. All the way through."

{Wait for user response. Coach on specifics — tone, word choice, tentative language, safety.}

### Step 5: Anticipate their response
Agent says: "Now let's war-game. Based on what you know about this person, what are the three most likely ways they'll respond to your opening?"

{Wait for user response}

For each likely response, plan the user's move:
- "If they get defensive, what will you do? How will you restore safety?"
- "If they shut down, how will you draw them out?"
- "If they counterattack or deflect, how will you stay in dialogue without getting hooked?"
- "If they say something that surprises you or challenges your story, how will you stay curious instead of defensive?"

{Wait for user response to each}

### Step 6: Plan the environment
Agent says: "The last piece is logistics. Small things matter."
- "When will you have this conversation? Pick a specific day and time."
- "Where? Choose a private, comfortable space where neither of you will be interrupted."
- "How much time do you need? Block enough so you're not rushed."
- "What's your physical state going to be? Avoid scheduling it when you'll be hungry, exhausted, or already stressed."
- "Is there anything you need to remove? Phone notifications, distractions, other people who might interrupt?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 7: Build the one-page prep sheet
Agent says: "Let's distill everything into a one-page prep sheet you can review right before the conversation. I'll help you format it. Here are the sections:"

Walk through and help them write each section:
1. **The Facts** — 3-5 bullet points of observable, verifiable facts
2. **My Story (Useful Version)** — 2-3 sentences of their honest, balanced interpretation
3. **Mutual Purpose** — One sentence: what we both want
4. **My Opening** — The exact words they'll use to start
5. **Contrasting Statement** — The "I don't want / I do want" backup
6. **Their Likely Response** — Top 2-3 expected reactions with planned responses
7. **My Commitment** — What they'll do if they feel themselves going to silence or violence ("If I notice myself shutting down, I'll..." or "If I notice myself getting aggressive, I'll...")

Agent says: "Write this down somewhere you can review in the five minutes before the conversation. Not to read from — to anchor yourself in. You want these ideas fresh in your mind so they're available when adrenaline hits."

## Branching Responses
- If the user is overplanning and trying to script every possible response: "I appreciate the thoroughness, but you can't script a conversation — only your half of it. And even your half will need to adapt in real time. What we're doing is preparing your foundation: your facts, your story, your purpose, and your opening. Everything after that is responsive. Trust the skills you've built."
- If the user is rushing through and not taking it seriously: "Slow down. The quality of your preparation directly predicts the quality of the conversation. Every minute you invest here saves you from a potential blow-up or shutdown during the real thing. Let's go back to the story piece — I don't think we've fully examined it yet."
- If the user realizes their story was mostly fiction: "That's a major insight. You were about to walk into a conversation armed with a story that wasn't based on solid facts. Imagine how differently the conversation will go now that you're leading with what actually happened instead of what you assumed happened."
- If the user gets anxious about the conversation as preparation makes it real: "Feeling anxious means you're taking this seriously — it means the conversation matters to you. That anxiety isn't a sign you shouldn't do it. It's a sign that what's at stake is worth the discomfort. The preparation we're doing right now is how you turn anxiety into readiness."
- If the user identifies safety concerns during prep that suggest abuse or danger: Stop the exercise. "What you're describing about how this person might respond raises a concern for me. If there's any possibility that having this conversation could put you in danger — physically, financially, or in terms of retaliation — then preparation skills aren't what you need. Your safety comes first. Can we talk about that before going further?" Follow the red flag protocol.

## Debrief Questions
1. "Do you feel more prepared now than you did an hour ago? What specific piece of preparation gives you the most confidence?"
2. "What was the most useful part of this process — mastering the story, designing the opening, or anticipating their response?"
3. "Is there anything that still feels unresolved or that you're worried about?"
4. "What's your plan if the conversation goes completely off-script? What's your north star — the one thing you'll hold onto no matter what?"

## Real-World Action
"You have your prep sheet. Here's your plan:
1. Review the prep sheet 5-10 minutes before the conversation.
2. Take three deep breaths before you walk in — activate your parasympathetic nervous system.
3. Have the conversation.
4. Immediately after, write down: what worked, what surprised you, what you'd do differently, and how full the shared pool of meaning was at the end.
5. Bring your notes to our next session.

The conversation won't go exactly as planned. That's not the point. The point is that you walk in grounded, prepared, and committed to dialogue — not to winning and not to avoiding."

## Report Back
"Next session, I want the full story. Walk me through what happened: how did you open, how did they respond, where did it get hard, how did you handle the hard parts, and how do you feel about where things landed? We'll celebrate what worked, learn from what didn't, and start building toward the next conversation on your list. This is how the skill becomes permanent — one real conversation at a time."
