# Exercise: Crucial Conversation Audit

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (What Makes It Crucial). Use when the user understands the concept of crucial conversations and their silence/violence default, and needs to map the specific conversations currently weighing on them.

## Setup
Agent says: "You understand what makes a conversation crucial — high stakes, strong emotions, differing opinions — and you know your default pattern when things get heated. Now we're going to take inventory. Every person walking around has a set of conversations they need to have but haven't. They carry these like invisible weights. Today we're going to make the invisible visible. We're going to map every crucial conversation currently in your life — not to overwhelm you, but to give you clarity on where the real work is."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Cast a wide net
Agent says: "I'm going to walk through different areas of your life, and for each one, I want you to tell me: is there a conversation you've been avoiding, dreading, or struggling with? Start with whatever comes to mind first."

Go through each domain:
- "Work or career — is there something you need to say to a boss, colleague, direct report, or client?"
- "Romantic relationship — is there an issue, concern, or unspoken truth between you and your partner?"
- "Family — is there a conversation with a parent, sibling, or extended family member that you keep putting off?"
- "Friendships — is there a friend you're frustrated with, worried about, or growing apart from?"
- "Your own behavior — is there a conversation you need to have with yourself about a pattern, a commitment, or a change you keep delaying?"

{Wait for responses to each domain. Capture specifics — who, what, how long.}

### Step 2: Rate the weight
Agent says: "Now let's prioritize. For each conversation you named, I want you to rate two things on a 1-10 scale. First: how important is this conversation for your life or this relationship — 10 being critically important? Second: how much dread or avoidance do you feel about it — 10 being maximum dread? Give me both numbers for each one."

{Wait for user response. Capture the ratings.}

### Step 3: Identify the cost of silence
Agent says: "Now the hard question. For each conversation you've been avoiding, I want you to name the cost. What is it costing you RIGHT NOW to not have this conversation? Think about: resentment building, trust eroding, your own self-respect, the relationship slowly deteriorating, opportunities lost, stress on your body. Be specific. What's the price of silence?"

{Wait for user response for each conversation.}

### Step 4: Identify your default pattern per conversation
Agent says: "Last step. For each conversation, identify which default pattern would likely show up if you tried to have it today — with no preparation, no skills. Would you go to silence — soften the message, avoid the real issue, withdraw? Or violence — push too hard, get accusatory, try to win? Some conversations might be one, some might be the other. Name it for each."

{Wait for user response.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user identifies many conversations (5+): "That's a lot of weight to carry. The good news is we don't need to tackle all of them. We just need to start with the one that would make the biggest difference — or the one that's most urgently needed. Which one, if you resolved it, would create the most relief or the biggest positive change in your life?"
- If the user identifies only one: "That one must be carrying a lot of weight. Let's go deep on it. What has kept you from having this conversation so far? What's the worst-case scenario you're imagining?"
- If the user struggles to identify any: "Sometimes the most important conversations are the ones we've buried so deep we don't even see them anymore. Let me ask differently: is there a relationship in your life that feels stuck, distant, or tense — where something feels off even if you can't name exactly what? Start there."
- If the user gets emotional identifying conversations: "That reaction tells us something. The weight of unspoken truth is heavier than most people realize. You've been carrying this. Naming it is the first step to setting it down — or more accurately, to transforming it from something you carry into something you address."
- If the user describes a situation that raises safety concerns (partner who is threatening or controlling): "Before we go further with this conversation, I want to ask something important. When you imagine having this conversation, are you afraid of how the other person might react — not just emotionally, but in terms of your safety? If there's any risk of retaliation, intimidation, or harm, that changes everything about how we approach this." Follow the red flag protocol in SKILL.md if domestic violence or abuse indicators emerge.

## Debrief Questions
1. "Looking at your full list — what pattern do you notice? Are most of your avoided conversations in one domain? Are they all with one type of person — authority figures, partners, peers?"
2. "What surprised you about the costs you named? Was the price of silence higher than you expected?"
3. "If you could wave a magic wand and resolve one conversation perfectly — everything goes well, everyone is heard, it works out — which one would you choose? Why that one?"
4. "What do you think having these conversations would make possible that isn't possible right now?"

## Real-World Action
"Choose one conversation from your audit — ideally the one that would create the most positive change with manageable risk. This is your 'practice conversation.' Over the next week, I want you to do three things: First, observe the other person. Watch for moments when they seem open, relaxed, and receptive versus closed, stressed, or defensive. Second, write down the facts of the situation — only what a camera would record, with no interpretation. Third, notice your own body when you think about having this conversation — where do you feel tension, and what story is driving it? You're not having the conversation yet. You're gathering intelligence. Bring everything you observe to our next session."

## Report Back
"Next session, I'll ask you: which conversation did you choose? What did you observe about the other person — when were they open versus closed? What are the plain facts? And what did you notice in your own body when you thought about having it? All of this becomes the raw material for our next step — making it safe."
