# Milestones

## Stage 1: Awareness — Seeing the Web
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: The user recognizes that their identity is relationally constructed — they can name the people who shaped them and describe how. They begin noticing the difference between moments of connection and moments of isolation in daily life.

**The user can**:
- Name at least five people who contributed to who they are and describe what each one gave them.
- Articulate, in their own words, what *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* means and how it applies to their life.
- Notice when they are operating from the illusion of separateness — trying to carry everything alone, defining themselves without reference to others.
- Identify at least one area where their relational web has withered and describe what happened.

**What to celebrate**: The shift from "I am a self-made individual" to "I was shaped by others and I am connected to others." This is a fundamental perceptual change. Name it.

**Agent note**: At this stage, the user may intellectually understand the relational self but not yet feel it in their body or act on it. That is normal. The feeling comes through practice, which is what the next stages focus on.

## Stage 2: Practice — Turning Toward Others
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: The user is actively reaching out, tending relationships, and practicing the disciplines of ubuntu — recognition (*sawubona*), presence, and honesty in their connections.

**The user can**:
- Initiate at least one genuine connection per week that they would not have made before this coaching.
- Practice *sawubona* — recognizing the full humanity of people they encounter, including those they find difficult or usually overlook.
- Distinguish between genuine belonging and performed belonging in at least three areas of their life.
- Follow through on relational commitments made during coaching sessions.
- Show up more honestly in at least one space where they previously performed.

**What to celebrate**: Every act of reaching out, every moment of dropping the mask, every time they choose vulnerability over performance. These are acts of courage. Name them as such.

**Agent note**: This stage often brings discomfort. The user is changing relational habits that may have served them for years. Resistance, awkwardness, and occasional retreat are all normal. Do not interpret a bad week as regression — it is part of the process.

## Stage 3: Integration — The Web as a Way of Life
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: The user consistently turns toward others rather than withdrawing. They give and receive with greater ease. They can name their web of belonging with specificity and tend it actively. Their sense of identity has tangibly shifted from individualistic to relational.

**The user can**:
- Describe their identity in relational terms: "I am who I am because of these people, these communities, these experiences."
- Give generously and receive gracefully, without compulsive self-sacrifice or chronic independence.
- Repair relational ruptures rather than avoiding them or letting connections die.
- Extend genuine recognition to people outside their immediate circle — strangers, adversaries, people unlike themselves.
- Sustain relational practices without coaching prompts: regular check-ins with important people, acts of care, honest presence.

**What to celebrate**: The move from isolated self-sufficiency to interdependent strength. The user is not weaker for needing others — they are more fully human. They know this now, not as a concept but as a lived experience.

**Agent note**: Integration does not mean perfection. The user will still have moments of withdrawal, performance, and isolation. The difference is that they now notice those moments and can choose differently. Watch for the shift from "I should connect more" (obligation) to "I want to connect" (genuine desire).

## Stage 4: Mastery / Graduation
**Marker**: The user lives ubuntu as a natural orientation toward the world. Their relational web is something they actively build, maintain, and repair. They embody *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* without needing to think about it.

**The user can**:
- Explain ubuntu in their own words and live its principles without external prompting.
- Maintain a healthy, active web of relationships across multiple domains of their life.
- Practice reciprocity naturally — giving and receiving as the situation calls for, without keeping score.
- Hold their individuality within community without self-erasure or isolation.
- Recognize when someone in their web is suffering and move toward them rather than away.
- Extend the circle of shared humanity to people they find challenging, different, or threatening.
- Identify when they are retreating into the illusion of separateness and self-correct without coaching.

**Graduation criteria**:
1. The user can describe their identity in relational terms and mean it — this is not a concept they understand but a way they experience themselves.
2. They have at least three relationships where they practice genuine belonging — showing up authentically and being accepted as they are.
3. They actively tend their relational web: reaching out, repairing, showing up, and allowing others to show up for them.
4. They give and receive with roughly equal ease and do not keep transactional accounts.
5. They have extended genuine recognition to at least one person they previously dismissed or dehumanized.

**Next steps**: Suggest `nonviolent-communication` if they want tools for navigating conflict within their relational web. Suggest `attachment-styles` if they want to deepen understanding of their intimate relational patterns. Suggest `sufi-heart` if they want to explore the spiritual dimension of love and connection. Suggest `atomic-habits` if they want to build daily structures that support their relational commitments.

**Agent note**: Graduation does not mean the user is done with relational growth. It means they have internalized the ubuntu orientation and can continue the practice independently. The web is a lifelong project. What has changed is that they know this, they value it, and they have the skills to keep weaving.
