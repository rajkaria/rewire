# Exercise: Radical Generosity Experiment

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (Giving and Receiving). Use when the user understands the ubuntu ecology of care and is ready to practice generosity as a relational discipline — not charity, but intentional participation in the web of mutual aid.

## Setup
Agent says: "We are going to run a one-week experiment. For seven days, you are going to practice intentional generosity — not grand gestures, not performative giving, but real, specific acts of care directed at the people in your web. The ubuntu concept is *ukusizana* — mutual aid. It is not about being a hero. It is about being a participant in the ecology of care that sustains everyone. You are going to give something every day this week and pay close attention to what happens — in you, in the other person, and in the relationship."

Agent says: "But here is the important part: this is not a kindness challenge from social media. We are not counting random acts of kindness. Every act of generosity this week should be directed at someone specific in your relational web — and it should be something that person actually needs, not something that makes you feel good to give. Real generosity requires seeing the other person clearly enough to know what would matter to them."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Identify seven recipients
Agent says: "Let's start by naming seven people from your relational web — one for each day. Mix it up: include someone from your inner circle, someone from your wider community, someone you have a complicated relationship with, and someone you tend to overlook. Who are your seven?"

{Wait for user response. Help them broaden beyond the obvious if needed.}

For each person, ask: "What do you think this person actually needs right now? Not what would be easy to give — what would genuinely land for them? Maybe it is your time. Maybe it is honest feedback. Maybe it is a meal. Maybe it is just being fully present the next time you see them. What would matter?"

### Step 2: Design the acts
Agent says: "Now let's make this concrete. For each of the seven days, you have a person and an act of generosity. Let's map them out. Day one — who and what?"

{Walk through each day. Help the user be specific. Challenge vague intentions like "I will be nicer to them." Push toward specifics: "I will call my sister and ask about her job interview without talking about myself." "I will bring coffee to my coworker who always looks exhausted on Wednesday mornings." "I will write a message to my old teacher telling them what they gave me."}

### Step 3: Set the observation frame
Agent says: "Here is what makes this an experiment and not just a to-do list. For each act of generosity, I want you to notice three things:

One — what happened in your body before you did it? Was there resistance, excitement, anxiety, avoidance? Name the feeling.

Two — how did the other person respond? Not what you wished they would do — what they actually did. Sometimes generosity lands powerfully. Sometimes it confuses people. Both are useful data.

Three — what shifted in the relationship afterward, even slightly? Did the space between you feel different? Did you see each other differently?"

### Step 4: Address the hard day
Agent says: "One more thing. I deliberately asked you to include someone you have a complicated relationship with. That day will be the hardest. Generosity toward someone who frustrates you, someone you resent, or someone you have been avoiding — that is where ubuntu really lives. It is easy to give to people you love. The practice deepens when you extend care across difficulty. Are you willing to try that?"

{Wait for response}

If the user resists: "I hear you. And I am not asking you to fake warmth or pretend resentment does not exist. I am asking you to find one genuine thing you can offer this person — even something small — that acknowledges their humanity without requiring you to like them. Can you think of what that might be?"

If the user is open to it: "Good. Pay special attention to that day. It will teach you the most."

## Branching Responses

- If the user says "I already do this — I am always giving": "Then let me redirect the experiment. For you, the radical act might be receiving. Replace three of your seven days with receiving practice: let someone do something for you without deflecting, minimizing, or immediately reciprocating. That is your edge."

- If the user feels overwhelmed by seven days: "We can start smaller. Pick three days. The principle is the same — intentional, specific generosity directed at someone who would genuinely benefit. Quality matters more than quantity. Which three people and acts feel most important?"

- If the user worries about being taken advantage of: "That is a valid concern and worth being honest about. Generosity within ubuntu is not about being a doormat. It is about freely choosing to participate in the web. If someone in your life consistently takes without giving, this experiment is not about serving them more. It is about strengthening the relationships where reciprocity already exists or could exist."

- If the user tries to give money as every act: "I appreciate the impulse, but let's dig deeper. Money is the easiest thing to give because it requires the least vulnerability. What about giving your time, your attention, your honesty, your presence? Those are the currencies that actually build relational connection. Can you redesign at least five of the seven acts as non-monetary?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "Which act of generosity felt most natural? Which felt most uncomfortable? What does that tell you about your relationship to giving?"
2. "How did people respond? Were there any reactions that surprised you — positive or negative?"
3. "Was there a moment when giving felt like it cost you something real — your time, your comfort, your ego? What happened?"
4. "Did the experiment change anything about how you see your relational web? Do any relationships feel different?"
5. "What happened on the hard day — the day with the complicated person? What did you learn?"

## Real-World Action
"The experiment is over, but the practice is not. Going forward, I want you to sustain one act of intentional generosity per week — not as a chore, but as a relational discipline. Pick one person each week and offer them something they actually need. Keep it specific. Keep it real. Over time, this practice rewires how you participate in your web."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I will ask you to walk me through the week — all seven days if you did them, or however many you completed. I want to hear not just what you did, but what you felt, what you noticed, and what shifted. Be honest. The acts that did not land or felt awkward are as valuable as the ones that felt beautiful."
