# Exercise: Relational Map

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (I Am Because We Are). Use when the user has begun to see their identity as relationally constructed and needs to map the actual web of relationships that sustains them — identifying where connection thrives and where it has withered.

## Setup
Agent says: "We are going to map your relational web — the network of people who shape your life and your sense of self. This is not a contacts list or a social media friends count. This is a map of the living connections that make you who you are. Some of these threads will be strong and humming with life. Others will be frayed or silent. Both are important to see."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Name the center
Agent says: "Start with yourself at the center. Now, I want you to think in concentric circles. The innermost circle is the people who know you most fully — the ones who have seen you at your worst and stayed. Not people you wish were close, but people who actually are. Who is in that innermost ring? Name them."

{Wait for user response. Do not rush. This can be emotionally charged. Some people will have many names. Some will have none.}

If the user names several people: "Good. These are your anchors — the people through whom your identity is most actively sustained. We will come back to them."

If the user struggles to name anyone: "That is important information, not a verdict. It means this innermost ring needs attention. It does not mean you are unworthy of closeness — it means the conditions for deep connection have not been built or maintained. That is something we can work with."

### Step 2: Expand the rings
Agent says: "Now, the next ring out. These are people you care about and who care about you, but with less depth or less frequency. Friends, family members, colleagues who matter to you. People you would miss if they disappeared. Name as many as feel real."

{Wait for response}

Agent says: "One more ring. These are the people in your wider community — acquaintances, neighbors, people you see regularly but do not know deeply. The barista who knows your order. The colleague you nod to. The parent from your child's school. These peripheral connections matter more than most people realize — they form the fabric of daily belonging. Who comes to mind?"

{Wait for response}

### Step 3: Identify the living and the withered
Agent says: "Now look at your whole map. I want you to notice two things. First: where are the connections that feel alive — where there is real exchange, real care, real seeing? Mark those. Second: where are the connections that have gone quiet — relationships that once mattered but have faded, people you have drifted from, threads you have stopped tending? Mark those too."

{Wait for response}

Agent says: "What do you notice about the pattern? Is your web rich and active, or has it thinned? Are the living connections clustered in one area of your life — say, work — while other areas have withered? Are there important people you have been neglecting?"

### Step 4: Name the gaps
Agent says: "Finally, I want you to look for what is missing. Is there a type of connection your web lacks entirely? Maybe you have friends but no one you can be fully vulnerable with. Maybe you have family but no chosen community. Maybe you have deep relationships but no casual daily connections. What is the gap?"

{Wait for response}

## Branching Responses

- If the user has a rich, active web: "This is a genuine strength. But even rich webs need tending. Let me ask: are there any relationships here that you have been coasting on — assuming they will sustain themselves without active care? Ubuntu teaches that relationships are not static. They are alive, and they need feeding."

- If the user has a sparse web and seems sad about it: "I want to be direct with you: this is not a character flaw. Many things can thin a relational web — moving to a new place, a painful breakup, grief, depression, a culture that tells you self-sufficiency is the goal. The web can be rebuilt. It starts with one thread. Let's figure out which thread to start with."

- If the user has a sparse web and seems unbothered: "I hear you, and I want to respect your experience. But let me ask: is the independence a choice that serves you, or has it become a habit that protects you from the vulnerability of connection? Sometimes 'I do not need people' is a story we tell ourselves to avoid the risk of being let down."

- If the user discovers withered connections they care about: "Those withered threads are not dead — they are dormant. Most relationships can be revived with a single genuine act of reaching out. The question is: do you want to? Which of these dormant connections, if you brought it back to life, would make your web significantly stronger?"

- If the user becomes emotional: "Stay with this. What you are feeling right now — whether it is grief, loneliness, gratitude, or something you cannot name — is the emotional reality of your relational life becoming visible. Ubuntu says your well-being is inseparable from the health of your web. What this map shows you matters."

## Debrief Questions
1. "What surprised you about your map? Was your web richer or thinner than you expected?"
2. "Who is the most important person on your map that you have been neglecting? What would it take to tend that connection?"
3. "Is there a pattern in which connections thrive and which ones wither? What do the thriving ones have in common?"
4. "Looking at the whole map, what is the single most important thing you could do to strengthen your relational web?"

## Real-World Action
"Choose one withered connection — a relationship that once mattered but has gone quiet. This week, reach out to that person. Not with a grand gesture. With something honest: 'I have been thinking about you,' 'I miss our conversations,' 'I want you to know you mattered to me.' One thread, brought back to life. That is the assignment."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I will ask: who did you reach out to? What happened? How did it feel — for you and, if you know, for them? And looking at your map now, does it feel any different?"
