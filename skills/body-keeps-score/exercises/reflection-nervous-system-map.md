# Exercise: Nervous System Map

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (The Window of Tolerance). Use when the user has learned the three nervous system zones (hyperarousal, hypoarousal, and the window of tolerance) and is ready to map their own personal patterns. This exercise creates a personalized reference document for nervous system self-awareness.

## Safety Preamble

Before beginning, say: "This exercise asks you to look at your nervous system patterns -- the ways your body ramps up and shuts down. For most people, this is interesting and clarifying. But I want to be upfront: mapping these patterns can sometimes bring up the experiences that created them. We're NOT going to explore those experiences. We're just going to map what happens in your body and what triggers it. If at any point you find yourself sliding into a memory or emotional experience that feels bigger than a mapping exercise, we pause. No questions asked. We ground, we take care of you, and we move on. This is meant to be like drawing a weather map, not standing in the storm. Sound OK?"

{Wait for response. If the user expresses hesitation, say: "We can go slower than you think. We can also skip any part of this. You're in charge."}

## Setup

Agent says: "In our last session, you learned about the three zones: the Red Zone (revved up, hyperaroused), the Blue Zone (shut down, hypoaroused), and the Green Zone (your window of tolerance, where you function best). You started noticing which zone you're in during the day. Now we're going to create a personal nervous system map -- a reference guide specific to you. This map will help you recognize your patterns faster and respond to them more skillfully."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Map the Green Zone

Agent says: "Let's start with the good stuff -- your window of tolerance. I want you to describe what the Green Zone feels like for you, specifically in your body. Not 'I feel good' -- but what does 'good' feel like physically? Think about a recent time when you felt regulated, present, and capable. What was your body doing?"

{Wait for response. Help them get specific with body sensations:}

Prompts if they're stuck:
- "What was your breathing like? Deep, shallow, easy?"
- "Were your muscles tense or relaxed? Specifically where?"
- "What was your energy level? Calm and alert? Relaxed and easy?"
- "How did your stomach feel? Your chest? Your jaw?"

Agent says: "Good. Now, what conditions tend to keep you in the Green Zone? What helps you stay regulated? It might be sleep, routine, exercise, social connection, time in nature, a manageable workload. What are your Green Zone supports?"

{Wait for response. Record these.}

### Step 2: Map the Red Zone (Hyperarousal)

Agent says: "Now let's map your Red Zone. Think about a time in the past couple of weeks when you were clearly activated -- anxious, agitated, angry, restless, wired. Not the most extreme time ever, just a normal-ish episode. If you're willing to bring that to mind briefly -- just enough to remember, not enough to re-live it -- what was your body doing?"

{IMPORTANT: The agent is asking the user to recall a MILD recent example, not a traumatic one. If the user begins to describe something extreme, redirect: "Let's use a milder example for this mapping. Something stressful but not the worst thing ever. That way we can map the pattern without activating it."}

{Wait for response. Help them identify body signals:}

Prompts:
- "What happened to your heart rate? Your breathing?"
- "Where did tension show up? Jaw? Shoulders? Stomach? Chest?"
- "What did your thoughts do? Speed up? Loop? Catastrophize?"
- "What did you want to DO? Move? Flee? Fight? Hide?"
- "What was your body temperature like? Hot? Cold? Flushed?"

Agent says: "Now let's identify your Red Zone triggers. What typically pushes you from the Green Zone into the Red Zone? It could be situations, people, environments, times of day, or internal states. Give me 3-5 triggers."

{Wait for response. Record these.}

Agent says: "And what are the early warning signals? Before you're fully in the Red Zone, what are the first body signals that you're heading there? The ones that come before the full activation?"

{Wait for response. These early signals are the most valuable part of the map.}

### Step 3: Map the Blue Zone (Hypoarousal)

Agent says: "Now the Blue Zone. Think about a time recently when you shut down -- felt numb, flat, disconnected, foggy, unable to think or care. Again, not the worst ever, just a regular episode. If you can bring it to mind briefly, what was your body doing?"

{Same cautions as the Red Zone. Keep it mild.}

{Wait for response. Help them identify body signals:}

Prompts:
- "What happened to your energy? Did it drain away? Was it sudden or gradual?"
- "What did your muscles feel like? Heavy? Limp? Frozen?"
- "What happened to your thinking? Foggy? Blank? Slow?"
- "Did you feel present or checked out? Did the world feel real or distant?"
- "What did you want to do? Withdraw? Sleep? Disappear? Nothing?"

Agent says: "What are your Blue Zone triggers? What pushes you from the Green Zone into the Blue Zone?"

{Wait for response. Record these. Note that some triggers may overlap with Red Zone triggers -- some people go through Red on their way to Blue.}

Agent says: "And the early warning signals for the Blue Zone? What are the first signs your body is heading toward shutdown?"

{Wait for response.}

### Step 4: Map the Transitions

Agent says: "Here's the part that most people don't think about: the transitions between zones. Some people go straight from Red to Green (they calm down). Some people go from Red to Blue (they crash after being activated). Some people go from Blue to Red (they swing from shutdown to agitation). And some people bounce between Red and Blue without spending much time in Green at all."

Ask: "What's your typical pattern? When you leave the Green Zone, which direction do you usually go first -- Red or Blue? And then what happens next? Do you recover to Green, or do you swing to the other extreme?"

{Wait for response. Help them see their personal cycle. If they describe a pattern of bouncing between Red and Blue with little Green: "That pattern is really common, and it's exhausting. Your nervous system is swinging between overdrive and shutdown. The grounding practices we'll build in Lesson 3 are designed to help with this. And if the pattern is really persistent, a Somatic Experiencing therapist can work with the nervous system directly to widen your Green Zone."}

### Step 5: Compile the Map

Agent says: "Let's put it all together. Here's your Nervous System Map:

**Green Zone (Window of Tolerance)**
- Body feels: [their description]
- Supports that keep you here: [their list]

**Red Zone (Hyperarousal)**
- Body signals: [their description]
- Triggers: [their list]
- Early warning signs: [their list]

**Blue Zone (Hypoarousal)**
- Body signals: [their description]
- Triggers: [their list]
- Early warning signs: [their list]

**Transition Pattern**: [their pattern description]

Does this feel accurate? Anything you'd add or change?"

{Wait for response. Adjust as needed.}

## Branching Responses

- If the user cannot identify Green Zone experiences: "If the Green Zone feels hard to recall, that's really important information. It might mean your nervous system doesn't spend much time there, which is worth exploring with a therapist. For now, can you describe what you imagine the Green Zone would feel like? Or a time in your life, even years ago, when you felt regulated and present? We'll use that as our reference point."

- If the user becomes activated while mapping the Red Zone: "I can sense that describing the Red Zone is bringing some of it up for you right now. Let's pause the mapping. Feet on the floor. Look around the room. You're here, right now, doing an exercise. The Red Zone experience you were describing is not happening right now. Take a breath. Good. We can come back to this or leave it for today -- your call."

- If the user says their Blue Zone is their "normal state": "Thank you for being honest about that. If shutdown or numbness feels like your baseline, that's your nervous system telling you something significant. Body awareness coaching can help you recognize the pattern, but widening the window -- moving your baseline toward the Green Zone -- is work that a therapist can support much more effectively. Would you be open to exploring that possibility?"

- If the user identifies very extreme patterns (frequent dissociation, panic attacks, complete shutdown lasting days): "What you're describing sounds like your nervous system is working very hard to protect you, and it's doing so in intense ways. This kind of pattern really benefits from professional support -- not because something is wrong with you, but because your system needs more than a mapping exercise can provide. A therapist trained in Somatic Experiencing or EMDR can work directly with your nervous system to help it find more regulation. I'd really encourage you to look into that. In the meantime, this map is still useful -- it helps you recognize what's happening, even if you need more tools to respond to it."

## Debrief Questions

1. "Looking at your map, what surprises you? Was there anything about your patterns you hadn't noticed before?"
2. "Which early warning signal do you think you're most likely to catch in real time? That's your 'canary in the coal mine.'"
3. "Which zone do you spend the most time in? Is that different from what you would have guessed before this exercise?"
4. "Is there a trigger that you can do something about -- a situation you could modify, an environment you could change, a pattern you could interrupt?"

## Real-World Action

"Keep your Nervous System Map somewhere accessible -- your phone, a notebook, wherever you'll actually see it. This week, practice checking in with the map 2-3 times a day: 'What zone am I in right now? What are the body signals telling me?' If you catch an early warning signal -- great. You don't have to do anything about it yet. Just notice. 'Ah, my jaw is clenching. I'm heading toward the Red Zone.' Noticing is the first step. In our next session, we'll build the tools to respond."

## Report Back

"When we reconnect, I'll ask: how accurate was your map in real life? Did the triggers you identified actually push you out of the Green Zone? Did you catch any early warning signals before you were fully in the Red or Blue Zone? And was there anything your map missed -- a pattern or trigger you didn't think of during the exercise? This is a living document. We'll keep refining it."
