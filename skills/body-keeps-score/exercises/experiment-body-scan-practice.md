# Exercise: Body Scan Practice

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (The Body Remembers). Use when the user has had their first experience of body awareness and is ready to develop a daily body scan habit. This is a 7-day experiment in gentle, non-pressured body listening.

## Safety Preamble

Before beginning, say: "This exercise sets up a daily body scan practice for the coming week. I want to be really clear about something before we start: this is meant to feel gentle and curious. Like checking in with a friend, not interrogating a witness. At every step, you have full permission to stop, skip, modify, or abandon the practice entirely. If the body scan ever brings up something intense -- a wave of emotion, a memory, a feeling of being overwhelmed or disconnected -- your only job is to stop the scan and take care of yourself. That might mean opening your eyes, pressing your feet into the floor, getting a glass of water, or calling someone you trust. It does NOT mean pushing through. This practice builds slowly, and going slow is the point. Ready to design your practice, or do you have questions first?"

{Wait for response. Address any concerns before proceeding.}

## Setup

Agent says: "In Lesson 1, you did a brief 60-second body scan -- checking in with each region of your body and noticing what was there. This week, we're going to turn that into a daily experiment. We'll design a scan that fits your life, set a time for it, and then you'll try it for seven days and report back on what you noticed. The goal is not perfection. The goal is repetition -- showing up to listen to your body, even briefly, every day."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Choose the length

Agent says: "First, let's decide how long your daily scan will be. I'd recommend starting small. Options:

- **30 seconds**: Just feet, belly, shoulders. Three checkpoints. Good if you're very busy or body awareness feels new and uncomfortable.
- **60 seconds**: Feet, legs, belly, chest, shoulders, face. The version we did in our session. Good middle ground.
- **3 minutes**: Same regions but with more time at each one. Allows for deeper listening. Good if you're already somewhat body-aware.
- **5 minutes**: Full scan with the 'what do you need?' question at each region. This is the extended version from Lesson 4. Only if you're comfortable with body awareness.

Which length feels right for a daily practice? Remember, we can always adjust after the first few days. Smaller is usually better at the start."

{Wait for response. If the user chooses 5 minutes on the first try, gently suggest starting shorter: "That's great ambition. But for the experiment to work, we want it to be so easy you can't skip it. What about starting at 60 seconds and building up? If 60 seconds feels too easy after a few days, you can extend it."}

### Step 2: Choose the time

Agent says: "When will you do your daily scan? Pick a time that's tied to something you already do -- an existing habit or a predictable moment in your day. Some options people find work well:

- Right after waking up, still in bed
- While your coffee or tea brews
- At your desk before starting work
- During a lunch break
- Right before bed

The best time is the one you'll actually do. Not the most 'optimal' time -- the most realistic time. When works for you?"

{Wait for response. Help them be realistic. If they say "6 AM when I wake up" but also mention they're not a morning person, gently challenge: "Will you actually do it at 6 AM, or will you hit snooze? Let's find the time that works with your actual life, not your ideal life."}

### Step 3: Walk through the scan together

Agent says: "Let's do the scan together right now so you have the sequence fresh. This way, when you do it on your own, you'll know exactly what to do. Remember: you can stop at any point. There's no wrong way to do this."

{Guide the user through the body scan at their chosen length. Use the same approach as Lesson 1, with opt-outs at each step:}

Say: "If you're ready, let's begin. You can close your eyes or keep them softly focused -- whatever feels safest."

**For the 60-second version:**

Say: "Bring your attention to your feet. Just notice. Pressure, temperature, sensation, nothing. All valid. ...

If it feels OK, move to your legs. What's there? Tension, ease, weight, lightness? ...

Now your belly. This area often registers emotion before you're aware of it. What do you notice? If anything uncomfortable comes up, just note it and move on -- or stop entirely. ...

Your chest. Is it open or tight? Moving with your breath? ...

Your shoulders and neck. Where a lot of people carry the weight of the day. What's there? ...

And your face -- jaw, eyes, forehead. Any holding? ...

Take a breath. Open your eyes. That's your daily practice."

{Debrief: "What was that like? Anything different from the first time we did it?"}

### Step 4: Plan for when it goes wrong

Agent says: "Here's the realistic part. You will probably miss some days. That's not failure -- that's human. I want you to plan for it now:

- If you miss a day, what will you tell yourself? Not what your inner critic will say, but what you'd want to hear. Maybe: 'I missed today. I'll try again tomorrow. No big deal.'
- If the scan brings up something uncomfortable, what will you do? I'd suggest: open your eyes, press your feet into the floor, and get a drink of water. Then decide if you want to try again or leave it for today.
- If you forget three days in a row, what will you do to restart? Maybe a phone reminder, or tying it more firmly to an existing habit."

{Wait for response at each point. Help them build a compassionate contingency plan.}

### Step 5: Set up the safety boundary

Agent says: "Last piece, and this is important. I want you to define a clear signal that means 'stop the scan and take care of yourself.' This is your safety boundary for the week. What would tell you that the body scan has crossed from 'gentle and curious' to 'too much'? It might be: unexpected crying, feeling disconnected from reality, a sudden wave of fear, feeling like you can't move, or a memory that intrudes. What would your signal be?"

{Wait for response. Help them define it concretely.}

Agent says: "Good. If that signal appears during any scan this week, your practice is not to push through. Your practice is to stop, ground (feet on floor, eyes open, name what you see), and take care of yourself. And if it happens more than once, please tell me about it next time -- because it might mean your body is asking for support that goes beyond daily practice. A trauma-informed therapist -- Somatic Experiencing, EMDR -- is the right partner for that kind of work."

## Branching Responses

- If the user says "I tried the scan and felt nothing all week": "That's completely fine and actually common, especially at the beginning. 'Nothing' is data. It means the channel is still warming up, or it means your body was genuinely settled during those scans. Keep going -- the sensitivity develops over time. Did you notice anything different in your body during the rest of the day, even outside the scan?"

- If the user says "The scan made me anxious": "Thank you for telling me that. Let's adjust. We can shorten the scan, keep your eyes open the whole time, or focus only on your feet and hands -- the safest, most peripheral parts of the body. The belly and chest are where activation is most likely, so we can skip those for now. And if even the modified version feels activating, it's genuinely worth talking to a therapist about. Anxiety during body awareness is not a failure -- it's important information."

- If the user says "I cried during the scan": "First, are you OK right now? Good. Tears during a body scan are not uncommon. Sometimes when we stop and listen, the body expresses things it's been holding. If it was a gentle release and you felt better afterward, that can be fine. But if it felt overwhelming, or if it keeps happening, that's your body saying it needs more than a daily check-in -- it needs someone who can sit with whatever is underneath those tears. A therapist who works with the body would be ideal. How did the crying feel to you -- was it releasing, or was it overwhelming?"

- If the user says "I don't want to do the scan anymore": "That's completely OK. You're not required to do this. If the scan doesn't feel right, we can find another way to build body awareness -- maybe through movement, or grounding practices, or just the nervous system zone check from Lesson 2. And if the resistance is because the scan brings up difficult things, that's worth naming and perhaps exploring with a professional. What feels right to you?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "How many of the seven days did you do the scan? No judgment -- just the number."
2. "What did you notice most consistently? Was there a body region that always had something to report?"
3. "Did your experience change over the week? Did the scan feel different on day 7 compared to day 1?"
4. "Were there any moments during the week -- outside the scan -- where you noticed your body speaking to you because you'd been practicing?"
5. "Did your safety boundary get triggered at any point? If so, what happened?"

## Real-World Action
"Continue the daily scan for another week if it felt valuable. If you want to extend the length, add 30 seconds at a time -- don't jump from 60 seconds to 5 minutes. If you found a body region that consistently held tension or sensation, pay extra attention to it this week. And start to notice: does the information from your body scan affect anything in your day? Does knowing that your shoulders are tight change how you approach a stressful meeting? Does noticing belly tension prompt you to slow down? That's where body awareness becomes body wisdom."

## Report Back
"Next time, I'll ask: did the daily scan become easier, harder, or the same? What's your body's 'headline' from the week? And was there a single moment where body awareness changed what you did -- not just what you noticed? That moment, if it happened, is the seed of everything we're building."
