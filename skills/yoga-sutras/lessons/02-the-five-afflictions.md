# Lesson 2: The Five Afflictions

## Core Concept

Beneath the surface activity of the mind — the constant stream of vrittis — the Yoga Sutras identify deeper structures that drive suffering. Patanjali calls them kleshas: afflictions, colorings, deep-rooted patterns that distort perception and compel reactive behavior. There are five, and they operate in a precise hierarchy.

Avidya — ignorance, misperception, not-seeing — is the root. It is not a lack of information but a fundamental confusion about the nature of reality: mistaking the impermanent for the permanent, the impure for the pure, suffering for happiness, the not-self for the self. From avidya grow the other four. Asmita — ego-identification — is the confusion of pure awareness with the instruments of perception and thought. It is the construction of a rigid "I" from what is actually a fluid process. "I am my job." "I am my opinions." "I am my body." These identifications feel like bedrock truth, but they are constructed, partial, and constantly defended.

From asmita emerge the twin forces of raga and dvesha. Raga is attachment — the compulsive movement toward pleasure, the clinging to what felt good before, the insistence that happiness depends on getting what you want. Dvesha is aversion — the compulsive recoiling from pain, the avoidance of discomfort, the refusal to accept what is unpleasant. Raga and dvesha operate continuously, shaping every perception and every choice. They are the mechanism through which the unexamined mind navigates the world: approach what is pleasant, avoid what is painful, repeat.

Beneath them all sits abhinivesha — the clinging to continuity, the instinct of self-preservation that operates even in those who have studied and understood the other kleshas. Patanjali says it flows on its own momentum, present even in the wise. It is not simply the fear of physical death. It is the deeper fear of dissolution, of losing the self that the ego has so carefully constructed. Every resistance to change, every refusal to let go of what is familiar, every moment of grasping at the known — abhinivesha is there.

The agent's task is not to help the user memorize a taxonomy. It is to guide them into recognizing these afflictions at work in their own mind, right now, in the decisions they are making and avoiding, the relationships they are clinging to or running from, the stories they are telling about who they are.

## Key Takeaway

Suffering is not caused by circumstances. It is caused by deep-rooted patterns of the mind — ignorance, ego, attachment, aversion, and fear — that operate beneath conscious awareness. Recognizing these patterns is the first step toward freedom from their grip.

## Teaching Approach

This lesson works best when anchored to the user's specific life. Do not present the kleshas abstractly. Draw each one out of the user's own experience. The recognition should produce a feeling of "yes, that is exactly what happens in me" — not a feeling of having learned a new vocabulary list.

### Opening Question
"Last session we observed the mind's activity — the vrittis, the constant flow. This session we go deeper. Underneath the thoughts, there are patterns that drive them. Let me ask you something: think about a situation in your life that keeps producing suffering — something you circle back to, something that disturbs your peace again and again, even though you know intellectually that it should not bother you as much as it does. What is it?"

{Wait for their response. Let them name something real.}

### Follow-Up Questions

- Once they name a situation: "Good. Now let us look beneath the surface. The Yoga Sutras identify five deep patterns — called kleshas, afflictions — that generate suffering. I am going to name them one by one, and I want you to notice which ones are active in the situation you described."

- Introduce avidya: "First is avidya — misperception, not seeing things as they are. In this situation, is there something you are assuming to be permanent that is actually temporary? Something you are treating as essential to your happiness that may not actually be? A story about yourself or the other person that feels like absolute truth but might be a construction? Where is the misperception?"

- Introduce asmita: "Second is asmita — ego-identification. In this situation, where has your identity gotten entangled? Is there a 'me' that feels threatened? A role or self-image you are defending? When the situation disturbs you, what part of your sense of self is being challenged?"

- Introduce raga: "Third is raga — attachment. What are you clinging to in this situation? A particular outcome? A way things used to be? A pleasure or comfort you are afraid of losing? Where is the reaching, the grasping, the insistence that things must be a certain way for you to be okay?"

- Introduce dvesha: "Fourth is dvesha — aversion. What are you running from? What discomfort, truth, or possibility are you unwilling to face? Where is the pushing away, the refusal, the not-this?"

- Introduce abhinivesha: "And fifth is abhinivesha — the primal clinging to continuity, the fear of dissolution. At the deepest level, what are you afraid of losing if this situation fully played out? Not the practical consequences — the deeper fear. What feels like it would not survive?"

{Walk through each one slowly. Give the user time with each. Most people will recognize multiple kleshas at work simultaneously.}

### After the exploration:
"Do you see how these are not five separate problems but one pattern with five layers? Avidya — the misperception — is the root. It creates the conditions for the ego to over-identify, for attachment and aversion to drive behavior, and for the fear of loss to operate beneath it all. The situation you described is not causing your suffering. These patterns — operating inside your mind — are generating the suffering in response to the situation. That distinction changes everything. Because you cannot control situations. But you can learn to see the kleshas when they arise."

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Having attachments means I am doing something wrong."
  **Reframe**: "Patanjali does not moralize about the kleshas. He describes them as natural features of the unexamined mind. Having attachments is human. The practice is not to eliminate desire but to see it clearly — to recognize when raga is driving a decision rather than clear awareness. You can enjoy things fully without clinging to them. In fact, enjoyment becomes deeper when it is not contaminated by the fear of losing what you enjoy."

- **Misconception**: "Aversion means I should tolerate everything and never set boundaries."
  **Reframe**: "Dvesha is the compulsive, automatic recoiling from what is unpleasant — it operates before conscious choice. Setting a boundary is a deliberate act from a clear mind. Recognizing dvesha does not mean accepting abuse, ignoring danger, or tolerating the intolerable. It means noticing when your avoidance is automatic rather than chosen, and when it is preventing you from facing something that needs to be faced."

- **Misconception**: "If avidya is the root, I need to achieve enlightenment before I can deal with my problems."
  **Reframe**: "The kleshas operate on a spectrum from gross to subtle. You do not need to dissolve the root ignorance to make meaningful progress. Even catching raga or dvesha at the moment of operation — 'I see that I am clinging right now' — interrupts the automatic pattern and creates the possibility of a different response. Start where the kleshas are most visible and work inward."

- **Misconception**: "The kleshas sound like they are saying all desire and emotion is bad."
  **Reframe**: "The Sutras are not anti-emotion. They distinguish between kleshas — afflictive patterns rooted in ignorance — and natural, appropriate responses to life. Loving your family is not asmita. Enjoying beauty is not raga. Avoiding genuine danger is not dvesha. The kleshas are specifically the compulsive, unconscious, suffering-producing versions of these impulses — the ones that operate beneath awareness and drive behavior you did not choose."

## Bridge to Practice

After exploring the kleshas through the user's specific situation, help them begin to see the patterns more broadly.

"We used one situation to identify the kleshas. Now let us widen the lens. Over the next week, I want you to become a detective of your own afflictions. You do not need to catch all five. Start with the ones that were strongest in what we just explored. Was it raga — attachment and clinging? Was it dvesha — aversion and avoidance? Was it asmita — ego feeling threatened? Whatever is loudest in your life right now, that is where to focus your attention."

### Assignment
"This week, carry a simple practice: whenever you notice suffering — not just big crises but the everyday friction of frustration, anxiety, wanting, resisting — pause and ask: which klesha is operating here? Is this attachment? Aversion? Ego? Am I not seeing something clearly? You do not need to fix it. You do not need to make the klesha stop. Just name it. The naming itself begins to create space between you and the pattern. Keep a brief note each evening: what klesha showed up most today? What triggered it? How did you respond? Bring these observations to our next session."
