# Exercise: Eight Limbs Integration

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 3 (The Eight-Limbed Path). Use when the user understands the overview of ashtanga yoga and is ready to design a personal practice that incorporates accessible elements of multiple limbs into their daily life. This exercise bridges understanding and implementation.

## Setup
Agent says: "You now have a map of the eight-limbed path — from how you treat others and yourself, through the care of body and breath, to the training of attention and the possibility of deep stillness. The map is clear. The question is: how do you walk it? Not all at once, not perfectly, but in a way that is real, sustainable, and rooted in your actual life. Today we are going to design your personal practice — not an ideal practice, but a practice that fits the person you are and the life you are living right now. Patanjali says: practice must be done for a long time, without interruption, with devotion. We need to build something you can sustain."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Audit the eight limbs
Agent says: "Let us start by taking an honest inventory of where you stand with each limb. I am going to name each one, and I want you to rate it — not as good or bad, but as: this is present in my life, this is partially present, or this is mostly absent. Be honest. The inventory is for you, not for anyone else.

Yamas — ethical commitments to the world:
- Ahimsa (non-harming): How do you treat others? How do you treat yourself? Is there carelessness or cruelty in your actions or speech?
- Satya (truthfulness): Do you speak honestly? Are you honest with yourself about what is real?
- Asteya (non-stealing): Do you take what is not offered — other people's time, energy, credit, attention?
- Brahmacharya (wise use of energy): Where does your energy go? Is it directed intentionally or scattered compulsively?
- Aparigraha (non-grasping): Are you accumulating more than you need — possessions, commitments, identities?

Niyamas — commitments to yourself:
- Shaucha (cleanliness): Is your environment clean? Your body cared for? Your mental inputs clean — or full of noise?
- Santosha (contentment): Can you be at peace with what is present, or is there a constant undercurrent of 'not enough'?
- Tapas (disciplined effort): Are you willing to do what is difficult in service of your growth?
- Svadhyaya (self-study): Do you regularly examine your own mind, patterns, and assumptions?
- Ishvara pranidhana (surrender): Can you let go of the need to control everything? Can you trust the process?

Asana: Do you have a physical practice that leaves your body stable, healthy, and capable of sitting still?

Pranayama: Do you work with your breath intentionally — using it to calm, energize, or steady yourself?

Pratyahara: Can you withdraw your attention from the pull of external stimulation when you choose to?

Dharana: Can you hold your attention on a single point for any sustained period?

Dhyana: Does your concentration ever become effortless — flowing naturally without the need to keep bringing attention back?

Samadhi: Have you experienced moments of complete absorption — where the boundary between self and experience dissolved?

Take your time with this. Where is each limb in your life?"

{Walk through each limb one by one. Let the user self-assess. Gently challenge where they may be over- or under-estimating. This audit should produce a clear picture of their strengths and gaps.}

### Step 2: Identify the foundation limb
Agent says: "Looking at your inventory, which limb feels like the foundation you need to strengthen first? This is not necessarily the weakest limb — it is the one that, if you developed it, would most support everything else. For some people, it is a yama — because inner friction from acting against their values is preventing any stillness. For others, it is asana or pranayama — because the body and breath need to be steady before the mind can settle. For others, it is dharana — because they have the ethical and physical foundation but have never trained their attention. Which limb, if you strengthened it, would create the most change across your whole life?"

{Wait for their response. Help them choose wisely — not the most appealing limb, but the most foundational for where they are.}

### Step 3: Design the daily practice
Agent says: "Now we build. A sustainable daily practice does not need to be long. It needs to be consistent and it needs to touch multiple dimensions. Here is the framework I want to use:

Morning (five to ten minutes total):
- One element of awareness from a yama or niyama — a quality to carry through the day
- A brief body and breath practice — even two minutes of conscious breathing or simple stretches
- Dharana practice — your sitting concentration

Evening (two to three minutes):
- A brief self-study reflection — what klesha arose today? Where did you act from awareness versus habit?

Let us fill in this framework with practices that work for you. What would the morning look like? Be specific — what will you actually do in those five to ten minutes, step by step?"

{Build the routine together. Make sure each element is concrete and time-bounded.}

For the yama/niyama element, help them choose one quality to focus on for the coming week: "Rather than trying to practice all five yamas and five niyamas, choose one that connects to the foundation limb you identified. If you chose satya, your morning practice might be a brief intention: 'Today I will notice when I am being dishonest — with others or with myself.' If you chose santosha, it might be: 'Today I will practice being at peace with what is present, without needing anything to change.' One quality, held with attention, changes more than ten qualities held vaguely."

For the body/breath element, scale to their capacity: "If you have an existing asana or exercise practice, this element might already be covered. If not, even two minutes of slow, conscious breathing counts as both asana (you are sitting steadily) and pranayama (you are regulating the breath). The goal is to arrive at your dharana practice with a body that is settled and a breath that is calm."

For the dharana element: "This is your core practice. Five minutes of single-pointed concentration — the practice you have been developing. Same object of focus, same committed attention."

For the evening element: "This does not need to be elaborate. Before sleep, ask yourself three questions: What klesha was most active today? Was there a moment where I acted from awareness rather than habit? What will I practice tomorrow? Write a few words if that helps, or simply reflect."

### Step 4: Address the obstacles
Agent says: "Now the realistic part. What is most likely to prevent you from doing this practice? Be honest. Is it time? Motivation? Forgetting? The feeling that it is not working? Name the top two obstacles."

{Wait for response.}

"Good. Now let us plan for each one.

If time is the obstacle: Where can the practice live in your existing routine? Can it attach to something you already do — right after waking, right after brushing your teeth, during your first moments of morning quiet? Practice does not need a special time slot. It needs a consistent trigger.

If motivation is the obstacle: Reduce the minimum. On the worst days — the days when you do not want to practice at all — what is the smallest version of this practice you would still be willing to do? Maybe it is two minutes of breathing. Maybe it is simply sitting for sixty seconds and setting an intention. Define the minimum now, before you need it. A practice you do for two minutes on a bad day is infinitely more valuable than a practice you skip entirely.

If forgetting is the obstacle: Anchor the practice to an existing habit. Put your cushion or chair in a visible place. Set a single gentle alarm. Leave a note where you will see it first thing. The mind will genuinely forget — not because you do not care, but because new habits have not yet wired themselves in. External cues help until the internal habit forms.

If the feeling that 'it is not working' is the obstacle: Define in advance what 'working' means. The mind will tell you it is not working after three days, then after a week, then after two weeks. This is a vritti, not an accurate assessment. Progress in dharana is subtle and cumulative. You will not feel dramatic shifts after a week. You will feel them after a month. Trust the process — that is ishvara pranidhana in action."

### Step 5: Write the commitment
Agent says: "Let us put this in writing. State your practice plan for the coming week — specifically what you will do, when, for how long, and what your minimum fallback is for difficult days. Saying it aloud or writing it down makes it real. It moves from intention to commitment."

{Wait for user response. Reflect it back to them clearly. This is their contract with their own practice.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user tries to design an overly ambitious practice: "I admire the ambition, and I want to protect it by making it sustainable. Patanjali says practice should be done without interruption. An ambitious practice that you abandon after ten days serves you less than a modest practice you maintain for months. Start with what you will actually do on your worst day, then let the practice grow from there. The tradition calls this approach krama — gradual, systematic progression."

- If the user says they do not know where to start: "Then start with the breath. Sit for five minutes each morning and observe the breath. That single practice touches asana (you are sitting steadily), pranayama (you are aware of the breath), pratyahara (you are withdrawing attention from external stimulation), and dharana (you are concentrating). Four limbs in one simple practice. Add one yama or niyama to carry as a daily intention, and you have five limbs active. The path does not require a complex beginning. It requires a sincere one."

- If the user is concerned about the spiritual dimensions: "You can practice every limb of the eight-limbed path without adopting any spiritual framework. The yamas and niyamas are ethical principles that any thoughtful person can work with. Asana and pranayama are physical and physiological practices. Dharana and dhyana are attention training. You can hold ishvara pranidhana as surrender to reality, to the nature of things, to what is larger than the individual ego — without any theological commitment. Practice sincerely and let your own experience determine what these principles mean to you."

- If the user already has a strong practice and wants to deepen it: "Then the question is not what to add but what to refine. Where are the gaps in your current practice? Most experienced practitioners have strong development in two or three limbs and underdeveloped others. Is your asana practice strong but your ethical self-examination weak? Is your dharana practice solid but your relationship with the yamas unexamined? The eight limbs are a diagnostic tool as much as a practice guide. Use them to find where your growth edge is."

## Debrief Questions
1. "How did the practice feel across the week? Was there a rhythm to it — good days and hard days? What influenced the difference?"
2. "Which limb felt most natural to practice? Which felt most challenging or awkward?"
3. "Did you notice any connection between the limbs — for example, that practicing a yama during the day affected the quality of your dharana in the morning? Or that steady concentration made ethical choices easier?"
4. "Looking at your plan now, after a week of living with it: what needs to change? What was too much? What was missing?"

## Real-World Action
"Continue the practice plan you designed for a second week. But this week, add one element: choose a specific relationship or situation in your daily life and deliberately bring the practice into it. If your yama focus is ahimsa, practice it specifically in a relationship where you tend to be careless. If your focus is dharana, bring concentrated attention to a task you usually do on autopilot. The Sutras describe yoga as skill in action — the practice is not something you do only on the cushion. It is how you move through the world. Choose one place to bring it alive this week."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I want to hear three things. First: how did the daily practice go — how many days, and what did the pattern look like? Second: what happened when you brought the practice into a specific relationship or situation? What changed — in you, in the interaction, in the quality of your attention? Third: which limb surprised you this week — either by being more powerful than you expected or more challenging? Those surprises are where the real learning lives."
