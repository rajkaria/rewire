# Exercise: Klesha Inventory

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (The Five Afflictions). Use when the user has been introduced to the five kleshas and needs to apply the framework systematically to their own inner life. This exercise turns abstract understanding into personal recognition.

## Setup
Agent says: "You now know the five kleshas — the afflictions that the Yoga Sutras identify as the root drivers of suffering. Avidya: misperception. Asmita: ego-identification. Raga: attachment. Dvesha: aversion. Abhinivesha: the deep clinging to continuity. You have seen glimpses of how they operate. Now we are going to do a thorough inventory — mapping which kleshas dominate your inner life, how they show up in specific patterns, and where they exert the most influence on your decisions and relationships. This is svadhyaya — self-study — in its most precise form."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Map the dominant klesha
Agent says: "Let us start with a broad question. Over the last month of your life — not the last hour, not a single bad day, but the general pattern — which of the five kleshas has been most active? I will name them again, and I want you to feel into which one resonates most, not which one you understand best intellectually.

Raga — attachment, clinging, the compulsive reaching toward what feels good, the inability to be at peace unless a particular desire is met. Is your mind constantly reaching for something — approval, comfort, a particular outcome, a person's attention?

Dvesha — aversion, pushing away, the refusal to face what is uncomfortable. Are you spending significant energy avoiding something — a conversation, a truth about yourself, a feeling, a responsibility?

Asmita — ego-identification, the defense of a self-image. Are you frequently feeling threatened, defensive, needing to prove yourself, comparing yourself to others, protecting a story about who you are?

Avidya — misperception, not seeing things as they are. Are you holding onto beliefs about a situation or a person that deep down you suspect are not accurate? Are you treating something temporary as permanent or something constructed as essential?

Abhinivesha — the deep fear of losing what you have, of change, of dissolution. Are you clinging to a situation, a relationship, a version of your life because the alternative feels like a kind of death — even if the current reality is not serving you?

Which one — or which two — feel most alive in you right now?"

{Wait for their response. Do not rush. This is a moment of honest self-assessment.}

### Step 2: Find the specific patterns
Agent says: "Good. Now let us get specific. For the klesha you named, I want you to give me three concrete examples from the last month. Not abstract descriptions — actual moments. When exactly did this klesha show up? What was the situation? What did the klesha make you do — or prevent you from doing? Be as specific as you can."

{Wait for user response. Walk through each example carefully. For each one, help them see the klesha operating beneath the surface behavior.}

For raga examples, ask: "What were you reaching for in that moment? And what was the belief underneath — what were you telling yourself would happen if you got it? Or if you did not?"

For dvesha examples, ask: "What were you avoiding? And what was the feared consequence if you had actually faced it? Was the avoidance protecting you from something real, or from a feeling you did not want to have?"

For asmita examples, ask: "What part of your identity felt threatened? What would it mean about you if you had let go of the defense? Who are you without that particular self-image?"

For avidya examples, ask: "What are you choosing not to see? If you allowed the clearer perception, what would change? What makes the misperception more comfortable than the truth?"

For abhinivesha examples, ask: "What are you afraid of losing? And underneath that — what would the loss mean? What part of you would not survive it?"

### Step 3: Trace the roots
Agent says: "Now let us look at the relationship between your dominant klesha and the root — avidya, the fundamental misperception. The Sutras say all the other kleshas grow from avidya. So underneath your attachment, or your aversion, or your ego-defense, there is a misperception — something you are taking to be true that is not entirely true. For the examples you shared, what is the avidya? What are you not seeing clearly?

Some common patterns: treating an impermanent situation as if it will last forever. Treating your emotional reaction as if it were the truth about the situation. Treating your self-image as if it were your actual self. Treating an outcome as if your peace of mind depended on it. What is the misperception underneath your pattern?"

{This is often the deepest and most transformative part of the exercise. Give it space. The user may need time and gentle questioning to reach the root misperception.}

### Step 4: The counter-practice
Agent says: "Patanjali does not just diagnose the kleshas — he prescribes a counter-practice. For the klesha patterns you have identified, let us find the specific practice that addresses them.

For raga: the practice is vairagya — non-attachment. Not renunciation of the thing you desire, but the loosening of the desperate grip. Can you want something without needing it? Can you move toward a goal without staking your inner peace on the outcome?

For dvesha: the practice is the willingness to face. Not forcing yourself into what you fear, but cultivating the capacity to stay present with discomfort rather than automatically fleeing.

For asmita: the practice is viveka — discernment, the ability to see the difference between who you truly are and the roles, stories, and identifications the ego has constructed.

For abhinivesha: the practice is recognizing that change is the nature of all experience, and that your deepest self — the awareness that witnesses all change — is not threatened by impermanence.

Which practice feels most relevant to you right now? What would it look like, specifically, in the situations you described?"

{Help them articulate a concrete practice, not an abstract intention.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user identifies strongly with all five kleshas: "That is honest and probably accurate — the kleshas operate together, not in isolation. But for practical purposes, choose the one that causes the most suffering right now. We start where the pain is sharpest because that is where attention is most available. Once you learn to see one klesha clearly, the skills transfer to the others."

- If the user resists identifying with any of the kleshas: "That resistance itself may be worth examining. Which klesha might be hiding behind the resistance? Sometimes asmita — the ego — protects itself by refusing to see its own patterns. Or dvesha — aversion — avoids the discomfort of honest self-examination. I am not saying you have a problem. I am saying these patterns are universal. Patanjali describes them as operating in all human minds. Where might they be operating in yours, even subtly?"

- If the user becomes emotional during the inventory: "What you are feeling right now is important. This kind of honest self-examination touches something real. Do not rush past the feeling to get to the analysis. Let yourself feel what is here. The fact that this moved you tells us exactly where the work is. Can you stay with this for a moment?"

- If the user intellectualizes the kleshas without personal connection: "You are describing the kleshas with precision, and I can see you understand the framework. Now I want to close the gap between understanding and recognizing. Close your eyes for a moment. Think about the last time you were genuinely upset — not mildly irritated, but genuinely disturbed. What was happening? Now, with what you know about the kleshas, which one was driving your reaction? Not in theory — in that moment, in your body, in your behavior. That is the level at which this needs to land."

## Debrief Questions
1. "Which klesha surprised you the most — was there one you did not expect to find so active in your life?"
2. "When you traced the pattern back to avidya — the root misperception — what did you discover? What are you not seeing clearly?"
3. "For the counter-practice you identified, does it feel possible? Not easy — possible. What would make it harder? What would make it easier?"
4. "How does it feel to see these patterns named and mapped? Does the naming itself change anything about your relationship to the pattern?"

## Real-World Action
"This week, your task is to become a field researcher of your own kleshas. Focus on the dominant klesha you identified today. Every time you catch it operating — the attachment pulling, the aversion pushing, the ego defending, the misperception distorting — make a brief note. Not a journal entry. Just: what happened, which klesha, and did you catch it before or after it drove your behavior? You are building a detailed map of how this pattern moves through your daily life. The more precisely you see it, the less power it has to operate invisibly."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I will ask: how many times this week did you catch your dominant klesha in action? Were there moments where you saw it coming before it fully took hold — where the recognition itself created a pause? And was there anything you discovered about the pattern that you did not see before? That discovery is svadhyaya at work — self-study changing the mind that is being studied."
