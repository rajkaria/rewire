# Exercise: Concentration Practice

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (Concentration and Stillness). Use when the user has experienced dharana in-session and is ready to establish a daily concentration practice. This exercise sets up a structured week-long experiment to build the muscle of single-pointed attention.

## Setup
Agent says: "In our last session, you practiced dharana — holding your attention on a single point. You felt what it is like when the mind settles, even briefly, and you experienced the constant pull of the vrittis trying to scatter your attention. Now we are going to turn that practice into a daily experiment. For one week, you will practice dharana every day, tracking what happens — not to judge yourself but to observe the mind's behavior with the same precision you would bring to any experiment. Abhyasa — sustained, dedicated practice — is what the Sutras say transforms the mind. This is where abhyasa begins."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Choose the object of focus
Agent says: "First, let us choose your object of concentration. In our session, you tried both breath focus and point focus. Patanjali is flexible about the object — what matters is that it holds your attention and that you stay with it consistently. Here are the most common options:

The breath — the physical sensation of air moving through the nostrils, the rise and fall of the chest or belly. This works well for most people because the breath is always available and has a natural rhythm that attention can rest on.

A mantra — a word or short phrase repeated silently with each exhale. In the yogic tradition, the sound 'Om' is considered the primal mantra. You could also use 'So Hum' (breathing in with 'So,' breathing out with 'Hum,' which means 'I am that'). Or any word that produces a feeling of steadiness in you.

A visual point — a spot between the eyebrows (the ajna point), the center of the chest (the hridaya, the spiritual heart), or an external point like a candle flame.

Based on what you experienced in our practice, which of these feels most natural? Which one gave your attention a stable resting place?"

{Wait for user response. If they are unsure, recommend starting with the breath — it is the most universally accessible.}

### Step 2: Set the parameters
Agent says: "Now let us set up the experiment so it is clear and sustainable.

Duration: five minutes per day. Not less, not more — for this first week. Five minutes is long enough to practice genuinely and short enough that you will not skip it.

Time of day: when will you practice? Morning is ideal — the mind is less cluttered, and you set the tone for the day. But any consistent time works. What time can you commit to every day this week, including the days when you are tired, busy, or not in the mood?

Place: where will you sit? Consistency helps. The same chair, the same corner, the same cushion — the mind begins to associate the place with the practice, and settling happens faster.

Posture: sit comfortably with your spine upright. You do not need a special cushion or position. A chair is fine. The only requirement is that your body is stable enough that it will not distract you and alert enough that you will not fall asleep.

Tell me your plan: what time, where, and what object of focus?"

{Wait for user response. Make sure the plan is concrete and realistic.}

### Step 3: Define the tracking method
Agent says: "Here is the experiment part. After each five-minute session, I want you to record three things. Keep it brief — thirty seconds of notation is enough.

First: the quality of the mind at the start. Was it agitated, restless, dull, foggy, scattered, or relatively calm? Use one or two words.

Second: an estimate of how many times attention wandered from the object. You will not get an exact count — that is fine. A rough sense: was it constant wandering (dozens of times), frequent (ten to twenty), moderate (five to ten), or occasional (a few)?

Third: was there a moment of genuine steadiness — even two or three seconds where attention was resting on the object without effort? If yes, describe what it felt like in a word or two. If no, that is fine — note that too.

This tracking is not about scoring yourself. It is data collection. You are studying the behavior of your own chitta the way a scientist studies a phenomenon — with curiosity, not judgment."

{Wait for user response. Confirm they understand the tracking method.}

### Step 4: Anticipate the resistance
Agent says: "Before we finish setting up, I want to prepare you for what will happen this week. The mind will resist this practice. Not dramatically — subtly. Here is what to expect:

Day one or two: novelty carries you. The practice feels interesting.

Day three or four: the mind produces reasons to skip. You are too busy. You forgot. You will do it later. You are not sure it is working. Recognize these as vrittis — fluctuations of the mind — doing exactly what vrittis do: pulling attention away from what you have chosen to focus on. The practice is not just what happens on the cushion. It is the moment you sit down despite the resistance.

Day five through seven: if you have been consistent, something begins to shift. The mind settles a little faster. The wandering may not decrease, but your recovery time — the speed at which you notice the wandering and return — improves. This is the first sign that the practice is working.

The most important instruction I can give you is this: do not evaluate the practice while you are doing it. Every session where you sit down and practice is a good session. Even the ones that feel terrible. Especially the ones that feel terrible — because those are the days when the discipline of abhyasa is being forged."

{Wait for user response.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user is already meditating daily: "Excellent. Then this is not about starting a new practice — it is about refining the one you have. For this week, I want to sharpen your approach. Use a single fixed object of focus — no switching — and track the wandering with more precision than you normally do. Many long-term meditators fall into a comfortable but unfocused sitting habit. This week, bring the intensity of dharana — the deliberate holding of attention — back into your practice. You may find it harder than your usual sit. That is the point."

- If the user says five minutes is too little: "I understand the impulse to do more. And I am asking you to resist it — for one week. There is a reason: I want every minute of your practice to be genuine dharana, not comfortable sitting with a wandering mind. It is harder to sustain full concentration for five minutes than to sit passively for twenty. If at the end of the week you have maintained five minutes of genuine focus every day, we will extend. Build the quality before building the duration."

- If the user says they cannot sit still: "Then we start with what you can do. Can you sit for two minutes? One minute? Can you stand? Can you walk very slowly and focus on the sensation of each step — walking dharana? The posture is secondary to the attention. Find a position where your body is stable enough that it is not the dominant experience, and practice there. We are training the mind, not the body."

- If the user misses a day and feels they have failed: "You have not failed. You have encountered one of the most universal patterns in practice: the gap between intention and execution. The question is not 'did I miss a day?' It is: 'what do I do now?' Patanjali says practice should be done for a long time, without interruption, and with devotion. Missing a day is not a failure of the practice — it is a moment where the practice of beginning again starts. Sit down today. That is the only relevant action."

- If the user reports that the practice is producing anxiety or agitation: "This sometimes happens, particularly in the early stages. When you deliberately still the mind, what was beneath the surface activity can rise up — anxiety, restlessness, emotion that was being suppressed by constant distraction. This is not a sign that the practice is failing. It is a sign that you are meeting what was always there. If the intensity feels manageable, stay with the practice and let the agitation arise and pass without engaging it. If it feels overwhelming or produces panic, stop the practice, open your eyes, feel your feet on the ground, and we will adjust the approach. Some minds need gentler entry points — perhaps a walking practice or a shorter duration."

## Debrief Questions
1. "How many of the seven days did you practice? What happened on the days you missed — what was the vritti that convinced you to skip?"
2. "Looking at your tracking notes across the week, do you see any pattern? Did the quality of the mind at the start change over the week? Did the frequency of wandering shift?"
3. "Was there a moment during the week — in any session — where the mind truly settled? Even for a breath or two? What did that feel like?"
4. "What did you learn about your mind that you did not know before this experiment? What surprised you?"

## Real-World Action
"Continue the daily practice for a second week. Same time, same place, same object. This week, add one element: notice the quality of your attention outside of formal practice. During ordinary activities — eating, walking, conversing, working — are there moments where you find yourself more present, more focused, less automatically pulled by distractions? The Sutras teach that dharana on the cushion eventually becomes dharana in life. You may begin to notice the spill-over effect this week. Track it. One note per day about a moment of unexpected presence in daily life."

## Report Back
"Next session, bring your tracking notes and we will analyze them together. I will ask: what did the experiment teach you about your mind's patterns? Were the restless days and the steady days different in any way you can identify — sleep, stress, time of day, what happened before you sat? And has the practice started to show up outside the five minutes — in a moment of focus at work, of presence with a person, of catching a vritti before it carried you away? Those carry-over moments are where the transformation lives."
