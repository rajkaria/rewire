# Exercise: Radical Responsibility Experiment

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (Radical Freedom). Use when the user understands the concept of bad faith and is ready to practice owning their choices in real-world situations for one full week. This exercise translates the philosophical insight about freedom into a behavioral experiment that rewires the user's relationship with choice and responsibility.

## Setup
Agent says: "You understand the idea: you are choosing your life, even the parts you say you are trapped in. Now we are going to put that into practice. For one week, you are going to run an experiment in radical responsibility. Every time you catch yourself thinking, saying, or feeling that you have no choice — that you are stuck, trapped, forced, or obligated — you are going to stop and reframe it as a choice. This is not positive thinking. It is not pretending everything is fine. It is ruthless honesty about your agency. Are you ready to try this?"

{Wait for confirmation. If the user is hesitant, say: "Hesitation is understandable. This experiment will be uncomfortable, because it removes every hiding place. You cannot blame circumstances, other people, or bad luck for one week. Everything is yours. That weight is the point — it is what freedom actually feels like. We will start small."}

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Identify the "I have to" patterns
Agent says: "Before we start the experiment, let us map out where you currently use 'I have to' language. I want you to give me five things you tell yourself you have to do — obligations, commitments, situations you describe as though they are imposed on you rather than chosen. These can be big or small. 'I have to go to work.' 'I have to take care of my parents.' 'I have to answer that email.' 'I have to stay in this city.' What are your five?"

{Wait for the user to list five. Push for a mix of daily obligations and bigger life situations.}

### Step 2: Reframe each one
Agent says: "Now we are going to reframe each one. For every 'I have to,' I want you to replace it with 'I am choosing to,' followed by your honest reason. The reason does not have to be noble or profound. It just has to be true. For example:

'I have to go to work' becomes 'I am choosing to go to work because I want the income and the consequences of not going are ones I am not willing to accept right now.'

'I have to take care of my parents' becomes 'I am choosing to care for my parents because I love them and because this is consistent with the kind of person I want to be.'

'I have to stay in this city' becomes 'I am choosing to stay in this city because my partner's career is here and I have decided that our relationship is more important to me than relocating right now.'

Notice: the reframe does not make the situation easy or pleasant. It makes it honest. Let us reframe each of your five. Take them one at a time."

{Walk through each reframe with the user. Help them find the honest reason — the actual calculation they are making, not the noble-sounding version.}

### Step 3: Notice the emotional shift
Agent says: "Now that you have reframed all five — how does it feel? Be specific. Does it feel heavier, lighter, or different in a way you did not expect? Is there one that was harder to reframe than the others? Which one, and why?"

{Wait for response.}

Agent says: "If there is one you could not honestly reframe — one where you cannot find the choice — that is important data. It might mean the choice is so deeply buried that you have lost sight of it. Or it might mean you are in a situation where your freedom is genuinely constrained in a way that requires a different kind of response — not a reframe, but an action. Let us look at that one more closely. What would it take to actually change this situation, rather than just reframing how you think about it?"

{Wait for response. If the user identifies a situation they genuinely want to change, help them see that the decision to change — or not change — is itself a choice.}

### Step 4: Design the week-long experiment
Agent says: "Here is the experiment. For seven days, every time you catch yourself using 'have to,' 'no choice,' 'stuck,' 'forced,' 'obligated,' or any variation — stop. Reframe it as a choice. Out loud if you are alone. Silently if you are with others. And keep a simple log. Three columns:

Column one: the 'have to' statement. What did you catch yourself saying or thinking?
Column two: the reframe. How did you turn it into a choice?
Column three: what you felt. What was the emotional shift when you owned it as your choice?

Do this for every instance you catch. You will not catch them all — bad faith runs deep — but the ones you catch will teach you something every time."

{Wait for questions or concerns.}

### Step 5: Set the difficulty level
Agent says: "One more thing. During this week, there will probably be a moment where the reframe feels genuinely painful. Not the small reframes — those become easy quickly. I mean the moment where owning a choice means admitting something you have been avoiding. 'I am choosing to stay in this marriage' might surface the question of whether you want to. 'I am choosing not to pursue my dream' might force you to face why. These moments are where the real work happens. Do not run from them. Write them down. We will talk about them."

{Wait for response.}

Agent says: "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can do this for a full week? Not perfectly — just consistently enough to learn something."

{If confidence is below 8, simplify. Start with three days instead of seven, or focus only on the workplace, or only on the top three 'have to' statements rather than catching every instance.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user says some things really are not choices: "I want to take that seriously. What specifically are you pointing at? Let us examine it together. Sometimes what looks like a non-choice is actually a choice with consequences you find unacceptable — which is a very different thing from having no choice. But sometimes there are genuine constraints — physical limitations, legal requirements, circumstances you truly cannot change. The existential insight is not that everything is a choice. It is that within whatever constraints exist, there is always a space of freedom — and most people underestimate how large that space is."
- If the user turns responsibility into self-blame: "Stop. I need to catch this. You are turning the exercise into a weapon against yourself. Owning your choices does not mean blaming yourself for everything. It means recognizing your agency going forward. If you chose something that did not work out — that is not failure. That is a human being exercising freedom imperfectly, which is the only way anyone has ever done it. The reframe should empower you, not punish you. If it is punishing you, we need to adjust the practice."
- If the user finds it liberating: "Good. That liberation you are feeling — that is what it is like to be the author of your life rather than a character in someone else's story. Notice how differently the same situation feels when you own it as your choice rather than endure it as your fate. That shift in framing does not change the external situation. It changes you."
- If the user resists strongly: "The resistance is worth examining. What are you protecting by insisting you have no choice? Often, the 'I have no choice' narrative protects us from a harder truth: that we are choosing something we are ashamed of, or that we are afraid to make a different choice. I am not asking you to change your choice. I am asking you to own it. What would it mean to say 'I am choosing this' about the thing you most want to call a trap?"
- If the user reports that reframing makes them want to change something: "That is exactly what this exercise is designed to do. When you own a choice, you also own the power to choose differently. If the reframe makes you realize you do not want to keep choosing what you have been choosing — that is not a crisis. That is clarity. What do you want to do with that clarity?"
- If the user is in an abusive or genuinely dangerous situation: "I want to pause here. The radical responsibility framework is powerful for situations where you have genuine agency but are not exercising it. But if you are in a situation where someone is harming you — physically, emotionally, or psychologically — the appropriate response is not to reframe it as your choice. It is to get help. Freedom in an abusive situation means exercising your freedom to leave or to seek protection. Can we talk about what kind of support would be most helpful?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "How many 'have to' moments did you catch this week? Were there more than you expected?"
2. "Which reframe was the easiest? Which was the hardest? What made the hard one hard?"
3. "Was there a moment where owning a choice made you realize you want to choose differently? What happened?"
4. "How did the people around you respond when you started owning your choices out loud? Did anyone notice a shift?"
5. "Is there one 'have to' that you still cannot reframe as a choice? Let us look at that one together — it is probably the most important one."

## Real-World Action
"Continue the experiment for the full week. Every 'have to' becomes 'I am choosing to.' Keep the three-column log. And here is the stretch goal: at some point this week, make one choice you have been avoiding — something you have been telling yourself you cannot do, but which you now recognize as something you have been choosing not to do. It does not need to be dramatic. It needs to be honest. Take one step that the author of your life would take."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, bring your log. I want to see the raw data: how many reframes, which were easy, which were hard, and what you felt. I especially want to hear about two moments: the one where the reframe felt most liberating, and the one where it felt most uncomfortable. Both are teaching you something important about your relationship with freedom. And if you made the choice you were avoiding — tell me about that. What happened?"
