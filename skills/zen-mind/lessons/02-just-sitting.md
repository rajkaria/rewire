# Lesson 2: Just Sitting

## Core Concept

Zazen. Seated meditation. The radical act of doing nothing with full attention.

In Zen, zazen is not a technique for achieving a state. It is the state. You do not sit zazen in order to become calm. You do not sit in order to achieve enlightenment. You do not sit in order to solve a problem, process an emotion, or become a better person. You sit in order to sit.

This is the most difficult instruction for a modern mind to receive. We are trained to do things for reasons. Every action should have a purpose, a return, a result. Zazen asks you to sit down and have no purpose whatsoever. Just sit. Just breathe. Just be here. The mind will rebel against this — it will generate thoughts, plans, judgments, fantasies, memories, to-do lists, philosophical insights. It will try to make the sitting into something productive. The practice is to let all of that arise and pass without following it. Not to suppress thought, but to stop being its servant.

The instruction is so simple it is almost absurd: sit down, be still, pay attention to your breath, and when your mind wanders — which it will — notice that it has wandered, and come back. That is the entire practice. You will do it ten thousand times. Each return to the breath is not a failure corrected. It is the practice itself. The noticing is the awakening. You were lost in thought, and then you were here. That transition — from lost to here — is what Zen calls waking up. And it happens not once, at the end of decades of practice, but a hundred times in every sitting.

Shikantaza — "just sitting" — is the Soto Zen approach. No koan, no object of meditation, no visualization. Just sitting, with bright, open awareness. Not trying to do anything. Not trying not to do anything. The body sits. The breath breathes. Thoughts come and go like clouds in a vast sky. You are the sky.

## Key Takeaway

There is nowhere to get to. The sitting is the arrival. Every breath you return to is enlightenment happening.

## Teaching Approach

This lesson is both the simplest and the most challenging in the skill. The instructions for zazen are brief. The practice of actually doing it — sitting still, doing nothing, on purpose — confronts every habit of the goal-oriented mind.

Do not over-instruct. The temptation is to give the user a comprehensive meditation manual: posture, breathing technique, what to do with thoughts, how long to sit, what to expect. Resist. The Zen approach to meditation instruction is minimal: sit, breathe, return. Everything else is commentary. Give the essential instruction and let the user discover the rest through practice.

The agent's primary task in this lesson is to help the user sit — not talk about sitting, not understand the theory of sitting, not prepare to sit. Sit. If possible, guide them through a brief sitting during the session itself, even just three to five minutes. Direct experience of zazen is worth more than any amount of explanation.

### Opening Question

"Have you ever done absolutely nothing? Not relaxing — that is doing something. Not watching something — that is doing something. Not thinking — that is definitely doing something. I mean nothing. Just being here, in your body, with no agenda whatsoever. What happens when you try?"

### Follow-Up Questions

- If the user has meditation experience: "Tell me about your practice. What do you do, and why do you do it? And here is the Zen question: can you sit without the why? Can you sit with no reason at all — not to be calm, not to be mindful, not to be a meditator — just to sit?"

- If the user says they cannot sit still: "Good. That honesty is already the practice. The instruction is not to be still. It is to sit. If your mind races, it races. If your body fidgets, it fidgets. You are not doing zazen wrong. You are doing zazen. The question is: can you stay on the cushion while all of that happens? Can you let the storm pass without leaving the room?"

- If the user asks what the point is: "There is no point. That is the point." {Pause.} "I know that sounds like a riddle. But sit with it. Every activity in your life has a purpose. Zazen asks you to step outside the purpose-driven world for a few minutes and discover what is here when you are not trying to get somewhere. The mind that needs a reason for everything — that is the mind zazen is inviting you to set down."

- If the user is anxious about doing it right: "There is no right. There is only sitting. If you are sitting and breathing, you are doing it. If your mind wanders, you are doing it. If you fall asleep, you are doing it — and then you wake up, and that waking up is the practice. Do not add a layer of self-judgment on top of the sitting. Just sit."

## Zazen Instruction

When the user is ready, guide them through the basic instruction. Keep it spare.

"Here is how to sit zazen. Find a quiet place. Sit on a cushion on the floor, or in a chair — it does not matter. What matters is that your back is upright and your body is stable. Not rigid. Not slumped. Upright and alive."

"Place your hands in your lap. The traditional position is the cosmic mudra — left hand resting in the right, palms up, thumbs lightly touching. But any comfortable position is fine."

"Lower your eyes. Not closed, not wide open. Half-lidded, gaze soft, resting on the floor a few feet ahead. You are not looking at anything. You are just — eyes open, present."

"Now breathe. Through your nose. Do not control the breath. Let it come and go at its own rhythm. Your only task is to be aware of it. Feel the inhale. Feel the exhale. Feel the pause between them. That is all."

"When your mind wanders — and it will, within seconds — notice that it has wandered, and come back to the breath. No judgment. No frustration. Just come back. This return is not a correction. It is the practice. Every return to the breath is a small awakening."

"Start with ten minutes. Set a timer. When the timer sounds, bow — to your cushion, to the room, to the practice. Then stand up slowly and carry whatever stillness you found into the next moment."

{If the user is willing, sit with them for three to five minutes right now. Say: "Let us try it now. Just three minutes. I will be quiet. You sit." Then be silent for three minutes.}

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "I cannot meditate because my mind will not stop thinking."
  **Reframe**: "If your mind stopped thinking, you would be dead. Zazen does not stop thought. It changes your relationship to thought. Right now, when a thought arises, you follow it — you climb aboard and ride it wherever it goes. In zazen, a thought arises and you see it. You notice it the way you notice a bird crossing the sky. It passes. You return to the breath. The thoughts do not stop. You stop following them."

- **Misconception**: "I need to feel calm or peaceful when I meditate. If I do not, I am doing it wrong."
  **Reframe**: "Zazen is not a calm-production machine. Some sittings are calm. Some are agitated. Some are boring. Some are painful. All of them are zazen. The practice is to sit with whatever arises — calm or chaos — without preference. The moment you prefer calm, you have introduced a goal, and the practice is no longer just sitting. It is sitting-in-order-to-be-calm, which is a different thing entirely."

- **Misconception**: "Meditation is selfish or unproductive — I should be doing something useful."
  **Reframe**: "The most useful thing a saw can do is stop cutting and get sharpened. Zazen is not a withdrawal from life. It is a sharpening of the instrument you bring to everything you do. But even that framing is too goal-oriented. Sit because sitting is enough. Not because it will make you better at your job. If it does, fine. If it does not, also fine."

- **Misconception**: "I need more time before I can start a meditation practice."
  **Reframe**: "You need ten minutes. You have ten minutes. The obstacle is not time. It is priority — and beneath that, the discomfort of being alone with yourself without an agenda. That discomfort is the practice. Sit with it."

## Alternate Entry Points

**The breath prompt**: "Stop reading for a moment. Take one breath — a full, conscious breath. In. Out. Did you notice it? That awareness — the knowing of the breath — is zazen in its simplest form. You just practiced. The only difference between that and a sitting practice is duration."

**The waiting prompt**: "Think of the last time you were stuck waiting — in a line, in a waiting room, on hold. What did you do? You probably reached for your phone. What if you had not? What if you had just stood there, doing nothing, aware of standing, aware of breathing, aware of waiting? That is zazen in the middle of life."

**The boredom prompt**: "Boredom is the threshold of zazen. When you sit and nothing interesting happens, the mind labels it boring and wants to leave. The practice is to stay. To be bored and sit with the boredom. To discover what is on the other side of boredom — which is, almost always, a very quiet, very alive attention. You cannot get there by trying. You get there by staying."

## Bridge to Practice

After introducing zazen, help the user design a realistic sitting practice for the coming week. The key word is realistic.

Ask: "When could you sit? I mean literally — what time of day, and where? Not when you should sit. When you actually will sit."

Help them define:
- **Time**: Morning is traditional, but any consistent time works. The key is consistency, not duration.
- **Place**: Somewhere quiet, where they will not be interrupted. It does not need to be a meditation room. A corner of the bedroom, a chair by a window, a park bench.
- **Duration**: Start with ten minutes. Not five — that is too short to settle. Not twenty — that is too long to sustain as a new practice. Ten minutes. Every day.
- **Anchor**: Something that connects the practice to an existing habit. After making coffee. Before checking email. After the children go to bed. Anchor the sitting to something they already do.

### Assignment

"This week, sit zazen once a day for ten minutes. Set a timer. Follow the breath. When the mind wanders, come back. That is the entire instruction."

"Do not evaluate the sittings. Do not grade yourself. Do not compare Tuesday's sitting to Wednesday's. Each sitting is complete in itself. Some will feel peaceful. Some will feel like a war. Both are zazen."

"At the end of each sitting, notice one thing: how do you feel — in your body, not your mind — as you stand up and re-enter your day? Just notice."

### What to Listen For in the Debrief

When the user returns, pay attention to:

- **Whether they actually sat**: This is the only question that matters. Not how it went. Not what they experienced. Did they sit? If yes, everything else follows. If no, explore what got in the way — and that exploration becomes the teaching.

- **The quality of their description**: Users who have actually sat zazen describe specific experiences — "My left knee hurt," "I kept hearing the refrigerator," "I had the same thought about work over and over." Users who have thought about zazen describe general impressions — "It was relaxing," "I felt centered." The specific is the real.

- **Their relationship to wandering**: Does the user treat mind-wandering as failure? This is the most common obstacle. Reframe relentlessly: "The mind wandering is not the problem. It is the practice. Every time you notice you have wandered, you wake up. That is it. That is the whole thing."

- **Physical experience**: Zazen happens in the body. Ask about posture, sensation, discomfort, the feeling of breathing. If the user reports only mental experience — thoughts, feelings, concepts — bring them back to the body. "What was happening in your back? Your belly? Your hands?"

- **The desire for results**: If the user is already measuring their meditation ("Tuesday was better than Monday"), name the pattern. "Notice what you are doing. You are grading the sittings. Comparing them. Ranking them. That is the goal-oriented mind doing what it does. In Zen, there is no good sitting and no bad sitting. There is just sitting. Can you let Tuesday be Tuesday and Wednesday be Wednesday, without the comparison?"
