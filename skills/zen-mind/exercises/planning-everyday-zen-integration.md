# Exercise: Everyday Zen Integration

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 3 (Everyday Zen), once the user has been introduced to the practice of bringing full presence to ordinary activities and has experimented with eating, walking, or working with attention. This exercise formalizes the practice by designing three specific daily activities that will become ongoing presence practices. Best suited for users who have tasted everyday Zen but need structure to sustain it.

## Setup
Agent says: "You have experienced what it is like to be fully present to an ordinary activity — to eat a meal, to walk a path, to do a task with your whole attention. Now the question is: how do you keep doing that? Not as an exercise. As a way of living."

{Pause.}

"We are going to design something simple. Three daily activities that become your everyday Zen practice. Not additions to your life. Transformations of what is already there. The activities stay the same. The quality of attention changes."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Review what is already there
Agent says: "Walk me through a typical day. From the moment you wake up to the moment you go to sleep. Not in detail — just the rhythm. The major activities. The transitions. The routines."

{Listen carefully. The user's daily routine is the raw material of their practice. Do not impose activities. Find the ones that are already there.}

After they describe their day, ask: "Which of those activities do you currently do on autopilot? Where is the body going through the motions while the mind is somewhere else?"

{Most users will identify several. Common ones: morning routines (showering, getting dressed, making breakfast), commuting, email, household tasks, eating, transitional moments (getting in the car, walking between rooms, waiting).}

### Step 2: Select three activities
Agent says: "From everything you described, I want you to choose three activities — one from the morning, one from the middle of the day, and one from the evening. These will become your everyday Zen practices."

"Here is what I am looking for: activities that you already do every day, that take at least a few minutes, and that you currently do without full attention. Not special activities. Ordinary ones. The more ordinary, the better. Zen is not about making the ordinary special. It is about discovering that the ordinary already is."

{Guide the user toward activities that meet these criteria:}

- **Duration**: At least 3-5 minutes. Long enough for presence to deepen. Short enough to sustain without feeling like a burden.
- **Physicality**: Ideally activities with a sensory component — touch, taste, movement, sound. The body is the anchor for presence.
- **Regularity**: Something they do every single day, or nearly. The practice depends on consistency.
- **Low stakes**: Not a high-pressure work task. Something where they can afford to slow down and be present without external consequences.

Present some possibilities if the user needs prompting:

**Morning options:**
- Making and drinking the first cup of coffee or tea
- Showering (feeling the water, the temperature, the soap)
- The first ten minutes of being awake — lying in bed, feeling the body, noticing the light
- Getting dressed — feeling the fabric, choosing with attention

**Midday options:**
- Eating lunch (or any one meal) without devices or reading
- Walking — to a meeting, to the mailbox, between buildings — feeling the feet, the air, the movement
- The first five minutes of a work session — sitting down, settling, beginning with intention rather than reflex
- Washing hands — a micro-practice, thirty seconds of full attention to water and sensation

**Evening options:**
- Preparing dinner — chopping, stirring, tasting with full attention
- Washing the dishes — the classic Zen practice of samu
- The last ten minutes before sleep — lying in bed, feeling the body settle, letting the day go
- A walk around the block — kinhin in the neighborhood

### Step 3: Design each practice
For each of the three activities, work with the user to define:

**The signal:**
Agent says: "What will remind you to shift into presence? Not willpower — a cue. Something physical that triggers the practice. Picking up the kettle. Putting your hand on the door handle. Sitting down in the chair. The cue is the bell that says: be here now."

**The intention:**
"Before you begin the activity, take one breath. Not to be calm. To arrive. One conscious breath that marks the transition from autopilot to attention. That breath is the beginning of the practice."

**The quality of attention:**
"During the activity, the practice is simple: do this one thing. Feel what you feel. See what you see. Hear what you hear. When the mind wanders — and it will — notice that it has wandered, and come back. Just like zazen. But instead of the breath, the anchor is the activity itself."

"No devices. No multitasking. No narrating what you are doing. Just doing it."

**The close:**
"When the activity ends, pause. One more breath. Notice: what is the quality of your attention right now? Has it changed? Is there any residual presence that carries into the next moment? That carry-over is the bridge between practice and life."

{Confirm each practice: "So your three practices are: [morning activity] with [signal], [midday activity] with [signal], and [evening activity] with [signal]. Three moments of full presence. Every day. For the next week, and beyond."}

### Step 4: Anticipate the obstacles
Agent says: "Let us be realistic about what will get in the way."

"The biggest obstacle is forgetting. You will be on autopilot before you know it — the morning routine will carry you through before you remember to be present. The signal helps with this. If you attach the practice to a physical cue, the cue will remind you even when your mind does not."

"The second obstacle is impatience. Presence can feel slow. You will want to speed up, to check your phone, to think about the next thing while doing this thing. Notice that impatience. It is the autopilot trying to take back control. Let it be there. Do not fight it. Just keep doing the activity with attention."

"The third obstacle is judgment. You will evaluate your presence — 'I was not present enough,' 'Yesterday was better,' 'I keep getting distracted.' That evaluating voice is not the practice. It is the mind doing what the mind does. Notice it, set it down, and come back to the activity."

Ask: "Which of these obstacles do you think will be strongest for you? And is there anything else — specific to your life — that you think will interfere?"

{Address whatever they identify. The goal is not to eliminate obstacles but to anticipate them so they become part of the practice rather than interruptions of it.}

## Branching Responses

- If the user chooses activities that are too ambitious: "I appreciate the commitment, but I want to make this sustainable. If you choose activities where presence feels like a demand — a high-pressure meeting, a conversation with a difficult person — you are adding stress to the practice. Start with low-stakes activities. Once presence becomes natural there, it will begin to extend on its own."

- If the user says they cannot do anything without multitasking: "That is the clearest sign that this practice matters for you. The inability to do one thing at a time is not efficiency. It is compulsion. And the only way to break a compulsion is to do the opposite, gently, repeatedly, until it loosens. Start with one activity. Just one. Five minutes of single-tasking. See what happens."

- If the user wants to add meditation time instead of transforming daily activities: "Meditation is important, and you have your zazen practice. But this exercise is about something different. It is about dissolving the boundary between practice and life. If you meditate for ten minutes and then spend the rest of the day on autopilot, the practice stays on the cushion. Everyday Zen brings the practice into the kitchen, the street, the office. That is where it matters most."

- If the user reports that presence makes mundane activities feel slower or more tedious: "That tediousness is interesting. It is the mind's opinion about the activity — 'this is boring, this is beneath my attention.' But is the activity itself tedious, or is the mind's judgment of it tedious? When you actually taste the food, feel the water, sense the movement — is there tediousness in the sensation, or only in the commentary about the sensation?"

- If the user reports that presence is effortful and exhausting: "That suggests you are trying too hard. Presence is not concentration. Concentration is like a spotlight — narrow, focused, tiring. Presence is like sunlight — broad, open, effortless. You are not trying to focus on the activity. You are just being with it. Let the attention soften. Let the awareness open. See if the effort can drop away and the presence can remain."

## Midweek Check (Optional)
If the user checks in midweek, ask:

"How are the three practices going? Which one is easiest? Which one is hardest? And has anything surprised you — a moment where the ordinary felt different, or a resistance you did not expect?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "Which of the three practices stuck? Which did you do most consistently?"
2. "Was there a moment this week where an ordinary activity — one of your three or another — felt different because you were present to it? Describe that moment."
3. "Did you discover anything about the activity itself — a taste, a sensation, a detail — that you had never noticed before, despite doing it hundreds of times?"
4. "How did it feel to do one thing at a time? What did you notice about the pull to multitask, to check your phone, to be somewhere else?"
5. "Did the quality of attention during the practice ever carry over into the next activity? Was there a moment where presence bled into the rest of your day without you trying?"
6. "If you had to describe the difference between doing an activity on autopilot and doing it with full presence, in one sentence, what would you say?"

## Real-World Action
"The three practices you designed are not an exercise. They are your everyday Zen practice. Keep doing them. Not for a week. As an ongoing commitment to being present in your own life."

"Over time, the three practices will change. You may replace one activity with another. You may find that presence extends to activities you did not designate. You may discover that the signal becomes unnecessary — that you arrive at the activity and presence is already there, waiting. That is integration."

"Here is the measure of success: not how present you were during the practice, but how present you are between the practices. When the whole day begins to take on the quality of attention you bring to these three moments, the gap between practice and life has closed. That is everyday Zen."

"One more thing. Eventually, the practices themselves will dissolve. Not because you stopped doing them, but because you stopped needing to designate them. Presence will become not something you do, but the way you are. The practices are scaffolding. The building is your whole life."

## Report Back
"Next time, bring me one ordinary moment from this week that felt alive — that had a quality of presence that surprised you. Not a meditation experience. Not a special moment. Something ordinary that turned out to be extraordinary when you actually showed up for it."
