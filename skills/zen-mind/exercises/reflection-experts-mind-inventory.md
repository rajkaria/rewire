# Exercise: Expert's Mind Inventory

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (Beginner's Mind), once the user has been introduced to shoshin and has practiced looking at something familiar with fresh eyes. This exercise deepens the inquiry by mapping the specific domains where expertise has narrowed perception. Best suited for users who connect with the concept of beginner's mind but need to see concretely where expert's mind operates in their life.

## Setup
Agent says: "In our last conversation, we explored shoshin — beginner's mind. Now I want to go deeper. Not into the concept, but into your life. I want us to map where expert's mind has taken over — where you have stopped seeing because you believe you already know."

{Pause.}

"This is not about beating yourself up for having expertise. Expertise is valuable. But expertise that is never questioned becomes a cage. Today, we are going to look at the cage."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: The five domains
Agent says: "I want you to examine five areas of your life. In each one, I am going to ask the same question: where have you stopped being curious? Where have you replaced seeing with knowing? Take your time with each one."

**Domain 1 — Work or craft:**
"What do you do for a living, or what is the skill you have practiced the longest? Now — where in that work have you gone on autopilot? Where do you rely on what has always worked instead of looking at what is actually here? Where have you stopped learning because you decided you already know?"

{Wait for the user to reflect. If they say "nowhere," push gently: "Everyone who has practiced something long enough has blind spots. The expert eye surgeon who no longer sees the patient's face. The experienced teacher who no longer hears the question behind the question. Where is your blind spot?"}

**Domain 2 — Relationships:**
"Think of the people closest to you — partner, family, close friends. Where have you stopped seeing them? Not stopped caring — stopped seeing. Where do you think you know what they will say, what they need, how they feel? Where have your assumptions replaced your attention?"

{This is often the most confronting domain. Go gently. If the user resists — "I pay close attention to the people I love" — do not argue. Ask: "When was the last time someone close to you surprised you? Not with a gift or an event. With who they are. If it has been a while, that may be because you have a very complete picture of them — or because the picture has replaced the person."}

**Domain 3 — Self:**
"This is the hardest one. Where have you stopped seeing yourself? What have you decided about who you are — your strengths, your limitations, your personality, your patterns — that you no longer question? What would you discover if you looked at yourself the way you looked at a stranger?"

{Many users will have a fixed self-concept — "I am an introvert," "I am not creative," "I am the responsible one." These are often true, but they are also filters. Ask: "How old is that story about yourself? When did you decide that? Is it still true — or is it a map you drew a long time ago that you never updated?"}

**Domain 4 — Environment:**
"Where do you live? How long have you lived there? Now — what have you stopped seeing? The street you walk down. The room you sit in. The view from your window. What is actually there that you no longer notice because you have seen it a thousand times?"

**Domain 5 — Beliefs and opinions:**
"What are you certain about? Pick two or three things you believe strongly — about the world, about people, about how things work. Now ask: when was the last time you seriously questioned those beliefs? Not defended them. Questioned them. When was the last time you genuinely considered that you might be wrong?"

{If the user is uncomfortable: "I am not asking you to abandon your beliefs. I am asking you to hold them up to the light and look at them with beginner's eyes. A belief you cannot question is not a conviction. It is a prison."}

### Step 2: Map the pattern
Agent says: "Now look at what you have found. Across these five domains — work, relationships, self, environment, beliefs — is there a pattern? Do you tend to close down in the same way? Some people close through certainty — they stop questioning. Some close through efficiency — they stop exploring. Some close through comfort — they stop challenging. What is your pattern?"

{Help the user identify their primary mode of expert's mind. This is not about judgment. It is about clarity. The first step to opening is seeing where you are closed.}

### Step 3: Choose three doors
Agent says: "I want you to choose three specific places where expert's mind is operating — one from any three of the five domains. These are the doors we are going to practice opening."

For each one, ask:
- "What would it look like to bring shoshin here — to look at this person, this skill, this belief as if for the first time?"
- "What question could you ask — of yourself, of someone else, of the situation — that expert's mind has been preventing you from asking?"
- "What might you see that you have been missing?"

{Write down or confirm the three doors. These become the user's practice for the week.}

## Branching Responses

- If the user is defensive about their expertise: "Your expertise is real and valuable. This is not about pretending you do not know things. It is about noticing where the knowing has calcified — where it has become so automatic that it prevents you from seeing what is new, what has changed, what you might be wrong about. The strongest experts are the ones who can hold their expertise and their beginner's mind at the same time."

- If the user is overwhelmed by how many domains expert's mind has invaded: "That is a lot to see at once. Do not try to open all the doors. Choose three. Or one. Shoshin is not a renovation project. It is a practice. You open one door. Then another. Then one that was open closes, and you open it again. It never ends, and that is fine."

- If the user identifies a relationship where they have stopped seeing the other person: "That is courageous to notice. And the good news is that the remedy is simple — not easy, but simple. Look at them. Really look. Listen to them as if you have just met. Ask them something you have never asked. You do not need to change the relationship. You need to see the person who is actually there, not the person you decided they were."

- If the user identifies a fixed self-concept that is limiting them: "You have been carrying that definition of yourself for a long time. I am not asking you to throw it away. I am asking: what if you looked at yourself the way a portrait painter looks at a face — not to judge, not to categorize, but to see? What is actually there, underneath the label?"

- If the user says they do not have strong beliefs or opinions: "That itself might be worth examining. Do you not have strong opinions, or do you hold your opinions so naturally that they feel like reality rather than opinions? The deepest assumptions are the ones we do not recognize as assumptions. They are the water the fish does not see."

## Debrief Questions
1. "Which domain was the hardest to examine? Where did expert's mind resist being seen?"
2. "What surprised you? Was there something you thought you knew that turned out to be more complex, more uncertain, or more interesting than you assumed?"
3. "Of the three doors you chose, which one did you practice opening this week? What happened when you brought beginner's mind there?"
4. "Did anyone in your life notice a change? If you looked at someone with fresh eyes, did they respond differently?"
5. "What is the cost of expert's mind? Now that you have mapped it, can you see what it has been costing you — in curiosity, in connection, in aliveness?"
6. "Where do you most want to keep practicing shoshin? Which closed door matters most to you?"

## Real-World Action
"The inventory is complete. Now the practice begins. From the three doors you identified, choose one — the one that matters most, the one where opening would change something real in your life. Commit to practicing shoshin in that domain for the next month."

"The practice is not dramatic. It is this: every time you approach that person, that skill, that belief, that environment, pause. One breath. And then look as if you have never seen it before. Do not change anything. Just look. Notice what you see when you are not seeing through your assumptions."

"At the end of the month, you will know whether beginner's mind is a nice idea or a way of life. The difference is entirely in the practice."

## Report Back
"Next time, bring me one moment of genuine seeing — one time this week when you looked at something familiar and saw it fresh. Not the concept. The moment. What was it? What did you see?"
