# Exercise: Zazen Practice

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (Just Sitting), once the user has been introduced to zazen and ideally has sat at least once during a session. This exercise establishes a seven-day sitting practice. It is the foundational practice of the entire skill — without sitting, everything else remains conceptual. Best suited for users who understand the idea of zazen but have not yet established a daily practice.

## Setup
Agent says: "You have been introduced to zazen. Now the practice begins. Not the idea of practice — the actual practice. Sitting. Every day. For seven days."

{Pause.}

"I am not going to give you a lot of instructions. Zen pedagogy tends toward the minimal. The instruction is: sit, breathe, return. Everything else you will discover by doing it. The body is the teacher. The cushion is the classroom. I am just here to hold the structure."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Establish the conditions
Agent says: "First, let us make this real. When will you sit? Not 'sometime in the morning.' What time? Where? For how long?"

Help the user define:
- **Time**: A specific time of day they can protect. Morning is traditional because the mind is relatively quiet, but any consistent time works. What matters is that they choose a time and keep it.
- **Place**: A specific location. It does not need to be special. A corner of a room. A chair by a window. The edge of a bed. What matters is consistency — the body learns to settle when it returns to the same place.
- **Duration**: Ten minutes. Not five, not twenty. Ten minutes is long enough for the mind to settle and short enough to sustain for seven days without resistance. They can extend later if they wish.

{Once defined, confirm: "So you will sit at [time], in [place], for ten minutes, every day for seven days. That is the commitment. Not flexible. Not negotiable. Ten minutes. Can you do that?"}

### Step 2: Review the instruction
Agent says: "The instruction for zazen is simple enough to fit in one breath."

"Sit upright. Eyes half-open, gaze soft. Hands resting in your lap. Breathe naturally through your nose. When you notice your mind has wandered, return to the breath. That is it."

"No counting breaths unless you find it helpful. No mantras. No visualizations. No goals. Just sitting. Just breathing. Just being here."

"Set a timer for ten minutes. When it sounds, bow if you wish — to the practice, to the room, to your own willingness to be here — and stand up slowly."

### Step 3: Prepare for difficulty
Agent says: "I want to prepare you for what will happen, because if I do not, you might think you are failing when you are actually practicing."

"Day one or two: it will feel new and possibly interesting. The mind will cooperate because this is a new activity, and beginner's mind is natural with new things."

"Day three or four: the novelty wears off. The mind will start resisting. You will think: 'I do not have time.' 'I will do it later.' 'I did not sleep well, I should rest instead.' 'This is not doing anything.' These thoughts are not obstacles to practice. They are the practice. They are the expert's mind reasserting itself, telling you it already knows that sitting is pointless. Notice the thoughts. Sit anyway."

"Day five through seven: something may shift. Not necessarily. But many people report that around the fourth or fifth day, the sitting changes quality. It stops being something you are doing to yourself and starts being something you are doing for yourself. The body begins to settle more quickly. The mind's commentary becomes background noise rather than foreground. You may have a moment — even a few seconds — of genuine stillness. Not the absence of thought, but the presence of awareness without the need to do anything about it."

"Or none of that may happen. You may sit for seven days and find it frustrating, boring, and pointless. That is also the practice. The practice is sitting. Not the experience of sitting."

### Step 4: Establish the observation
Agent says: "After each sitting, I want you to notice three things. Not during the sitting — after. Just a few seconds of observation before you move on."

1. **Body**: What is happening in your body right now? Any residual stillness? Any tension? Any change from before you sat?
2. **Mind**: What is the quality of your mind right now? Quiet? Busy? Spacious? Contracted? Different from when you sat down?
3. **Transition**: What happens as you move from sitting to your first activity? Is there a moment — even a second — where the quality of attention from zazen carries into the next thing you do?

"If you want, write one sentence after each sitting. Not required. But if you do, it becomes a record of seven days of practice, and that record will be useful when we talk."

## Branching Responses

- If the user is enthusiastic and wants to sit longer than ten minutes: "I appreciate the commitment. But start with ten. Zen practice is not about ambition. It is about consistency. Sitting for an hour once is less valuable than sitting for ten minutes every day. Let ten minutes become easy. Let it become as natural as brushing your teeth. Then, if you want more, you will know."

- If the user is anxious about doing it wrong: "You cannot do it wrong. If you are sitting and breathing, you are doing it. The mind will tell you that you are not calm enough, not focused enough, not Zen enough. That voice — the one evaluating your meditation — is just another thought. Notice it. Return to the breath. You are already practicing."

- If the user says they have tried meditation before and failed: "You did not fail. You stopped. Those are different things. And you stopped probably because you were judging the practice against some ideal — it was supposed to be peaceful, or productive, or transformative, and it was not. In Zen, there is no ideal. There is just sitting. If you can let go of what meditation is supposed to be, you can sit. Just sit."

- If the user has a physical limitation that prevents traditional sitting: "Zazen is not about the posture. It is about the attention. If you cannot sit on the floor, sit in a chair. If you cannot sit, lie down. If you cannot be still, do walking meditation — kinhin. The body position matters less than the quality of awareness. Adapt the form. Keep the attention."

- If the user asks about guided meditations or apps: "Guided meditations can be helpful for some practices. But zazen is not guided. The silence is the instruction. In zazen, you sit with yourself — no voice telling you what to notice, no bell telling you when to breathe. Just you and the breath and whatever arises. That simplicity is the practice. Try seven days without guidance and see what happens."

## Midweek Check (Optional)
If the user checks in midweek, keep it brief:

"Have you been sitting?"

{Wait for the answer.}

If yes: "Good. What have you noticed? Not what the sittings were like — what is different in you?"

If no: "What got in the way? Not as a judgment — as genuine curiosity. The obstacles are part of the practice. What did the mind say to keep you from the cushion?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "How many of the seven days did you sit? Be honest. There is no grade."
2. "Describe one sitting — any one. Not what you thought about it. What happened. What did you feel in your body? Where did your mind go? What was it like to return to the breath?"
3. "Was there a day when sitting was particularly difficult? What was happening — in the sitting, and in your life?"
4. "Was there a moment — even a second — where the practice felt like something other than an exercise? A moment of genuine stillness, or arrival, or presence? If so, what was it like?"
5. "What did you notice in the transition — the moment after the timer sounded and you stood up? Was there any residual quality of the sitting that followed you into the next activity?"
6. "Do you want to continue sitting? Not because you should. Not because it is good for you. Because you want to. What does your body say?"

## Real-World Action
"The seven-day experiment is over. Now comes the real question: will you keep sitting?"

"If the answer is yes, establish the practice. Same time, same place, every day. Ten minutes. No exceptions. Not for a week — as an ongoing practice. Like eating or sleeping. Something you do because you are alive and this is how you want to meet the day."

"If the answer is not yet, that is fine. But try this: leave the cushion or the chair in place. Leave the space set up. Every morning, it will be there. One day, you will sit down. Not because you decided to. Because the cushion was there and you were ready."

"If you do continue, here is one adjustment: at the end of each sitting, before you stand, take one more breath. Dedicate it. Not to yourself. To everyone who is struggling with something today. In Zen, we call this turning the merit outward. Your sitting is not just for you."

## Report Back
"Next time, I will ask one question: what is zazen teaching you that no one can explain? Not what you have read about it. Not what you think about it. What has the sitting itself shown you?"
