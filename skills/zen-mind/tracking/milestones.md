# Milestones

## Stage 1: Arriving
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: User begins to see the difference between thinking about experience and having experience. Expert's mind becomes visible as a pattern rather than invisible as a default.
**The user can**: Describe at least one domain where expertise or familiarity has narrowed their perception — where they have stopped seeing because they believe they already know. Report at least one moment of shoshin — genuine fresh seeing of something familiar — from between sessions. Distinguish between direct experience (sensory, immediate, specific) and narrative about experience (conceptual, interpretive, general). Articulate the cost of expert's mind in their own life — the curiosity lost, the connections dimmed, the aliveness sacrificed to competence. Sit for at least one period of zazen, even brief, and describe what happened in concrete terms rather than abstract ones. Demonstrate willingness to not know — to hold a question without rushing to answer it, to sit in uncertainty without demanding resolution.

## Stage 2: Sitting
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User has begun a zazen practice and is developing a relationship with sitting. The practice is becoming theirs rather than the skill's assignment.
**The user can**: Sit zazen with some regularity — not perfectly, not every day necessarily, but with genuine intention and follow-through. Describe the experience of sitting in embodied terms — what happens in the body, where the mind goes, what the return to breath feels like — rather than theoretical ones. Recognize the goal-oriented mind's intrusion into meditation — the desire for calm, the evaluation of sittings, the pursuit of states — and hold that recognition with humor rather than frustration. Complete the Expert's Mind Inventory and identify specific domains where they will practice shoshin, with concrete commitments. Notice when they are operating on autopilot in daily life — even if they cannot always stop it, the noticing itself is the practice. Describe the difference between sitting with purpose and sitting without purpose — even if they cannot yet sustain the latter.

## Stage 3: Practicing
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User is extending presence from the cushion into daily life. Ordinary activities are becoming practice opportunities. The gap between formal meditation and everyday awareness is beginning to close.
**The user can**: Maintain a zazen practice and describe its evolution — how sitting has changed over the weeks, what they have discovered, where they still struggle. Bring full presence to at least one ordinary daily activity — eating, walking, working, listening — with regularity and intention. Complete the Everyday Zen Integration exercise and report on what happened when they treated ordinary activities as practice. Identify their primary attachment pattern — whether they grasp at outcomes, identity, opinions, control, or experiences — and practice holding that pattern with awareness and open hands. Report moments of mushin — action without self-conscious interference, presence without effort — even if brief. Carry a koan or a paradoxical question without needing to resolve it, letting it work below the level of conceptual thought. Demonstrate shoshin in relationships — listening without predicting, seeing a familiar person with fresh eyes, asking genuine questions.

## Stage 4: Inhabiting / Graduation
**Marker**: User has moved from practicing Zen to inhabiting it. The practices have become less effortful and more natural. Presence is not something they do — it is the way they meet experience.
**The user can**: Meet a familiar situation — a person, a task, a routine — with fresh eyes and genuine curiosity, not because they are performing shoshin but because the habit of beginner's mind has taken root. Sit zazen as an ongoing practice that is theirs — not an assignment, not an obligation, but a choice they make because it is how they want to begin their day or settle their mind. Bring the quality of zazen attention — open, non-grasping, present — to activities throughout their day without needing to designate them as "practice." Notice grasping as it arises — at outcomes, opinions, identity, control — and release it with increasing ease, not through force but through clear seeing. Describe their experience in concrete, immediate, specific terms rather than Zen concepts — they are living the practice rather than talking about it. Understand that there is no graduation from Zen practice. There is only returning, again and again, to this moment. They are not graduating from the practice. They are graduating from needing this structure to support it. The practice continues. It has no end.
**Next step**: Suggest `mindful-path` for users who want to deepen systematic meditation practice, `wabi-sabi` for users drawn to the aesthetic and philosophical dimensions of simplicity and imperfection, `way-of-tao` for users who resonate with the non-striving quality of Zen and want to explore its Taoist roots, `ikigai` for users whose clarity of attention has cleared the noise and they now need help finding direction, or `atomic-habits` for users who want practical systems to sustain their practice in daily life.
