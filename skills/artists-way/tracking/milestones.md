# Milestones

## Stage 1: Recognition
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: User can identify their creative wound and name the beliefs that formed from it. They recognize the inner censor as a distinct voice, not as truth.
**The user can**: Articulate what happened to their creative self — the specific moments, people, or patterns that shut it down. They can separate the censor's voice from their own voice. They have begun morning pages (even imperfectly) and understand why the practice matters.
**What to celebrate**: "You can see the wound now. That might not sound like much, but most people spend their whole lives believing the censor is just 'being realistic.' You know better. That awareness is the foundation for everything that comes next."

## Stage 2: Practice
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User has established a consistent morning pages practice (5+ days per week) and has taken at least two artist dates. Creative experiments are beginning — small, tentative, but real.
**The user can**: Show up on the page most mornings without agonizing over it. Take themselves on solo creative adventures without guilt. Catch the inner censor mid-sentence at least some of the time. Complete a small creative act (a sketch, a poem, a rearranged room, a recipe improvised from scratch) without requiring it to be good.
**What to celebrate**: "You're doing the work. Morning pages are happening, you're filling the well, and you made something this week. This is what creative recovery looks like in practice — not a dramatic breakthrough, but a steady rebuilding of trust between you and your creative self."

## Stage 3: Integration
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User is integrating creative practices into their life as regular, protected rhythms rather than one-off experiments. The inner censor is still present but has lost much of its authority.
**The user can**: Maintain morning pages as a non-negotiable daily practice. Schedule and protect artist dates without prompting. Recognize the censor immediately when it speaks and choose to create anyway. Take on a slightly larger creative project — not just exercises, but something self-directed. Share creative work with at least one trusted person.
**What to celebrate**: "Something has shifted. You're not just doing creative exercises — you're living a more creative life. The practices aren't assignments anymore; they're part of how you operate. And you're making things and sharing them. That takes real courage."

## Stage 4: Creative Identity / Graduation
**Marker**: User identifies as a creative person without qualification, apology, or comparison. Creative practice is self-sustaining — they don't need coaching prompts to maintain it.
**The user can**: Maintain morning pages and artist dates independently, adjusting the practices to fit their evolving life. Name their inner censor, understand its origins, and refuse to give it editorial authority — consistently, not just on good days. Complete creative projects and share them without perfectionism paralyzing the process. Seek out creative input instinctively — they naturally fill the well because they recognize when it's running low. Describe themselves as a creative person in conversation, without hedging ("I'm not really an artist, but...").
**What to celebrate**: "You came in believing you weren't creative, or that creativity wasn't for you. Look at where you are now. You write every morning. You feed your creative self every week. You make things. You share them. The censor still talks, but you don't let it run the show anymore. You didn't become someone new — you found someone who was always there."

## Graduation Criteria

A user is ready to graduate when all five of these are true:

1. **Self-sustaining practice**: They maintain morning pages (or an equivalent daily creative practice) without coaching reminders for at least three consecutive weeks.
2. **Censor mastery**: They can name the censor, trace its origins, and create despite its protests — not perfectly, but consistently.
3. **Well-filling habit**: They regularly seek out experiences that nourish their creative self, whether through formal artist dates or a general orientation toward beauty, novelty, and play.
4. **Creative output**: They have completed at least one creative project (however small) that they are willing to share with someone.
5. **Identity integration**: They call themselves creative — or at minimum, they no longer deny it — without needing external validation or comparison to "real" artists.

| Go deeper into focus and flow during creative sessions | `deep-work` — structured attention for sustained creative production |
| Understand and work with their inner critic at a deeper level | `inner-parts` — the censor as a protective part, explored through IFS |
| Overcome the belief that they lack innate talent | `growth-mindset` — reframe ability as developable, not fixed |
| Find their creative purpose and direction | `ikigai` — clarify where passion, skill, need, and livelihood intersect |
| Process grief or trauma connected to creative loss | `act-values` — acceptance and committed action for carrying pain while moving forward |
