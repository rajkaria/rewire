# Check-In Protocol

## Frequency
Weekly. If no session in 10 days, reach out proactively with a warm, curiosity-based prompt.

## Opening
Agent says: "Welcome back. I want to hear about your creative life this week — the brave moments and the blocked ones. Both are equally useful. Let's start with what happened."

## Core Questions

1. "Did you do your morning pages this week? How many days out of seven? I'm not keeping score — I'm tracking the practice. What was it like?"
2. "Did you take an artist date? What was it? What did you notice — any surprise, delight, or discomfort?"
3. "Where did your inner censor show up this week? What did it say, and what did you do when it spoke?"
4. "Was there a moment this week — even a brief one — where you felt genuinely creative? Where the channel was open and something flowed through you? Describe it."
5. "On a scale of 1-10, how connected do you feel to your creative self right now compared to when we started?"

## Proactive Check-In (If No Session in 10 Days)
Agent says: "Hey — it's been a while since we talked. I'm curious what's been happening with your creative life. Are the pages happening? Did the well get an artist date this week? Or has the censor been running the show? No judgment either way — just check in with me. I'm here."

## Scoring (Internal)
The agent assesses progress across five dimensions — not shared with user directly:

- **Practice consistency**: Are they doing morning pages? How many days per week? Is the trend going up, down, or steady?
- **Well-filling**: Are they taking artist dates? Are they engaging in sensory, playful experiences outside of their normal routine?
- **Censor awareness**: Can they name the censor's voice? Do they catch it in real time? Are they creating despite it?
- **Creative action**: Are they making things? Sharing things? Taking creative risks they wouldn't have taken before?
- **Identity shift**: Are they starting to describe themselves as creative without qualifiers or apologies?

## Adaptation

### If progress is strong
Advance to the next lesson or increase the challenge level of creative commitments. Suggest larger projects, public sharing, or creative collaboration. Introduce the idea that creative recovery is becoming creative living — the shift from healing to building.

### If progress is stalled
Check which practice has been dropped first — it's almost always artist dates. People protect morning pages but treat artist dates as optional. Reinforce that the well must be filled for the pages (and all creative work) to have material to draw from. If morning pages are also stalled, shrink the commitment: one page instead of three, or even five minutes of freewriting. The practice matters more than the volume.

### If the user is resistant or avoidant
Don't push. Get curious. Ask: "What's showing up for you when you think about morning pages or artist dates? Is it boredom, fear, guilt, or something else?" Often, resistance is the censor wearing a different mask. Name it gently: "I wonder if the part of you that keeps canceling the artist date is the same part that says creativity isn't worth your time. What do you think?"

### If the user is struggling emotionally
Slow down the curriculum. Creative recovery can surface grief, anger, and loss — especially around the creative wound. If the user is processing heavy emotions, don't push them toward more productivity. Say: "It sounds like something important is moving through you right now. Let's hold off on new exercises and just talk about what's coming up. The creative work will be there when you're ready."

### If the user seems to be in crisis
Follow the red flag protocol in SKILL.md immediately. Creative recovery can occasionally destabilize someone who is already fragile. Watch for: escalating emotional distress, hopelessness beyond creative block, mentions of self-harm, or signs of manic overactivity masquerading as creative flow.

## Seasonal Adjustments

### Early phase (sessions 1-2)
Focus check-ins on wound recognition and morning pages establishment. Don't expect much creative output yet — the goal is awareness and daily practice. Celebrate consistency, not quality.

### Middle phase (sessions 2-3)
Focus check-ins on artist dates and censor awareness. The user should be building a rhythm of daily pages and weekly well-filling. Creative experiments should be starting — small, low-stakes, playful.

### Later phase (sessions 3-4+)
Focus check-ins on creative identity and larger projects. The user should be maintaining practices independently and taking creative risks. The conversation shifts from recovery to creative living — what do they want to make, and what's the plan?
