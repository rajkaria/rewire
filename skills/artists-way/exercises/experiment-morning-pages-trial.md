# Exercise: Morning Pages Trial

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (Morning Pages and Showing Up). Use when the user understands the purpose of morning pages and is ready to commit to a 7-day trial of the practice.

## Setup
Agent says: "You've heard the case for morning pages. Now it's time to stop talking about it and do it. We're setting up a 7-day experiment. Not a lifelong commitment — just seven mornings. Your job is to show up with a pen and write three pages of whatever is in your head. My job is to check in with you about what happens. This isn't about the writing. It's about what shifts in you when you consistently drain the mental noise before your day begins."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Set up the physical infrastructure
Agent says: "Let's get practical. I need you to tell me: what notebook will you use? I don't care if it's a dollar composition book from the drugstore. In fact, the cheaper and less precious, the better — a beautiful journal creates performance pressure. What do you have or what can you get by tomorrow morning?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Good. And a pen — any pen you like writing with. Where will the notebook live overnight? I want it within arm's reach of where you sleep, so there's zero friction between waking up and writing. Bedside table, pillow, floor next to the bed — where?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 2: Set the time commitment
Agent says: "What time do you wake up on a typical morning? And what's the first thing you currently do — check your phone, make coffee, lie there for a while? I need to know because morning pages need to happen before all of that. They go between 'eyes open' and 'day begins.' When can you fit in 20 minutes of writing?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Here's the deal: for the next seven mornings, the notebook comes before the phone. Before email. Before news. Before social media. Your creative self gets you first, before the world's demands get you. Can you commit to that?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 3: Set expectations
Agent says: "I want to prepare you for what's going to happen. Day 1 and 2 will probably feel awkward. You'll write things like 'this is stupid' and 'I don't know what to write.' That's normal — that's the censor talking. Keep writing. Day 3 and 4, some interesting things might start bubbling up — half-formed ideas, emotions you didn't expect, old memories. Pay attention but don't force it. Day 5, 6, and 7, the practice starts to feel like clearing a path. The writing might still be messy, but you'll notice something has shifted in how you feel when you finish. What questions do you have before we start?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 4: Address resistance preemptively
Agent says: "Let me ask you directly: what's the voice in your head saying right now about this experiment? Is it saying you'll forget, you'll be too tired, you can't write three pages, you don't have time? Whatever it's saying — tell me. Let's get the resistance on the table now so it doesn't ambush you at 6 AM on Tuesday."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Good. Now here's how you handle that when it shows up at 6 AM: you notice it, you pick up the pen, and you write it down. 'I don't want to do this. I'm tired. This is pointless.' Congratulations — that's your first paragraph. The resistance is the raw material. Use it."

### Step 5: Define the tracking method
Agent says: "Last thing. I want you to track one simple thing each day: after you finish your three pages, rate how you feel on a 1-5 scale. 1 is 'that was miserable and pointless.' 5 is 'something shifted and I feel lighter.' Just a number, jotted at the bottom of the last page. We'll look at the pattern together after seven days."

{Wait for user response}

## Branching Responses

- If the user is enthusiastic and says "I love this, I'm going to do five pages!": "I appreciate the energy, but stick to three. The practice has a specific design: three pages is enough to drain the surface noise and reach something deeper, without turning it into a marathon that's hard to sustain. Enthusiasm that leads to burnout by day 3 is worse than steady commitment. Three pages. Every day. That's the experiment."

- If the user is skeptical and says "I don't see how writing nonsense helps anything": "You don't have to see it yet. That's why it's an experiment, not a belief system. Give it seven days and judge by results, not theory. Plenty of skeptics have been converted by day 5. And if after seven days you feel nothing different — we'll stop and try something else. Fair?"

- If the user says they missed a day during the trial: "That happens. Here's what matters: don't make up for it. Don't write six pages tomorrow. Just pick up the pen the next morning and start fresh. The practice is about consistency over time, not perfection. What happened that morning — did the resistance win, or did life genuinely intervene?"

- If the user says their pages are just complaints and grocery lists: "Perfect. That's exactly what's supposed to happen in the early days. The complaints and lists are the top layer of mental clutter. Writing them down gets them out of your head and onto the page, where they can stop circulating on repeat. Keep going — the deeper material lives underneath. You have to drain the swamp to find the spring."

- If the user reports something surprising or emotional emerging in their pages: "This is exactly what we're looking for. Morning pages bypass the censor, and when the censor isn't editing, real things come through — feelings you've been suppressing, ideas you've been dismissing, truths you've been avoiding. You don't have to do anything with what emerged. Just notice that it was there, waiting. The pages gave it a way out."

## Debrief Questions
1. "You've done seven days. What was the hardest day, and what made it hard?"
2. "Did anything surprise you in your pages — an emotion, an idea, a memory you didn't expect?"
3. "Look at your 1-5 ratings over the week. Is there a trend? Did the numbers change from day 1 to day 7?"
4. "How did the rest of your mornings feel after writing? Did anything shift in your mood, your focus, or your creative energy during the day?"
5. "The big question: do you want to keep going? Not because you should, but because something in the practice is working. What does your gut say?"

## Real-World Action
"If the experiment worked — if you noticed even a small shift — I want you to commit to three more weeks of morning pages. Not forever. Just 21 more days. By the end of a month, the practice will either be something you can't imagine giving up, or you'll know it's not for you. But seven days isn't enough data. Commit to the next 21 days. Same rules: three pages, longhand, before the world gets you. Will you do it?"

## Report Back
"Next time we talk, I'm going to ask you two things: first, how many of the seven days did you actually do? Honesty matters more than a perfect score. Second, tell me about one moment from the pages that stuck with you — something that came up in the writing that surprised you or felt important. That moment is the practice working."
