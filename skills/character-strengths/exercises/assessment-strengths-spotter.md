# Exercise: Strengths Spotter

## Type
assessment

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (Discovering Your Strengths). Use when the user has started observing energy and engagement patterns and is ready to systematically identify their signature strengths without taking the formal VIA survey. This exercise works through guided self-reflection and behavioral evidence rather than standardized questionnaire items.

## Setup
Agent says: "We are going to do a guided assessment to identify your signature strengths. This is not a test — there are no right or wrong answers. We are going to look at your life from several different angles, and each angle will give us clues about which strengths are most central to who you are. By the end, you will have a working list of your top 5 signature strengths. Ready?"

## The Exercise

### Step 1: The Energy Audit
Agent says: "I want you to think about the activities and situations that energize you most. Not just things you enjoy — things that leave you feeling more alive than when you started. These could be at work, at home, with friends, alone, anywhere. Give me 3-5 examples."

{Wait for user response. Write down each activity.}

Agent says: "Now, for each one, I want to name the character quality underneath. When you [activity], what strength is at work? Is it curiosity — the drive to explore and learn? Creativity — making something new? Kindness — connecting through generosity? Social intelligence — reading the room and navigating people? Let's go through each one."

{Walk through each activity. For each, name the likely strength and ask the user if it resonates. Build a tally of which strengths appear most frequently.}

### Step 2: The Drain Audit
Agent says: "Now the flip side. What situations drain you? Not hard situations — draining ones. The ones where even if you succeed, you feel depleted. Give me 2-3."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Draining situations usually require strengths that are not naturally yours. They tell us what you are NOT by contrast. If administrative detail drains you, that might mean prudence and self-regulation are lower-ranking for you. If networking drains you, social intelligence might not be signature. What strengths do these draining situations require that do not come naturally to you?"

{Help the user identify the strengths these situations require, and note that those are likely not signature strengths.}

### Step 3: The Reputation Check
Agent says: "Here is a different angle: what do people come to you for? Not your job title or official role — what do the people in your life actually rely on you to bring? When your friend calls you in a crisis, what are they seeking from you specifically? When a colleague asks for your input, what do they expect you to add? Give me 2-3 things people consistently come to you for."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "This is how your strengths look from the outside. The things people come to you for are usually your signature strengths in action — even if you do not recognize them as special. Let me reflect what I hear: when people come to you for [thing], that sounds like [strength]. Does that track?"

{Map each reputation item to a character strength.}

### Step 4: The Childhood Clue
Agent says: "One more angle — and this one often surprises people. Think about what you loved doing as a child, between ages 8 and 14, before the world started telling you what you should be. What activities absorbed you? What did you do when nobody was making you do anything?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Childhood interests are one of the purest windows into signature strengths, because they existed before socialization and obligation covered them up. When you were [childhood activity], what strength was already at work? Does that strength still show up in your life today?"

{Connect childhood activities to strengths. Note whether those strengths are currently active or dormant.}

### Step 5: The Envy Signal
Agent says: "Last angle. Think about someone you admire or envy — not their success, but their qualities. Who makes you think 'I wish I could be more like that'? What specifically do you admire about them?"

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Here is the insight: the qualities you admire in others are almost always qualities you already possess — you just may not have given yourself permission to fully own them. The reason you recognize that quality and are drawn to it is because it resonates with something inside you. Does [admired quality] show up in your own life, even in small ways?"

{Connect admired qualities to strengths the user may be underusing.}

### Step 6: Synthesize the Top 5
Agent says: "Let's pull all of this together. From the energy audit, the drain audit, the reputation check, the childhood clue, and the envy signal, here is what I see as your emerging signature strengths profile."

{List the strengths that appeared across multiple angles, ordered by how frequently and strongly they showed up.}

Agent says: "Here are the strengths that came up across multiple angles — these are your likely signature strengths:
1. [Strength] — showed up in [which angles]
2. [Strength] — showed up in [which angles]
3. [Strength] — showed up in [which angles]
4. [Strength] — showed up in [which angles]
5. [Strength] — showed up in [which angles]

How does this list feel? Does anything surprise you? Anything missing that you expected to see?"

{Discuss and adjust based on user response. The user has the final word on their own strengths.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user struggles with the energy audit: "Let me make it simpler. In the last month, what was one moment where you thought 'this is great, I want more of this'? Just one. Start there."
- If the user has too many strengths and cannot narrow down: "Having many strengths is great — now we need to find the signature ones. Here is the test: if you had to give up all but 5 of these qualities, which 5 would feel the most painful to lose? Those are signature."
- If the user resists the childhood question: "Some people find childhood hard to revisit, and that is OK. We can skip this one and still get a clear picture. We have plenty of data from the other angles."
- If the user says their top strengths feel boring or unremarkable: "That is actually the hallmark of a signature strength — it feels so normal to you that you do not see it as special. But I promise you: not everyone has [strength] at this level. What feels ordinary to you is extraordinary to someone who does not naturally possess it."
- If the user's results from different angles conflict: "Interesting — the energy audit points toward [A] but the reputation check points toward [B]. That sometimes means you have an underused strength. [A] might be who you are in private, while [B] is who the world asks you to be. Which one feels more essentially you?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "Looking at your top 5, which one feels the most 'you' — the one you would never want to give up?"
2. "Were there any surprises? A strength you did not expect, or one you expected that did not show up?"
3. "Is there a strength on this list that you have been underusing — one that is really you but has not had much room to show up lately?"
4. "How does it feel to name these out loud? Most people have never articulated their strengths this clearly."

## Real-World Action
"This week, take your top 5 list and test it against daily life. Each evening, spend 60 seconds reviewing your day and asking: which of my top 5 strengths showed up today? Which ones were missing? Keep a simple tally — even just checkmarks next to each strength on a piece of paper. After 7 days, you will have real behavioral data about which strengths are active and which are dormant. Bring that data to our next session."

## Report Back
"Next time, I will ask you: which strengths got the most checkmarks? Which got the fewest? And after a week of watching, would you change your top 5 at all — add anything, remove anything, reorder anything? Your strength profile should feel alive, not fixed."
