# Lesson 3: Strengths at Work

## Core Concept

Most people experience their work as a set of tasks, responsibilities, and roles that someone else defined. They adapt to what is expected rather than shaping work around who they are. Strengths-based work flips this: instead of asking "what does my job need me to be?", you ask "how can I bring who I already am into my job?" Research consistently shows that people who use their signature strengths at work are more engaged, more productive, and more satisfied — not because they have better jobs, but because they approach their existing jobs differently. This practice is sometimes called "job crafting" — the deliberate redesign of how you do your work to bring more of your strengths into play.

The agent's job is to help the user see their current work through a strengths lens and identify concrete changes — some small, some structural — that would let them deploy their signature strengths more often. This is not about finding a new job (though that may emerge). It is about redesigning the relationship with the work they already have.

## Key Takeaway

You do not need a different job to use your strengths at work. You need to see your current work differently — and make specific, deliberate changes to how you do it.

## Teaching Approach

Start by mapping the user's current work against their strengths profile. Where are strengths being used? Where are they starving?

### Opening Question
"Walk me through a typical workday or workweek. I do not need every detail — just the major chunks of time and energy. What does your time go to?"

### Follow-Up Questions
- After they describe their work: "Now, looking at that through the lens of your signature strengths — where in your day or week do your strengths actually get to show up? And where are they completely absent?"
- If they struggle to connect strengths to work: "Let me help. You told me [strength] is a signature strength. Where in your work do you get to use [specific strength-behavior]? And where do you wish you could but cannot?"
- If they say their work does not use any of their strengths: "That is important information — and it might explain some of the frustration or drain you feel. But before we jump to changing jobs, let's see if there are ways to bring your strengths into how you do the work, even if the work itself stays the same."
- If they love their work and see strengths everywhere: "That is great — and it explains why this work fits you. Let's look for the areas where there is still room to grow. Is there a strength that is getting no airtime at work? What would it look like to give it some?"

### Teaching the Three Crafting Moves

Say: "There are three ways to redesign your work to bring more strengths into play. You do not need permission for most of these — you can start this week."

**Move 1 — Task crafting:** "Change what you do. Volunteer for projects that use your strengths. Delegate or trade tasks that drain you. Reshape how you approach existing tasks to involve more of your strengths. If curiosity is your strength, turn a boring report into a mini-investigation. If creativity is your strength, redesign a process instead of just following it."

Ask: "Looking at your current task list, what is one task you could reshape to use more of [their strength]?"

**Move 2 — Relationship crafting:** "Change who you work with. Seek out collaborators whose strengths complement yours. Offer to help in areas where your strengths add value. If social intelligence is your strength, become the person who navigates team dynamics. If kindness is your strength, mentor someone new."

Ask: "Who do you work with regularly? Is there a relationship or collaboration where your strengths could add more value than they currently do?"

**Move 3 — Meaning crafting:** "Change how you see your work. Reframe your role in terms of the impact it has, not just the tasks it involves. A hospital cleaner who sees their work as preventing infections is using a completely different set of strengths than one who sees their work as mopping floors. How would you describe the purpose of your work — not the job title, but the impact?"

Ask: "If you described your work to someone in terms of your signature strengths rather than your job description, what would you say? Try it: 'I use my [strength] to [impact].'"

### Identifying Strength Gaps at Work

Say: "Now let's look at the other side. Where in your work are you consistently using qualities that are NOT your signature strengths? Those are the spots that drain you — not because the work is hard, but because it requires you to be someone you are not."

Ask: "What part of your work makes you feel like you are wearing someone else's clothes? What strength does that task require that is not naturally yours?"

Follow up with: "What options do you have? Can you delegate that task? Can you partner with someone whose strength it is? Can you restructure it to use more of your own strengths? Or is it simply something you need to manage around — spending minimal energy there so you have more for the areas that light you up?"

### The Strengths Boundary

Say: "One more thing. Sometimes knowing your strengths helps you set better boundaries at work. If you know that your strength is creativity, not administration, you can stop volunteering for the spreadsheet tasks that drain you. If you know your strength is honesty, not diplomacy, you can prepare more carefully for political meetings. Strengths are not just about doing more of what you love — they are about doing less of what depletes you."

### When Work Genuinely Does Not Fit

Say: "I want to be honest about something. Job crafting works for most situations — but not all. If you have done the analysis and your work fundamentally blocks your signature strengths with no room for crafting, that is important data. It does not mean you should quit tomorrow, but it means the misalignment is real and worth taking seriously."

Ask: "Based on what we have mapped together — is this a crafting problem or a fit problem? Is there room to bring your strengths in, or is the structure actively working against who you are?"

If it is a fit problem: "Then part of our work together might be clarifying what a better-fit role looks like — using your strengths profile as a compass. What kind of work would let you use [their top strengths] most of the time? We do not need to solve this today, but let's name it."

If it is a crafting problem: "Good — then we have room to work. And the crafting experiments we design this week will test exactly how much room there is."

### Strengths and Energy Management

Say: "There is a practical application here that most people miss. Your energy throughout the day is directly connected to which strengths are in play. Most people design their schedule around urgency — what is due, what is on fire. Strengths-based scheduling means also considering energy: when in the day do you do the work that uses your signature strengths, and when do you do the work that drains you?"

Ask: "If you could redesign your daily work schedule to front-load your strengths work and back-load the draining tasks — or at least alternate between them — what would that look like? What is one scheduling change you could make this week to put your strengths in a better time slot?"

{Wait for response}

Say: "Even a small shift — moving your creative work to the morning instead of the afternoon, for example — can change how an entire day feels. You are not doing different work. You are doing the same work in a different order, and that order determines whether your strengths get to fuel you or whether you are running on fumes by the time they get their turn."

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "I need to find a job that perfectly matches my strengths."
  **Reframe**: "The perfect strengths-job match is rare. Most people get much more mileage from crafting their current work than from searching for a mythical perfect role. What is the smallest change you could make this week that would bring more of your strengths into play?"

- **Misconception**: "My boss or company has to change for me to use my strengths."
  **Reframe**: "Some changes require permission, but many do not. How you approach a task, who you collaborate with, and how you frame your work to yourself — all of those are within your control right now. Start there."

- **Misconception**: "Using strengths at work means ignoring my weaknesses."
  **Reframe**: "It means managing your weaknesses so they do not derail you, while investing your best energy in your strengths. Think of it as an 80/20 rule: 80% of your development energy toward strengths, 20% toward making sure your weaknesses do not cause problems."

- **Misconception**: "This is just positive thinking applied to a bad job."
  **Reframe**: "It might be a bad job. If your work actively prevents you from using any of your signature strengths, that is worth taking seriously. But most jobs have more flexibility than people think. Have you actually tested the limits of what you can change — or are you assuming the answer is nothing?"

## Bridge to Practice

Help the user design one specific job-crafting experiment for the coming week. It should be small enough to try without permission, concrete enough to observe results, and connected to a specific signature strength.

Validate their plan: "Before you go, let me check — does this experiment feel like something you would actually do? Rate your confidence 1-10. If it is below a 7, let's make it smaller. The best experiment is the one you actually run, not the most ambitious one."

### Assignment
"Pick one of the three crafting moves — task, relationship, or meaning — and design a specific experiment for this week. What strength will you bring into play? Where and when? What will you do differently than you normally would? Write it down: 'This week, I will use my [strength] to [specific action] during [specific work context].' Also, try one scheduling change: move one strengths-aligned task to a time of day when your energy is highest. Next session, we will talk about what happened with both experiments."
