# Milestones

## Stage 1: Awareness
**Timeframe**: Sessions 1-2
**Marker**: User sees clearly where their time goes and recognizes the gap between how they live and how they want to live. They have begun to notice their anger patterns — the triggers, the flash, the automatic response — even if they cannot yet interrupt them.
**The user can**: Describe, with honest specificity, how they spend a typical day and identify which hours are chosen versus which are on autopilot. Name their top three anger triggers and describe the physiological sensation of the flash. Engage with philosophical questions about time and mortality without deflecting into abstraction.
**What to celebrate**: "You are seeing your life clearly now — how you actually spend your time, how your anger actually operates. Most people never get this far. They go through decades on autopilot without ever examining the pattern. You have examined it. That is the foundation everything else builds on."

## Stage 2: Practice
**Timeframe**: Sessions 2-3
**Marker**: User actively practices the anger delay technique and has begun reclaiming time from default-mode activities. They are engaging with the concepts of grief and impermanence without suppression or avoidance.
**The user can**: Successfully insert a delay between anger trigger and response at least some of the time. Make deliberate choices about how to spend at least one hour per day that was previously on autopilot. Discuss loss and impermanence honestly — neither suppressing the emotional weight nor being overwhelmed by it. Distinguish between mourning and being consumed by grief.
**What to celebrate**: "You have moved from seeing to doing. The anger delay worked — even once — and that means you know the gap exists and you can use it. You are making choices about your time that you were not making a month ago. That shift from autopilot to intention is the core of this entire practice."

## Stage 3: Integration
**Timeframe**: Sessions 3-4
**Marker**: User has a regular premeditatio malorum practice that generates genuine gratitude. Time awareness and anger regulation are becoming habitual rather than effortful. The user carries impermanence as a source of appreciation rather than anxiety.
**The user can**: Practice premeditatio malorum daily (or near-daily) and report that it creates appreciation rather than dread. Catch themselves wasting time and self-correct without coaching. Handle anger-triggering situations with a noticeable pause and a chosen response more often than not. Hold grief, gratitude, and the awareness of impermanence simultaneously — understanding that these are not contradictions but companions.
**What to celebrate**: "The practices are becoming part of you rather than something you do on top of your life. You are not just managing your time — you are choosing your life. You are not just suppressing anger — you are governing yourself. And you are holding the full weight of impermanence while living more fully, not less. That is Stoic practice working from the inside."

## Stage 4: Mastery / Graduation
**Marker**: User applies Stoic principles as a natural framework for daily decision-making and crisis response. The practices have become internalized — they no longer require coaching prompts or structured exercises.
**The user can**: Live deliberately as a default mode, not an occasional achievement — catching autopilot quickly and redirecting intentionally. Regulate emotional responses (especially anger) consistently, even in high-stakes situations. Use premeditatio malorum as a regular tool for gratitude and resilience without sliding into anxiety. Carry loss and grief with dignity — mourning honestly while continuing to engage fully with life. Articulate the core Stoic principles in their own words and apply them to novel situations. Coach themselves through setbacks using the framework independently.
**What to celebrate**: "I want to name something. The way you just handled that — choosing your response, seeing where your time was going, holding the difficulty without flinching — that is not something I taught you. That is something you built. The philosophy has become yours. You are not reciting Stoic principles. You are living them."
