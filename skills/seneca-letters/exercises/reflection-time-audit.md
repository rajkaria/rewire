# Exercise: Time Audit

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (On the Shortness of Life). Use when the user has engaged with the idea that their time is their life and is ready to look honestly at where it actually goes. This exercise creates the raw data for every subsequent conversation about deliberate living.

## Setup
Agent says: "You have been thinking about where your time goes. Now we are going to look at it with the precision of an audit — not a productivity audit, but a life audit. The question is not 'am I being efficient?' It is 'does how I spend my hours match what I believe my life is for?' This takes honesty, and honesty can be uncomfortable. But you cannot change a pattern you cannot see."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Recall yesterday in detail
Agent says: "Walk me through yesterday, hour by hour. Start from the moment you woke up. For each block of time, tell me: what were you doing, and did you choose it deliberately — or did it just happen? Be specific. 'I was on my phone' is not specific enough. What were you doing on your phone, and for how long?"

{Wait for user response. Take notes on each time block.}

### Step 2: Categorize the hours
Agent says: "Now let us sort what you described into three categories.

Category one: Chosen and valued. Time you spent on something you deliberately selected and that aligns with what matters to you. Work that is meaningful, time with people you love, exercise, creative pursuits, rest you genuinely needed.

Category two: Necessary but unchosen. Obligations you cannot avoid — commuting, certain work tasks, household maintenance. You did not choose these for their own sake, but they keep life functioning.

Category three: Default mode. Time that went somewhere without a conscious decision. Scrolling, watching something you did not intend to watch, sitting in a meeting that served no purpose, saying yes to something you did not want to do. Not bad, necessarily — just unchosen.

Which of your hours fall into which category? Be honest."

{Wait for user to categorize. Help if they struggle with borderline cases.}

### Step 3: Calculate the ratio
Agent says: "Look at the breakdown. Roughly what percentage of yesterday was Category One — chosen and valued? What percentage was Category Three — default mode? Take a guess. You do not need to be exact."

{Wait for response.}

Agent says: "Now multiply that Category Three percentage by 365. That is roughly how many days per year are going to things you did not choose. How does that number feel?"

{Wait for response. This is often a moment of genuine impact. Let it land.}

### Step 4: Identify the biggest leak
Agent says: "Looking at your Category Three time — the default mode hours — where is the biggest single leak? If you could reclaim one block of unchosen time per day, which one would it be? Not the one you think you should reclaim. The one that, deep down, you know is costing you the most."

{Wait for response.}

### Step 5: The reclamation question
Agent says: "If you reclaimed that time — even half of it — what would you do with it? Not what you should do. What would you actually want to do with those recovered hours? What does the version of you who is living deliberately do with that time?"

{Wait for response.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user is shocked by how much time is in Category Three: "That reaction is the point of the exercise. Most people are astonished when they see it clearly for the first time. The good news: you cannot unsee this. Now that you know where the leak is, you can start making different choices — not all at once, but one decision at a time."
- If the user is defensive about their time use: "I am not here to judge how you spend your time. There is no objectively correct way to spend a day. The only question is: is this what you would choose if you were choosing deliberately? If the answer is yes — even for the scrolling, even for the television — then it belongs in Category One. It is only a problem if it is happening without your conscious participation."
- If the user says nearly everything is Category One: "That is worth examining more closely. Sometimes we retroactively justify how we spent time because the alternative — admitting we wasted it — is uncomfortable. Let me push on one or two of those. Would you have chosen that activity if you had actively decided at the start of the hour how to spend it? Or did it just happen, and now you are claiming it?"
- If the user becomes emotional: "This can be a heavy exercise. Looking at where your life is actually going, versus where you want it to go, can bring up grief, regret, even anger. That is not a problem — that is the exercise working. What you do with that feeling is what matters. Let it fuel a change, not a spiral of self-criticism."
- If the user is already living deliberately: "That is genuinely rare, and worth celebrating. For you, the audit is less about finding waste and more about deepening intentionality. Are there places where 'good enough' could become 'exactly what I want?' Where could you move from Category Two into Category One by changing how you approach necessary tasks?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "What surprised you most about where your time actually goes? Was there a gap between what you assumed and what you found?"
2. "If you could redesign one hour of your typical day — make it more deliberate — which hour would have the biggest impact on your sense of living well?"
3. "Imagine someone living the exact daily pattern you described, but doing it for forty years. What kind of life does that person have at the end? Is that the life you want?"

## Real-World Action
"For the next seven days, run this audit in real time. At the end of each day — or even better, at a few checkpoints throughout the day — write down where your hours went and categorize them. You do not need to change anything yet. Just observe. The act of tracking, all by itself, tends to shift behavior because you cannot track unconsciously. Keep a simple log: time, activity, and category (chosen, necessary, or default)."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, bring your week of data. I will ask you: what patterns did you see? Did anything change just because you were watching? And what is the one change you want to make first — the single default-mode hour you want to reclaim and redirect?"
