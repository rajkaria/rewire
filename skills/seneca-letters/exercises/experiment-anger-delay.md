# Exercise: Anger Delay Experiment

## Type
experiment

## When to Use
After Lesson 2 (On Anger). Use when the user understands the three-stage model of anger (trigger, flash, response) and is ready to practice inserting a deliberate pause between the flash and their response in real-world situations.

## Setup
Agent says: "You understand the mechanics of anger: trigger, flash, response. You know that the gap between the flash and the response is where your freedom lives. Now we are going to design a real-world experiment. This week, you are going to practice the delay — inserting a pause between the moment anger flares and the moment you act. This is not about suppressing anger. It is about giving yourself enough time to choose your response instead of being chosen by the anger."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Map the trigger landscape
Agent says: "Let us start by naming your triggers. I want you to give me three situations that reliably make you angry. Not hypothetical situations — real, recurring ones. The things that happen in your actual life, probably this week, that are going to set you off. Be as specific as possible. Not 'when people are rude' — more like 'when my partner leaves dishes in the sink after I asked them not to' or 'when my colleague takes credit for my work in meetings.'"

{Wait for user to name three triggers. Push for specificity.}

### Step 2: Rate the intensity
Agent says: "For each trigger, rate the typical intensity of your anger response on a 1-10 scale. 1 is mild annoyance. 10 is white-hot, can barely control yourself. Where does each one land?"

{Wait for ratings.}

Agent says: "Good. For this experiment, we are going to start with the trigger you rated lowest — the one where the anger is real but manageable. That gives you the best chance of successfully inserting the delay. We will work up to the harder ones. Which one is your starting point?"

{Wait for user selection.}

### Step 3: Design the delay
Agent says: "When this trigger fires, the flash will hit your body before your mind catches up. Your job is to have a delay strategy ready before the moment arrives. Let us design one. There are several options:

The breath method: Take three slow breaths before you say or do anything. Count them. This gives you roughly fifteen seconds.

The exit method: Physically leave the space for sixty seconds. Say 'I need a moment' if you need to say anything. Walk to another room, to the bathroom, outside. Come back when you have chosen your response.

The count method: Count backward from ten. Slowly. Do not speak or act until you reach one.

The label method: Silently name what is happening in your body. 'My jaw is clenching. My chest is tight. My hands want to make fists.' The act of labeling shifts you from the emotional brain to the observing brain.

Which of these sounds most doable for your trigger? Or do you want to design a different one that fits your specific situation?"

{Wait for user to choose or design their strategy.}

### Step 4: Rehearse the moment
Agent says: "Let us walk through it before it happens. Close your eyes if that helps. Imagine the trigger occurring. Your partner leaves the dishes. Your colleague takes credit. Whatever it is — see it happening. Feel the flash starting. Your body tightens. The anger rises. Now — this is the moment — instead of responding automatically, you deploy your delay strategy. Walk me through it. What do you do? What do you say to yourself? What happens in the pause?"

{Wait for user to narrate their imagined delay.}

Agent says: "Good. Now here is the question: after the delay, after you have given yourself fifteen seconds or sixty seconds or whatever it is — what do you want to do? Not what does the anger want you to do. What do you, the person who has had time to think, want to do? That might still be expressing frustration. It might be having a conversation. It might be letting it go. But it will be your choice, not the anger's."

{Wait for response.}

### Step 5: Set the experiment parameters
Agent says: "Here is the experiment for this week. Every time your selected trigger fires — and we know it will, because it is a recurring pattern — deploy your delay strategy. After each incident, write a brief note:

1. What was the trigger?
2. Did you manage to delay? (Yes, partially, or no — all are useful data.)
3. If yes: what did you do after the delay? How was it different from your usual response?
4. If no: what happened? Where did the automatic response take over?
5. How did you feel ten minutes later?

We are not looking for perfection. We are looking for data. Even a failed delay teaches you something about the speed and force of your anger pattern."

## Branching Responses
- If the user says their anger is too fast to catch: "That is honest, and it is common at the beginning. The flash happens in milliseconds. But here is the thing: you do not need to catch it before it fires. You need to catch it before you finish acting on it. Even if the first angry word comes out, you can still pause before the second one. The delay does not need to happen before the anger. It needs to happen during it. Stopping mid-sentence is still a success."
- If the user says they do not get angry, only frustrated or annoyed: "Frustration and annoyance are anger's quieter siblings. They operate on the same mechanism: trigger, flash, response. The delay technique works the same way regardless of intensity. Start with frustration. The skill transfers upward to stronger emotions as you practice."
- If the user is worried about looking strange: "You might feel self-conscious taking a breath or leaving the room. That is normal. But consider what looks stranger: a person who pauses for fifteen seconds before responding, or a person who says something in anger that damages a relationship? The pause might feel awkward. The alternative is worse."
- If the user has a history of harmful anger: "I want to check in here. If your anger has caused physical harm, verbal abuse, or made others feel unsafe, this experiment is useful but it may not be sufficient on its own. An anger management therapist could work with you more intensively. Would you be open to exploring that alongside this practice?"
- If the user is enthusiastic: "Good. Channel that energy into the experiment, not just the concept. Understanding the delay intellectually is step one. Executing it when the trigger fires — when your body is flooded with adrenaline and your mind is screaming at you to react — that is the real practice. It will be harder than you expect. That is normal."

## Debrief Questions
1. "How many times did the trigger fire this week? Of those, how many times were you able to deploy the delay? Do not judge the number — just name it."
2. "When the delay worked — when you managed to pause — what did you notice in that gap? What thoughts or feelings were present between the flash and your chosen response?"
3. "Was there a moment where the delay changed the outcome — where what you did after the pause was meaningfully different from what you would have done on autopilot?"
4. "For the times the delay failed — what got in the way? Was the anger too fast? Too intense? Or did you forget in the moment?"
5. "How did the people around you respond when you paused instead of reacting? Did anyone notice?"

## Real-World Action
"Continue the anger delay experiment for the full week. If you master the delay on your starting trigger, move to the next one on your list — the one with higher intensity. The goal is not to never feel angry. The goal is to have one successful delay per day — one moment where you caught the anger, paused, and chose your response. That is a win."

## Report Back
"Next time we talk, bring your log. I want to see the raw data: how many triggers, how many delays, what happened when it worked, and what happened when it did not. The failures are as valuable as the successes — they tell us where the practice needs refinement. And I especially want to hear about the moment where the delay made the biggest difference — the one where your life went differently because you paused."
