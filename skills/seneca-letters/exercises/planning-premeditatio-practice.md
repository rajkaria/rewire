# Exercise: Premeditatio Practice Design

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (Premeditatio Malorum). Use when the user understands the distinction between deliberate negative visualization and anxious rumination, and is ready to design a sustainable daily practice.

## Setup
Agent says: "You understand the principle of premeditatio malorum — imagining adversity to build gratitude and resilience. Now we are going to design a practice that fits into your real life. Not a theoretical practice. A specific, daily ritual with a time, a place, a structure, and a clear boundary. The Stoics did this the way a musician practices scales: regularly, deliberately, and with the understanding that the daily practice builds the capacity you need when the real performance comes."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Choose the time and place
Agent says: "First, let us anchor this practice in your actual schedule. When and where will you do it? The best time is one where you are awake and clear-headed but not yet swept into the day's momentum. Many people find that early morning works — before the phone, before the email, before the obligations begin. But it could also be during a commute, on a walk, or in a quiet moment before bed. The key is consistency: same time, same place, every day. When would work for you?"

{Wait for response. Push for specificity — not "in the morning" but "at 6:45 AM, sitting in my kitchen before anyone else is awake."}

### Step 2: Choose the first week's subjects
Agent says: "Next, what will you visualize? For the first week, I want you to rotate through seven subjects — one per day. We will choose them together. Pick things that range from the everyday to the profound:

Start with three things you take for granted in daily life — conveniences, routines, small pleasures that feel permanent. Your morning coffee. Your ability to walk. Hot water. Having a job.

Then add two people in your life you tend to assume will always be there. A partner, a parent, a friend, a sibling.

Then add one aspect of your health or physical capacity that you rely on without thinking about it.

And one larger condition of your life — your home, your safety, your country's stability, your freedom.

What are your seven?"

{Wait for user to name their seven subjects. Help them if they struggle — the point is a range from mundane to existential.}

### Step 3: Design the ritual structure
Agent says: "Now the structure. Each session should have three phases, and the whole thing should take five to eight minutes. Let us build it:

Phase one — Arrival (thirty seconds). Close your eyes or soften your gaze. Take three slow breaths. Arrive in the present moment. Set the intention: you are not worrying. You are practicing.

Phase two — Visualization (three to five minutes). Bring today's subject to mind. Imagine, with quiet specificity, that it is gone. Not dramatically, not as a horror movie. With the calm, honest detail of someone looking at life clearly. What would the first morning look like without this thing? What would you miss most? How would your daily rhythm change? What would you give to have it back? Sit with the absence. Let yourself feel the weight of the loss.

Phase three — Return (one to two minutes). Open your eyes. Take three breaths. Notice that the thing you imagined losing is still here. Look at it — really look at it — as if you are seeing it for the first time after it was returned to you. Let the gratitude arise naturally. Do not force it. The contrast between the imagined absence and the present reality does the work.

Does this structure feel workable for you? Do you want to modify anything?"

{Wait for response. Adjust based on their feedback.}

### Step 4: Design the boundary
Agent says: "This is critical. Premeditatio malorum must have a clear ending. When the practice is done, it is done. You do not carry the visualization into the rest of your day. Think of it like physical training: you go to the gym, you train intensely, and then you leave. You do not keep doing bicep curls at your desk.

Design an ending ritual. This is a physical action that signals to your mind: the practice is complete. It could be:

- Three claps or taps on your knees
- Standing up and stretching
- Saying a specific phrase to yourself — 'I return to what is here'
- Pouring your morning coffee (making the ritual transition into a daily pleasure)

What will your ending ritual be?"

{Wait for response.}

### Step 5: Set the safety valve
Agent says: "One more thing. If at any point during the practice you notice the visualization is not building appreciation but feeding anxiety — if it is looping, escalating, or leaving you more frightened than grateful — stop immediately. Use your ending ritual. And write down what happened so we can discuss it. This practice should leave you feeling more alive, not more afraid. If it is doing the opposite, something needs adjustment. Agreed?"

{Wait for confirmation.}

### Step 6: Summarize the plan
Agent says: "Let me repeat your plan back to you:

Time: [their chosen time]
Place: [their chosen place]
Duration: Five to eight minutes
Structure: Arrival (thirty seconds), Visualization (three to five minutes), Return (one to two minutes)
This week's subjects: [list their seven subjects, one per day]
Ending ritual: [their chosen ending ritual]
Safety valve: If it feeds anxiety instead of gratitude, stop and note what happened.

Does that feel right? On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you will do this every day this week?"

{If confidence is below 8, simplify. Reduce to every other day, shorten the duration, or start with only the most comfortable subjects.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user says they cannot sit still for five minutes: "You do not need to sit still. This can be done walking, standing, or lying down. Some people do it on their commute with headphones in and eyes open. The stillness is optional. The deliberateness is not. What environment would make it easiest for you?"
- If the user is afraid to visualize losing a loved one: "That is completely natural. Start with the less emotionally charged subjects — the hot water, the morning routine, the small conveniences. Build the muscle with lighter weights before you move to the heavier ones. When you feel ready for the more personal subjects, you will know. There is no rush."
- If the user says they already do something similar (gratitude journal, meditation): "Good. This is not a replacement — it is a complement. Gratitude journaling starts from what you have. Premeditatio starts from what you could lose. They work different muscles but build the same capacity. You might find they enhance each other."
- If the user is anxious and uncertain: "We go slowly. For the first three days, only visualize the everyday subjects — the coffee, the hot water, the small conveniences. See how it lands. If it builds appreciation, we continue. If it feeds worry, we adjust. You are in control of this practice at all times."
- If the user is deeply engaged and wants to go further: "Start with this structure for one week before you add anything. Once the daily practice is established, we can explore deeper subjects — your own mortality, the impermanence of your career, the eventual end of relationships. But the foundation comes first. Daily consistency beats occasional intensity."

## Debrief Questions
1. "How many of the seven days did you practice? What made the difference on the days you did versus the days you did not?"
2. "Which subject produced the strongest sense of gratitude on the return? What did that feel like — was it intellectual or did you feel it in your body?"
3. "Which subject was the hardest to visualize? Was there one that you resisted or that felt emotionally heavy?"
4. "Did the practice affect how you experienced the rest of your day? Did anything feel different — more vivid, more precious, more present?"
5. "Was there any point where the visualization started to feel anxious rather than contemplative? What happened, and what did you do?"

## Real-World Action
"Practice your premeditatio ritual every day for seven days using the structure we designed. Rotate through your seven subjects, one per day. After each session, write one sentence about what you noticed — not a journal entry, just a single honest observation. At the end of the week, read all seven sentences together and see what pattern emerges."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I want to hear three things: which session was the most powerful, which was the most difficult, and whether the practice changed anything about how you experience your ordinary daily life. The purpose is not to become a perfect practitioner. It is to build a relationship with impermanence that makes you more grateful, more present, and more prepared for whatever comes."
