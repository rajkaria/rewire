# Epictetus Manual Coaching

> What is up to you is your own mind. Everything else is not your affair.

## What This Skill Does

Coaches users through the core teachings of Epictetus — the dichotomy of control, the power of judgment, attention to impressions, and building inner freedom through disciplined daily practice. This is not a summary of Stoic philosophy. It is a structured coaching protocol that guides an AI agent to walk someone through applying these principles to their actual life, with real-world exercises and weekly accountability.

## Source

The Enchiridion (Handbook) and Discourses of Epictetus, as recorded by his student Arrian. Epictetus (c. 50-135 CE) was born into slavery, studied under the Stoic teacher Musonius Rufus, gained his freedom, and established a school in Nicopolis that attracted students from across the Roman world. His teachings represent Stoicism at its most practical and psychologically penetrating.

## What You'll Work On

- Distinguishing what is genuinely within your control (prohairesis) from what is not — and redirecting your energy accordingly
- Examining the automatic judgments that create unnecessary suffering
- Developing prosoche (attention) — the ability to catch your impressions before they run your behavior
- Building daily practices (askesis) that make clear thinking and purposeful action your default

## Structure

- **4 lessons** covering the dichotomy of control, the power of judgment, practicing with impressions, and freedom through discipline
- **3 exercises** including a control audit, impression journal, and Stoic morning routine design
- **Weekly check-ins** with progress tracking
- **4 milestones** from awareness to embodied practice

## A Note on Approach

This skill presents Stoic philosophy as a living tradition of practical wisdom, not as a set of productivity hacks or emotional suppression techniques. The Greek terminology is used alongside English to honor the tradition and build genuine philosophical literacy. The coaching tone mirrors Epictetus's own style: direct, challenging, rooted in care for the student's growth.
