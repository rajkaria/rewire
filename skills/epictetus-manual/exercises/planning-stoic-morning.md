# Exercise: Stoic Morning

## Type
planning

## When to Use
After Lesson 4 (Freedom Through Discipline). Use when the user is ready to build a sustainable daily askesis (training practice) and wants a structured morning preparation routine grounded in Stoic principles.

## Setup
Agent says: "The Stoics practiced what they called premeditatio — anticipatory reflection. Before the day begins, before events start pulling at you, you take a few minutes to prepare your prohairesis for what lies ahead. This is not visualization or affirmation. It is honest, clear-eyed preparation for the reality that some things today will challenge you, some things will not go as planned, and some things will be outside your control. You are going to design a morning practice that is genuinely yours — not a ritual you copied from a book but something that fits your life, your schedule, and the specific challenges you face."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Find the window
Agent says: "First, let us be realistic about time. This practice needs to be short enough that you will actually do it every day, even on bad days. How much time can you genuinely commit to every morning? Not on your best day — on your worst. Two minutes? Five? Ten? Give me the honest number."

{Wait for user response. If they say more than ten minutes, check: "Can you sustain that every day for a month? If not, start shorter. You can always expand. A practice you do daily for three minutes is worth more than one you do occasionally for twenty."}

### Step 2: Review the day ahead
Agent says: "The first element of your morning practice is a brief review of what lies ahead. Not your to-do list — your human landscape. What situations today might test you? A difficult conversation, a meeting where you want approval, a task with an uncertain outcome, time with someone who frustrates you? Name two or three things on your horizon today that might trigger reactive impressions."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Now, for each one, apply the dichotomy of control. What about this situation is up to you? What is not? Get clear before the moment arrives. If the meeting goes badly — that is not up to you. How you prepare, how you show up, whether you speak honestly — that is up to you. What would it look like to enter each of these situations focused only on the part that is yours?"

{Walk through each situation briefly}

### Step 3: Anticipate the impressions
Agent says: "The Stoics did not just prepare for events — they prepared for their own reactions to events. For each situation you named, what impression is your mind most likely to produce? 'They do not value my contribution.' 'This will not go well.' 'I cannot handle this.' Name the specific impressions that tend to ambush you."

{Wait for user response}

Agent says: "Good. Now you are forewarned. When that impression arrives today, it will not catch you by surprise. You have already seen it coming. You can greet it: 'Ah, there you are. I was expecting you.' That is premeditatio at work."

### Step 4: Set the prohairesis intention
Agent says: "Last element. In one sentence, state how you intend to exercise your prohairesis today. Not what you will accomplish — that depends on factors outside your control. How do you want to show up? What quality of character do you want to bring? For example: 'Today I will respond to difficulty with patience rather than reactivity.' Or: 'Today I will focus my energy on my effort rather than on outcomes I cannot control.' What is yours?"

{Wait for user response}

### Step 5: Write the routine
Agent says: "Now let us put this together into your actual morning routine. Given the time you committed, here is the structure. Tell me if this feels right, and we will adjust:

First, one minute: review the day ahead. What situations will test you? What is up to you in each one?

Second, one minute: name the impressions you expect. Identify which automatic reactions are likely to arise.

Third, thirty seconds: set your intention. One sentence about how you will exercise your prohairesis today.

Does this structure work for you? What would you change to make it something you will actually do?"

{Wait for user response. Adjust as needed.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user wants to add meditation or journaling: "Those are excellent complements. But I would suggest keeping the core Stoic premeditatio simple and separate at first. Once the three-minute practice is a solid habit — something you do without thinking about whether to do it — then you can layer on additional elements. Build the foundation first."
- If the user says they are not a morning person: "The practice does not have to happen at dawn. It needs to happen before your day engages you. If you roll out of bed and immediately check your phone, the day is already running you. Can you create a three-minute gap between waking and engaging? Even sitting on the edge of the bed before you pick up your phone? That gap is where the practice lives."
- If the user is skeptical that a few minutes can make a difference: "Test it. That is the Stoic way — do not take my word for it, run the experiment. Try this for one week. At the end of the week, compare: on the days you did the morning practice, were you even slightly more intentional in your responses? Even one moment of greater clarity is evidence that the practice has value. If after a week you see no difference, we will adjust."
- If the user already has a morning routine and does not want to change it: "We do not need to replace anything. We need to insert this. Where in your existing routine is there a natural transition — a moment between finishing one thing and starting another? That seam is where we place the practice. It might be while the coffee brews, between exercise and showering, or during the first minute of your commute. Where does it fit?"

## Debrief Questions
1. "How many mornings this week did you actually do the practice? What got in the way on the days you missed?"
2. "On the days you did it — did you notice any difference in how you moved through the day? Even a subtle one?"
3. "Were the impressions you anticipated in the morning the ones that actually showed up during the day? Or were you surprised by different ones?"
4. "What part of the routine felt most useful — the review, the impression preparation, or the intention? What part felt least useful?"

## Real-World Action
"Practice your Stoic morning every day this week. On the days you do it, notice the day. On the days you miss it, notice that day too. At the end of the week, you will have your own evidence about whether this practice makes a difference. Also: add a brief evening counterpart — just two minutes before sleep. Ask yourself three questions: Where did I respond from prohairesis today? Where did I react from an unexamined impression? What will I practice tomorrow? Morning preparation and evening review create a feedback loop that accelerates the whole process."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I want to hear: what did you learn from a week of morning premeditatio? Was there a moment during the week where you walked into a situation prepared — where you recognized an impression you had anticipated that morning and responded differently because of it? That is the goal. Not perfection. Preparation that shows up when it matters."
