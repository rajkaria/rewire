# Exercise: Control Audit

## Type
reflection

## When to Use
After Lesson 1 (The Dichotomy of Control). Use when the user has grasped the basic distinction between what is up to them and what is not, and needs to apply it systematically to their current life.

## Setup
Agent says: "You understand the dichotomy of control as a principle. Now let us put it to work on your actual life. I am going to walk you through an audit of the things currently taking up space in your mind. We will sort each one honestly. This is not about what you wish were in your control — it is about what actually is."

## The Exercise

### Step 1: Surface the worries
Agent says: "I want you to name everything that is currently weighing on you. Do not filter, do not rank. Big and small, rational and irrational. What are the things circling in your mind — the situations, concerns, fears, frustrations that keep pulling at your attention? Give me at least five."

{Wait for user response. Let them list everything without interrupting.}

### Step 2: Sort each item
Agent says: "Good. Now we are going to take each one and place it in one of three categories. First: 'Fully up to me' — meaning you can determine the outcome through your own choices alone. Second: 'Partly up to me' — meaning your effort matters but the outcome depends on factors beyond your control. Third: 'Not up to me at all' — meaning no amount of effort on your part will change it. Let us start with the first item on your list."

{Walk through each item one at a time. For each one, challenge the user's initial classification if needed.}

For items the user puts in "fully up to me," ask: "Can you guarantee this outcome? If you do everything perfectly, is the result certain? Or are you describing your effort — which is up to you — rather than the outcome — which may not be?"

For items in "partly up to me," ask: "What specifically is the part that is up to you? Name it precisely. And what is the part that is not? Be honest about where the boundary falls."

For items in "not up to me at all," ask: "Is there really nothing here that is up to you? Even your response to this situation? Your attitude toward it? How you prepare for it?"

### Step 3: Identify the energy leak
Agent says: "Now look at your sorted list. Where have you been spending most of your mental energy this week — on the items that are up to you, or on the ones that are not? Most people find that the majority of their worry goes toward what they cannot control. That is not a personal failing — it is the default setting of the untrained mind. But it is also where your suffering lives."

{Wait for user response}

### Step 4: Redirect
Agent says: "For each item in the 'not up to me' and 'partly up to me' categories, I want you to answer one question: What is the part of this that I can actually do something about? Not the outcome — the effort, the preparation, the response. Let us find where your prohairesis actually has purchase."

{Walk through each item. Help them find the actionable kernel inside each worry.}

## Branching Responses
- If the user resists categorizing something as "not up to me": "I understand the reluctance. Letting go of the illusion of control is uncomfortable. But consider: the energy you spend trying to control this — has it worked? Has it changed the outcome? Or has it mainly produced anxiety? What if you redirected that same energy toward what you can actually determine?"
- If the user puts everything in "not up to me" and feels helpless: "It might seem that way when you look at outcomes. But let us look again at what is always up to you: your effort, your preparation, your character, your response. You cannot control whether the job offer comes. You can control how well you prepare for the interview, how you carry yourself, and how you respond regardless of the answer. Those are not small things."
- If the user has a breakthrough moment of release: "That feeling — that lightness when you stop carrying what was never yours to carry — that is what Epictetus was pointing at. It is not resignation. It is precision. You just got clearer about where your energy belongs."
- If the user is emotional about recognizing how much energy they have wasted: "That recognition is not something to be ashamed of. Everyone does this. Every human being spends enormous energy trying to control what they cannot. You are not behind — you are awake to it. That is the beginning."

## Debrief Questions
1. "What surprised you about the sort? Was there anything you assumed was in your control that turned out not to be?"
2. "Looking at the 'not up to me' items — how much of your recent stress has come from those specifically?"
3. "Is there one item that you are ready to genuinely release — to stop spending energy trying to control? What would that free up for you?"

## Real-World Action
"Pick the item from the 'not up to me' column that has been consuming the most energy. This week, every time your mind goes to it, practice the redirect: 'This is not up to me. What is up to me right now?' Do not force yourself to stop thinking about it — just redirect your attention to what you can actually act on. Keep a count of how many times you catch yourself and redirect."

## Report Back
"When we talk next, I will ask: how many times did you catch the redirect this week? Was there a moment where the shift from 'not up to me' to 'what is up to me' actually changed what you did? That moment — that is the practice working."
